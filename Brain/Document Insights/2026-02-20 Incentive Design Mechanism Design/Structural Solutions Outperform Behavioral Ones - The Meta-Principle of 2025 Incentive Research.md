## The Meta-Principle

Across every domain examined in 2024-2025 incentive design research, **structural incentive design outperforms attempts to modify individual psychology**:

| Domain | Behavioral Approach | Structural Approach | Evidence |
|--------|---------------------|---------------------|----------|
| AI Alignment | RLHF (train behavior toward preferences) | Constitutional AI (encode rules structurally) | RLHF produces sycophancy; CAI more robust |
| Behavioral Nudging | Nudge effects on individual choices | Default settings, physical environment design | Nudge effects d=0.004 after bias correction |
| Organizational Motivation | Pay-for-performance targeting psychology | Work design (autonomy, mastery, challenge) | Work design explains more variance than pay design |
| Climate Policy | Carbon pricing targeting individual behavior | Fund structure targeting system-level incentives | Fund structure determines free-riding more than fund size |
| Gamification | Points/badges targeting extrinsic motivation | Narrative/challenge targeting intrinsic engagement | Extrinsic elements underperform structural/intrinsic ones |

## Why This Pattern Emerges

Three mechanisms explain why structural solutions consistently outperform behavioral ones:

**1. Behavioral interventions face Goodhart's Law**
Any attempt to modify behavior through targeted incentives will eventually be gamed - the gap between proxy metric and true goal is exploitable. Structural solutions close this gap by encoding the constraint directly.

**2. Behavioral interventions face context moderation**
Nudge effects collapse after publication bias correction (d=0.004). Performance pay reverses direction across cultures. Gamification effect sizes are almost entirely heterogeneous (I²=88.92%). Psychology varies by individual, culture, and context in ways that make universal behavioral interventions unreliable.

**3. Structural solutions are self-enforcing**
A structural change (default option, physical environment, algorithmic constraint) doesn't require ongoing compliance. A behavioral change requires ongoing motivation. This makes structural changes more durable, especially over time horizons where motivation naturally decays.
