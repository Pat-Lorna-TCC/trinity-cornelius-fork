# Context Moderation Dominates Main Effects in Incentive Research

**Source**: Multiple 2024-2025 meta-analyses (Hu et al. 2025, Kurnaz 2025, LSE/STICERD multi-country study, Kitayama & Rossmaier 2025)
**Document Type**: Research Synthesis across meta-analyses
**Extracted By**: AI (document-insight-extractor agent)
**Extraction Date**: 2026-02-20
**Session**: 2026-02-20 Incentive Design Mechanism Design

---

## Core Finding

The most consistent finding across 2024-2025 incentive research meta-analyses is that **effect sizes are driven by contextual moderators, not main effects**. Universal prescriptions in incentive design are increasingly untenable:

| Intervention | Published Effect | Corrected/Contextualized | What Controls Most Variance |
|-------------|-----------------|--------------------------|------------------------------|
| Nudges | d = 0.45 (PNAS 2022) | d = 0.004 (Hu et al. 2025) | Publication bias, context |
| Gamification | g = 0.654 average | I² = 88.92% | Context, design type, duration |
| Performance pay | Positive in West | Stronger positive in collectivist countries | Culture (individualism-collectivism) |
| Incentive programs | +30% at 6 months | +44% at 12+ months | Time horizon |

---

## Evidence

**Nudges**: 13 meta-analyses, 1,638 studies, ~30 million participants. Effect collapses from d=0.27 to d=0.004 after publication bias correction (Hu et al., Journal of Behavioral Decision Making, December 2025).

**Gamification**: I² = 88.92% means nearly all variance in effect sizes is explained by contextual factors, not the gamification intervention itself (Kurnaz, Psychology in the Schools, 2025).

**Performance pay**: Same packages, same jobs - effects moderated by cultural individualism-collectivism dimension. Counter-intuitively, stronger effects in collectivist cultures for employees experiencing input-output inequality (LSE/STICERD).

**Cultural neuroscience**: Cultural context shapes neural reward architecture itself (Kitayama & Rossmaier, 2025) - not just behavioral response, but neurological response.

---

## The Practical Implication

This finding fundamentally challenges the incentive design consulting industry, which packages interventions as universally applicable "best practices." The research says:

1. Test in your specific context before deploying
2. Preregister studies to avoid publication bias inflating your estimates
3. Treat prior research effect sizes as upper bounds, not expected values
4. Culture, individual differences, and temporal dynamics each explain more variance than the intervention itself
