# Present Bias is Partly Cognitive Load - Not Purely Time Preference

**Source**: Enke, Graeber & Oprea, "Complexity and Hyperbolic Discounting" - HBS Working Paper 24-048, 2024
**Document Type**: Working Paper (Harvard Business School)
**Extracted By**: AI (document-insight-extractor agent)
**Extraction Date**: 2026-02-20
**Session**: 2026-02-20 Incentive Design Mechanism Design

---

## Core Framework

Apparent present bias may stem primarily from **cognitive complexity**, not genuine time preference. When people appear to discount the future irrationally, they may simply be making cognitive approximation errors because precisely valuing future payoffs is hard work.

**The subadditivity finding**: People are approximately 10 percentage points less patient when a composite interval is broken up into two sub-intervals (vs. presented as one). This can't be explained by time preference alone - time didn't change, only the cognitive representation did.

**The mechanism**: Precisely valuing "$80 in two years" is cognitively non-trivial. People approximate heuristically, and that approximation systematically undervalues the future - creating the appearance of discounting even when genuine time preference is absent.

## Why This Distinction Matters

If present bias is partly cognitive load:
- **Simplifying delay representations** (not just making rewards immediate) should reduce present bias
- Organizational systems that make future consequences vivid and calculable should improve long-term decision quality
- Present bias may be more malleable than previously thought - not a fixed preference but a variable function of cognitive effort required

**Design implication**: To overcome temporal discounting, designers should:
1. Make immediate costs of bad decisions salient
2. Provide immediate micro-rewards for long-term behaviors
3. **Simplify delay calculations to reduce cognitive load** (the new insight)

This adds a third lever that pure time-preference models don't suggest.

## Supporting Context

Villmoare et al. (PLoS ONE, November 2024) provides the evolutionary frame: temporal discounting as evolved fitness-maximizing strategy under environmental uncertainty. Present bias is rational in environments where future payoffs were genuinely uncertain. This means some present bias is adaptive, not cognitive error - the two frameworks complement rather than contradict each other.
