# D1 vs D2 Dopamine Receptors - Overturning the Dopamine Equals Reward Simplification

**Source**: "Pharmacological Modulation of Dopamine Receptors Reveals Distinct Brain-Wide Networks Associated with Learning and Motivation in Nonhuman Primates" - Journal of Neuroscience, February 2025
**Document Type**: Research Paper (peer-reviewed, non-human primate study)

## Core Finding

Dopamine does not operate as a single "reward signal." D1 and D2 receptor pathways mediate fundamentally different aspects of learning and motivation - and they have opposite effects:

- **D1 receptor antagonism**: **Slowed** probabilistic learning, diminished corticocortical AND frontostriatal connectivity
- **D2 receptor antagonism**: **Improved** learning, broadly enhanced cortical connectivity

This directly challenges the simplified "dopamine = reward" model that underlies most popular neuroscience communication and some incentive design theory.

---

## Evidence

**Study Design**: Pharmacological modulation in non-human primates (directly manipulate specific receptor pathways - higher causal validity than human correlation studies)
**Method**: Systemic antagonism of D1 vs D2 receptors with behavioral and neuroimaging outcome measurement
**Key Results**:
- D1 antagonism: slowed learning + diminished brain connectivity
- D2 antagonism: improved learning + enhanced cortical connectivity
- The pathways operate on different circuit architectures (corticocortical vs. frontostriatal)
**Citation**: Journal of Neuroscience, February 2025, Vol. 45(6): e1301242024. https://www.jneurosci.org/content/45/6/e1301242024

**Supporting finding** (bioRxiv 2024): Intrinsic and extrinsic rewards implicate different striatal regions:
- Intrinsic rewards: **dorsal striatum** - sustained engagement, long-term learning, mastery
- Extrinsic rewards: **ventral striatum/nucleus accumbens** - acute spikes, contingent behavior

---

## Implications for Incentive Design

The intrinsic/extrinsic motivation distinction is not merely psychological - it is neurologically grounded in distinct anatomical circuits. Extrinsic rewards co-opt ventral striatum dopamine circuits, which can suppress the dorsal striatal circuits supporting intrinsic engagement (the mechanistic basis of the overjustification effect).

For incentive designers: leading with extrinsic monetary rewards doesn't just risk "crowding out" intrinsic motivation psychologically - it activates the wrong neural circuit, potentially suppressing the circuit that builds sustainable long-term engagement.
