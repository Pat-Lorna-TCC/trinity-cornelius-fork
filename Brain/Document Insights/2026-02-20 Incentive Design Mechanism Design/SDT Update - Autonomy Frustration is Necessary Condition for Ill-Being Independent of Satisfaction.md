## Core Finding

Self-Determination Theory receives a critical empirical update: **you cannot infer need frustration from the absence of need satisfaction.** They are theoretically and empirically distinct variables. This matters enormously for incentive design:

**Key Results**:
- Need support dimensions (autonomy, competence, relatedness): r = 0.64-0.68 with positive outcomes
- Need thwarting correlations: r = 0.56-0.66 with negative outcomes
- **Necessary Condition Analysis**: Both autonomy satisfaction AND lack of autonomy frustration are necessary for well-being - not just sufficient

**The critical asymmetry**: Removing frustration matters as much as adding satisfaction. A workplace could theoretically maximize autonomy opportunities but still produce misery if other aspects actively thwart autonomy.

## Evidence

**Meta-Analysis**: Howard, Slemp & Wang (2024) - comprehensive SDT need support meta-analysis
**Special Issue Finding (2025)**: Necessary Condition Analysis across multiple samples establishes that autonomy frustration is a necessary (not just contributing) condition for negative outcomes
**Competence finding**: Competence frustration consistently predicts negative outcomes (most robust of the three needs)
**Relatedness finding**: Relatedness did NOT consistently emerge as necessary for positive outcomes - more context-dependent than autonomy and competence
**Citation**: selfdeterminationtheory.org, February 2024; Motivation and Emotion Special Issue, 2025. https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11031-025-10160-y

## The "Need Crafting" Extension (2025)

New concept from Olafsen et al. (Applied Psychology, 2025): **Need Crafting** - employees can proactively shape their environment to meet their own SDT needs.

This extends job crafting theory to basic psychological needs. Cognitive and behavioral forms of need crafting can proactively foster well-being. The organizational implication: rather than top-down incentive design alone, organizations can scaffold employees' ability to craft their own need satisfaction - more scalable and self-sustaining.

**Citation**: Olafsen et al. (2025). "Crafting for autonomy, competence, and relatedness." Applied Psychology. https://iaap-journals.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/apps.12570

## Why This Reframes Incentive Design

The classical incentive design question is: "How do we satisfy autonomy, competence, and relatedness?"

The updated question must also ask: "How do we ensure we are not actively frustrating these needs?"

A high-salary job with excellent perks but micromanagement may satisfy relatedness and provide surface autonomy - but if managerial style actively frustrates autonomy on a daily basis, no amount of satisfaction elsewhere compensates.
