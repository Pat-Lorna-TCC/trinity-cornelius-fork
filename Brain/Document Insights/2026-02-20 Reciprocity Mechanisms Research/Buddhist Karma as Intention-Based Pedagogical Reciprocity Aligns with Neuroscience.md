## Core Framework

Buddhist karma represents a sophisticated **intention-based, pedagogical (non-punitive) reciprocity model** that aligns with modern neuroscience findings on reward prediction error and social learning. This framework transcends transactional reciprocity by incorporating ethical dimensions where compassionate actions generate positive outcomes through learning mechanisms, not cosmic punishment.

## Key Distinctions

### 1. Intention-Based vs. Action-Based

**Buddhist karma**: Moral quality dictated by **underlying intention**, not just action
- Each action (physical, verbal, mental) has consequences affecting future experiences
- Intention creates the karmic "seed" that ripens into outcomes

**Neuroscience parallel**:
- VTA dopamine neurons encode **social prediction error** based on expected vs. actual outcomes
- Expectations (intentions) drive prediction error learning
- Same computational principle: outcome depends on intention/expectation

### 2. Pedagogical vs. Punitive

**Buddhist view**: Karma is **not punitive but pedagogical**
- Errors are lessons, not punishments
- Each action plants a seed whose ripening becomes basis for further growth
- Learning-focused rather than punishment-focused

**Neuroscience parallel**:
- Prediction error signals (dopamine) drive **learning**, not punishment
- Negative outcomes update predictions for future behavior
- Reinforcement learning is inherently pedagogical

### 3. Non-Transactional Compassion

**Buddhist framework**:
- Compassionate and mindful actions generate positive karmic outcomes
- Transcends tit-for-tat transactionality
- Interdependence (pratītyasamutpāda) - actions ripple through network of relationships

**Neuroscience parallel**:
- Intrinsically motivated reciprocity (non-external reinforcement) creates **more stable behavioral changes** than reward-based reciprocity
- DMN connectivity increase suggests nonexternal reciprocity integrates with self-concept
- Long-term sustainability from intrinsic rather than transactional motivation
