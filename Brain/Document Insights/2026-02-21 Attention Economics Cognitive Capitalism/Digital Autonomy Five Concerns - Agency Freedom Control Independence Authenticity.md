## Core Framework

The philosophical literature on digital manipulation identifies **five distinct autonomy-related concerns** that dark patterns and addictive design violate. These are not redundant - each addresses a different dimension of self-authorship. Digital minimalism and cognitive sovereignty practices address all five simultaneously, which is why they function as comprehensive responses to cognitive capitalism rather than partial coping strategies.

The central ethical claim: if desires are being systematically manipulated by addictive design, digital minimalism is not a personal preference but a **moral obligation** - it is required for authentic self-authorship.

## The Five Autonomy Concerns

### 1. Agency
**Concern**: Dark patterns and addictive design reduce effective behavioral agency - the capacity to act in accordance with one's own intentions rather than platform-induced impulses
**Example violation**: Compulsive checking behavior driven by IVR schedules that users themselves describe as unwanted (the 31% problem)
**What digital minimalism restores**: Alignment between intention and action

### 2. Freedom of Choice
**Concern**: Manufactured urgency, manufactured scarcity, and dark-pattern friction in exit flows distort the choice architecture so that "free" choices are made under conditions that systematically favor platform engagement
**Example violation**: Subscription cancellation dark patterns; auto-play that removes the choice to stop
**What digital minimalism restores**: Genuine optionality at decision points

### 3. Control
**Concern**: Users lack effective control over their engagement patterns due to habituated responses and the neural recalibration effects of prolonged platform use
**Example violation**: Notification disabling does not significantly reduce checking frequency (2024 RCT, n=205) - the habit is deeper than the trigger
**What digital minimalism restores**: Effective (not merely nominal) control over digital behavior

### 4. Independence
**Concern**: Platform algorithms, recommendation systems, and filter bubbles systematically shape beliefs, preferences, and worldviews in directions that serve platform engagement rather than user autonomy
**Example violation**: Identity externalization through behavioral profiling; algorithmic selection of belief-confirming content
**What digital minimalism restores**: Epistemically independent belief formation

### 5. Authenticity
**Concern**: When the sense of what one wants is shaped by algorithmic profiling and intent commodification, the desires themselves may not be authentic expressions of the self - they may be manufactured preferences optimized for commercial extraction
**Example violation**: The intention economy's profiling of motivational patterns, then using those profiles to shape future desires
**What digital minimalism restores**: Confidence that one's desires reflect genuine self-expression rather than platform engineering

## The Duty Argument

The paper argues that because all five autonomy dimensions are systematically violated by current platform design, individuals who value self-authorship have a genuine duty to adopt digital minimalism practices - not merely a prudential reason, but an ethical obligation.

The argument structure:
1. Self-authorship (being the author of one's own life story) is a fundamental value
2. Addictive design systematically undermines the conditions for self-authorship
3. Therefore, practices that protect the conditions for self-authorship (digital minimalism) are obligatory for those who value self-authorship
