# ACC Prediction-Error Signals Drive Task-Switching - Optogenetic Evidence

## Core Finding

The anterior cingulate cortex (ACC) is specifically required for **rapid task-switching** - but only when it exhibits **neural prediction-error signals**. These signals are projection-target dependent and larger preceding successful behavioral transitions. Without ACC prediction-error signals, task-switching fails; silencing the ACC selectively impairs switching without disrupting stable task execution.

---

## Evidence

**Study Design**: Optogenetic silencing of ACC neurons + widefield calcium imaging in mice performing a task-switching paradigm

**Key Results**:
- ACC required for rapid behavioral transitions between tasks
- ACC only necessary WHEN it shows prediction-error activity (not for stable performance)
- Prediction-error signals vary by neural projection target (pathway-specific)
- Larger signals preceding SUCCESSFUL transitions (predictive rather than reactive)

**Why this is significant**: This is optogenetic evidence - the most causally rigorous available - not correlation. The ACC's role is gated by its prediction-error state, not uniformly active.

---

## The Prediction-Error Gating Principle

The ACC does not simply "monitor errors" and "signal task-switching." The mechanism is more specific:

1. **Stable performance**: ACC relatively quiet; prediction errors small; no task-switching
2. **Increasing prediction errors**: ACC becomes active with prediction-error signals
3. **Threshold crossed**: Sufficient prediction-error accumulation triggers task-switch signal
4. **Successful transition**: Larger prediction-error signal predicted this outcome

This makes the ACC a **Bayesian threshold detector** for task-switching - it monitors the reliability of current task representations and signals when prediction errors indicate a switch is needed.
