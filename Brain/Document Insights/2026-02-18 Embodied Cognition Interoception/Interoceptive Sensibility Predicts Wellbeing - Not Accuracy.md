# Interoceptive Sensibility Predicts Wellbeing - Not Accuracy

**Source**: "Minding the Body: A Meta-analysis of the Effects of Mindfulness Meditation Training on Self-reported Interoception" - Scientific Reports (Nature), 2025 - PMID: 40502790 - https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-22661-4; "Interoceptive Mechanisms and Emotional Processing" - Annual Review of Psychology, 2025 - https://www.annualreviews.org/content/journals/10.1146/annurev-psych-020924-125202

**Extraction Date**: 2026-02-18

## Core Insight

Objective interoceptive accuracy - the ability to accurately count one's own heartbeats - is **poorly correlated with wellbeing and clinical outcomes**. What predicts wellbeing is **interoceptive sensibility**: the subjective belief that one's bodily signals are meaningful and worth attending to. Meditation does not work by making you more accurate at detecting body signals. It works by changing your *relationship* to those signals - increasing acceptance and treating them as informative.

---

## Evidence

**Study Design**: Meta-analysis of 29 randomized controlled trials
**Sample Size**: n = 2,191 participants
**Effect Size**: Small-to-medium positive effect on self-reported interoception (low-to-moderate heterogeneity)
**Key Comparison**: Mindfulness-based programs showed larger effects than body-based approaches (massage, yoga)

**What Improved**: Self-reported interoceptive sensibility (the felt sense that body signals matter)
**What Did Not Reliably Improve**: Objective heartbeat detection accuracy
**What Drove Clinical Benefit**: The sensibility improvements correlated with reductions in psychological distress

---

## The Accuracy-Sensibility Distinction

| Construct | Definition | Measurement | Wellbeing Correlation |
|-----------|------------|-------------|----------------------|
| Interoceptive Accuracy | Correctly detecting body signals (e.g., heartbeat counting task) | Objective behavioral tasks | Weak / inconsistent |
| Interoceptive Sensibility | Belief that body signals are meaningful and informative | MAIA questionnaire, self-report | Strong |
| Interoceptive Awareness | Metacognitive awareness of interoceptive processes | MAIA subscales | Variable |

---

## Why This Is Counterintuitive

The assumption driving much interoception research has been that better = more accurate. More accurate heartbeat detection → better emotional awareness → better wellbeing. But this linear model is wrong.

The mechanism appears to be **attentional and relational** rather than perceptual:
- A person with high sensibility who cannot accurately count heartbeats still attends to what their body signals and treats those signals as worth understanding
- This relational stance - "my body is worth listening to" - changes behavior, reduces avoidance of bodily sensations, and improves emotional processing
- Accuracy without sensibility provides no clinical benefit (you can accurately detect a heartbeat and still dismiss it as irrelevant)

---

## Implications for Practice

**For Meditation Research**:
- Stop using heartbeat counting as the primary outcome measure
- The MAIA (Multidimensional Assessment of Interoceptive Awareness) captures the right construct
- Short interventions that improve sensibility may be as useful as accuracy training

**For Clinical Practice**:
- Exposure to and acceptance of uncomfortable bodily sensations (not just awareness) is what heals
- Mindfulness programs that cultivate a curious, non-avoidant stance toward body signals work via this mechanism
- The "not distracting" subscale of MAIA (ability to engage with unpleasant sensations without avoidance) showed the largest gains in body scan RCTs

**For Body Scan Practice** (two-week RCT, Wiley 2025):
- Two weeks of daily body scan improved interoceptive sensibility specifically
- Largest gains in acceptance of bodily sensations, not just awareness
- Minimum effective dose for interoceptive sensibility change: 2 weeks daily practice
