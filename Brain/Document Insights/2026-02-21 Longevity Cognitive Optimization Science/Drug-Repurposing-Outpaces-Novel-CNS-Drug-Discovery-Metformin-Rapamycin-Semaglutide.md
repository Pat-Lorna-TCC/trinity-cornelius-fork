# Drug Repurposing Outpaces Novel CNS Drug Discovery for Cognitive Aging - Pragmatic Pharmacology Wins

## Core Insight

The most promising cognitive aging interventions of 2024-2025 are **repurposed drugs already approved for other conditions** rather than novel pharmacological discoveries. Metformin ($0.10/pill for diabetes), semaglutide (obesity/diabetes), dasatinib (cancer), quercetin and fisetin (plant polyphenols), rapamycin (immunosuppression) are all being studied for cognitive protection. This reflects both scientific pragmatism and the extraordinary difficulty of novel CNS drug development - but also reveals a structural insight about how longevity mechanisms overlap with other disease pathways.

## Why Repurposed Drugs Are Winning

1. **Already approved**: Bypasses 10-15 years of safety development; can be deployed rapidly if cognitive trials succeed
2. **Known safety profiles**: Decades of patient-years data; risk is quantified
3. **Affordable**: Metformin ($0.10/pill), quercetin, fisetin accessible to anyone
4. **Multi-mechanism action**: These drugs weren't designed for single-target precision; their breadth is an advantage for multi-mechanism aging processes
5. **Self-selection effect**: Drugs that survived regulatory approval for one indication are structurally more robust than most candidates in CNS pipelines

**The structural insight**: Aging mechanisms (mTOR dysregulation, metabolic failure, inflammaging, cellular senescence) share pathways with diabetes, cancer, and metabolic syndrome. Drugs that treat these conditions *incidentally* modulate aging pathways - hence the repurposing opportunity.

---

## Why Novel CNS Drug Development Fails

- Blood-brain barrier: Most candidate molecules can't reach the brain at effective concentrations
- CNS complexity: Highly interconnected systems produce unpredictable off-target effects
- Target validation time: CNS drug trials require 5-10 year endpoints; feedback cycles are slow
- Failure rate: ~95% of CNS drug candidates fail Phase 2/3 trials - highest failure rate of any disease category
- The paradox: The most effective interventions are turning out to be the ones targeting conserved biology (mTOR, AMPK, inflammation) rather than disease-specific mechanisms

---

## Caveat: The Translation Problem Remains

The repurposing strategy faces a critical uncertainty: whether benefits observed in diseased populations (diabetics benefiting from metformin, obese patients from semaglutide) translate to **cognitively healthy, non-diabetic individuals** using these drugs preventively.

- Metformin users with T2DM show cognitive protection vs. other diabetics - but this compares to a diseased control, not healthy individuals
- Semaglutide's EVOKE trial targets early-symptomatic AD - not prevention in healthy populations
- The dose-response for non-diseased populations is largely undefined

This gap is the primary next frontier: defining optimal preventive protocols for individuals *before* metabolic or cognitive pathology develops.
