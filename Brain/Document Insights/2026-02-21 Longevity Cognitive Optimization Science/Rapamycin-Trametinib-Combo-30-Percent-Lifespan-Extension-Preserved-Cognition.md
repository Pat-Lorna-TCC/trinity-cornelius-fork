# Rapamycin + Trametinib Combination Produces 30% Lifespan Extension with Preserved Cognitive Function in Mice

**Source 1**: "Suppressing Two Signals of Aging: Rapamycin-Trametinib Study." *Frontiers in Aging* (2025). https://www.gethealthspan.com/research/article/rapamycin-and-trametinib

## Core Insight

The combination of rapamycin (mTOR inhibitor) and trametinib (MEK/ERK inhibitor) produces **non-overlapping, synergistic effects** on aging pathways - achieving 30% average lifespan extension in mice while *preserving* cognitive function. The critical insight is that targeting two independent aging pathways simultaneously (mTOR axis + Ras-ERK axis) produces supralinear benefits that neither drug alone achieves. This is the most compelling preclinical evidence for the "parallel pathway" strategy in longevity medicine.

**Critical human caveat**: Cognitive benefits have not yet been demonstrated in humans; CNS penetration of rapamycin is uncertain.

---

## Evidence

**Preclinical study (2025)**:
- Rapamycin + Trametinib vs. controls: **30% average lifespan extension**
- Reduced tumor burden and systemic inflammation
- Trametinib specifically reduced microglial and astrocyte activation in the striatum
- Brain glucose uptake - which abnormally increases with aging and neuronal stress - was normalized in treated mice
- Cognitive and metabolic health preservation exceeded either drug alone

**Human rapamycin trials (2024-2025)**:
- **PEARL Trial** (114 healthy adults, 50-85 years, 12 months, published September 2024): Largest, longest RCT of rapamycin in healthy aging
- **Gonzales et al. Phase 1** (Nature Communications Medicine 2025): Rapamycin for MCI/Alzheimer's dementia - favorable biomarker changes; cognitive outcomes not yet significant
- **ERAP Trial** (NCT06022068): 7mg rapamycin weekly, 6 months in early AD; primary endpoint: cerebral glucose uptake via FDG-PET
- **EVERLAST Trial** (NCT05835999): Everolimus for cerebral blood flow in insulin-resistant adults 55-80

## The Two-Pathway Strategy

Rapamycin and trametinib target **independent, non-overlapping** aging mechanisms:

| Drug | Target | Primary Aging Pathway |
|------|--------|----------------------|
| Rapamycin | mTOR (mechanistic target of rapamycin) | Cellular growth/repair balance; autophagy; protein synthesis |
| Trametinib | MEK/ERK (Ras-ERK signaling) | Cellular proliferation; inflammatory signaling; senescence |

The non-overlap is key: combining them doesn't just add effects - it blocks two independent entrances to the aging cascade simultaneously. The mouse data show normalized brain glucose metabolism and reduced neuroinflammation (trametinib's striatal glial suppression) + cellular recycling (rapamycin's mTOR pathway).

---

## Current Human Evidence Gap

The critical gap: longevity findings are compelling in mice, but:
1. CNS penetration of rapamycin is uncertain - the brain may be inadequately reached at doses used in peripheral aging studies
2. mTOR inhibition broadly (not selectively) may impair certain cognitive functions (mTOR is needed for memory consolidation)
3. Phase 1 human data (Gonzales 2025) show biomarker improvements but no significant cognitive benefit yet

Dedicated CNS-penetrant analogs of rapamycin (rapalogs) may be needed to translate the mouse cognitive findings to humans.
