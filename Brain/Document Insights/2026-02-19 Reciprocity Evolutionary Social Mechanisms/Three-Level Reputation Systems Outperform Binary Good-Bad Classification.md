# Three-Level Reputation Systems Outperform Binary Good-Bad Classification

**Source**: Yamamoto, H., Okada, I., & Suzuki, T. (2025). Gradual reputation dynamics evolve and sustain cooperation in indirect reciprocity. PLOS ONE, 20(8), e0329742
**Document Type**: Research Paper
**Extracted By**: AI (document-insight-extractor agent)
**Extraction Date**: 2026-02-19
**Session**: 2026-02-19 Reciprocity Evolutionary Social Mechanisms

## Core Insight

Three-level reputation systems (good/neutral/bad) with gradual transitions dramatically outperform binary (good/bad) systems in sustaining cooperation. The "gradating social norm" allows cooperative acts to improve reputation by one level and defection to decrease by one level, with the critical exception that **defection against bad actors does not harm the donor's reputation** - explaining why "punishing bad people" is not reputationally costly.

---

## Evidence

**Study Design**: Evolutionary game theory modeling + experimental validation with human participants

**Sample Size**: **1,850 responses** from Japanese participants across 18 scenarios

**Key Results**:
- **"Tolerant discriminator" strategy** (cooperating with good and neutral reputations) achieves **near-complete cooperation**
- Less effective strategies achieve only ~33% cooperation
- The gradual system matches human psychological reality - people naturally implement graded assessments, not binary judgments
- Neutral state acts as buffer allowing recovery from occasional defection

**Mechanism**:
1. Cooperative act → reputation improves by one level (bad→neutral→good)
2. Defection → reputation decreases by one level (good→neutral→bad)
3. **Exception**: Defection against bad-reputation actors doesn't harm donor

**Why It Works**:
- Allows reputation recovery (second chances)
- Neutral zone prevents harsh binary punishment
- Enables norm enforcement without punisher cost
- Matches human moral intuition
