## Core Framework

Building on Fricker's (2007) foundational taxonomy of epistemic injustice (testimonial injustice: when a speaker's credibility is diminished due to identity prejudice; hermeneutical injustice: when social experience is obscured due to gaps in collective understanding), Mollema proposes a new AI-specific category: **generative hermeneutical erasure**.

The key insight: AI systems don't just reflect existing epistemic injustice - they *automate and scale* it. Specifically:
1. AI "disqualifies persons in their capacity as knowers"
2. The complexity and opacity of AI-driven interventions are "inherent barriers to any independent effort at comparable sense-making"
3. AI systems suppress conceptual diversity that marginalized communities need to make sense of their own experiences

This is not merely bias in AI outputs but the automation of epistemicide - the systematic destruction of knowledge traditions - embedded in how AI systems process and generate language.

---

## The Three Levels of Fricker's Framework Applied to AI

**Testimonial Injustice via AI**:
- LLMs trained on WEIRD data systematically underweight contributions from non-WEIRD knowledge traditions
- When users ask AI about indigenous healing practices, the AI's authority framing implicitly positions Western medical frameworks as the legitimate baseline
- The AI "grants" or "denies" credibility to different knowledge claims based on its training distribution

**Hermeneutical Injustice via AI**:
- Communities that lack widely-shared conceptual vocabulary (because their experiences are marginal in training data) cannot effectively communicate with or through AI systems
- Example: Cultural concepts with no English equivalent are poorly handled by English-dominant LLMs
- The AI system doesn't just fail to express these concepts - it actively reconstructs them within the dominant conceptual framework, erasing their distinctiveness

**Generative Hermeneutical Erasure (New Category)**:
- Goes beyond passively reflecting existing gaps
- AI *generates* new language that fills gaps with dominant cultural assumptions
- When AI creates content about marginalized experiences, it generates culturally-filtered interpretations that then circulate as "objective" representations
- The opacity of AI generation makes this invisible - users cannot trace the cultural filtering in AI-generated text

## The New Category: Doxastic Injustice (2025)

A parallel 2025 *Social Epistemology* paper introduces **doxastic injustice**: epistemic disempowerment occurring "when individuals struggle to correlate their experiences with available linguistic resources, despite the existence of relevant concepts."

This names a phenomenon distinct from hermeneutical injustice:
- Hermeneutical injustice: the gap in collective understanding (structural, at the social level)
- Doxastic injustice: the individual's struggle to connect their experience to available concepts (psychological, at the individual level)

AI systems contribute to doxastic injustice when they:
- Present dominant cultural interpretations of experiences that the user doesn't recognize as their own
- Provide confident answers that short-circuit the user's own sense-making process
- Make users doubt their own experiential knowledge when it conflicts with AI-generated accounts

## Why This Matters for the Vault's AI Framework

The vault extensively covers AI through technical and performance lenses (architecture, memory, tool use, evaluation benchmarks). Hermeneutical erasure is a third axis - not performance (does AI do the task well?) or alignment (does AI behave according to instructions?) but **epistemic justice** (does AI participation in knowledge-making preserve or destroy the diversity of ways humans make sense of the world?).

This is a fundamentally different critique than "AI is biased toward Western values." It argues that AI participation in knowledge generation is structurally epistemically unjust - not because of correctable biases but because the *mechanism* (training on existing text, generating probability distributions over human language) tends toward suppression of minority conceptual frameworks.

---

## The Opacity Problem

The argument that AI's "complexity and opacity are inherent barriers to any independent effort at comparable sense-making" is critical. Traditional hermeneutical injustice (being unable to articulate one's experience due to lack of shared vocabulary) has in principle been correctable - communities have developed their own conceptual vocabularies, languages, cultural traditions.

Hermeneutical erasure via AI resists this correction:
- Users can't see which cultural assumptions are embedded in AI responses
- AI-generated content is produced at scale (overwhelming alternative framings)
- AI outputs have authority that marginalizes individual counter-framing
- The generation process itself encodes dominant frameworks in the statistical structure of language
