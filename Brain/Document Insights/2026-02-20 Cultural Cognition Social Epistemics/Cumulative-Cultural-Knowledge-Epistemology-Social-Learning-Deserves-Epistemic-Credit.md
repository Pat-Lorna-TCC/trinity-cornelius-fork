## Core Framework

**Cumulative Cultural Knowledge (CCK)** - knowledge refined and transmitted across generations through cultural practices, craft traditions, institutional wisdom, and embodied skills - has been systematically undervalued by mainstream epistemology. The core problem: under mainstream epistemological theories (especially testimony-based accounts), agents who acquire knowledge through social learning do not "deserve epistemic credit" for that knowledge. They didn't independently verify it; they received it socially.

The 2024 *Social Epistemology* paper argues this is a major theoretical failure because **CCK is the primary mode of knowledge acquisition for most of human history**. Hunter-gatherer knowledge of local ecology, traditional navigation techniques, medicinal plant knowledge, craft skills - all of these are CCK, and all are systematically undervalued by epistemological theories designed for individual knowers investigating propositions.

---

## The Epistemic Credit Problem

Under mainstream testimony epistemology:
- If I believe P because I witnessed it or reasoned to it independently, I deserve full epistemic credit for knowing P
- If I believe P because an expert told me, I deserve *conditional* epistemic credit (contingent on the expert's reliability)
- If I believe P because it is embedded in my cultural practices and I acquired it through participation, I deserve... what?

The third case (CCK) is the largest category of human knowledge acquisition, yet epistemology has focused almost entirely on the first two. The paper proposes that CCK through social learning (behavioral observation, participation in practice, imitation) deserves full epistemic credit under appropriate conditions - conditions that relate to the *fidelity and effectiveness of cultural transmission mechanisms*, not individual verification.

---

## Why This Connects to Cultural Evolutionary Mechanisms

The paper's proposed conditions for CCK epistemic credit map directly onto the cultural evolutionary mechanisms documented in this session:

- **Prestige bias transmission**: Knowledge transmitted from high-prestige practitioners has high fidelity - the prestige tracks track record of success
- **Payoff bias transmission**: Knowledge acquired by observing successful outcomes has direct epistemic warrant (observational, not testimonial)
- **Vertical transmission**: Parent-to-child transmission of embodied knowledge has high fidelity through sustained practice apprenticeship

These are not cases of testimony (someone tells you a proposition) - they are forms of **non-testimonial cultural learning** that convey genuine know-how alongside propositional knowledge.

## The Group Justification Problem

Brown's (2024) alternative group justification account addresses a related question: when can a group be said to *know* something, even when not all individual members have individually justified beliefs?

Standard accounts require individual members' beliefs to be individually justified for group belief to be justified. Brown argues this is too restrictive: a group can have collectively justified belief when the *group-level epistemic practice* is reliable, even if individual members defer without independent verification.

**Applied to cultural knowledge**: A community that has reliably navigated by stars for generations has epistemically justified collective knowledge of celestial navigation, even if no individual member has independently verified the astronomical calculations. The group-level epistemic practice (multigenerational refinement, practical testing, fidelity of transmission) justifies the collective knowledge.

This matters for **decolonial epistemology**: indigenous CCK has been dismissed as "not really knowledge" because it fails individual-epistemic-credit criteria. The group justification framework provides a rigorous epistemic defense.

## AI Training Data Implications

This framework has a significant implication for AI epistemics: LLM training data is almost entirely in the form of explicit propositional text. CCK - embodied skills, tacit knowledge, participatory know-how - is almost entirely excluded from training data.

This means:
- LLMs systematically underrepresent the largest category of human knowledge
- LLMs cannot evaluate the epistemic quality of CCK claims (having no framework for non-testimonial cultural learning)
- LLMs may systematically disadvantage communities whose knowledge is primarily CCK-form
- This is a structural form of hermeneutical erasure: the very form of valuable indigenous knowledge is invisible to AI epistemic evaluation
