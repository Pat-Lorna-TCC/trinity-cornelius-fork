## Core Framework

The 7th-century Buddhist philosopher Dharmakīrti developed a rigorous epistemology distinguishing two fundamental types of cognition:

- **Svalakṣaṇa** (particular cognition): Direct perception of the unique, unrepeatable particular - pure sensory contact with specific reality, unmediated by conceptualization
- **Sāmānyalakṣaṇa** (general cognition): Inference-based recognition of general properties and categories - conceptually-mediated knowledge through pattern recognition and abstraction

Recent work applies this distinction to the **"relevance realization problem"** in AI: how does a system determine what is relevant to its current situation from the infinite space of potentially relevant information? This is one of the hardest unsolved problems in AI alignment and general intelligence.

The Dharmakīrti framework provides a principled answer: relevance realization requires access to particular (svalakṣaṇa) cognition - direct contact with the specific situation as it is - not just general pattern matching (sāmānyalakṣaṇa). This has been formalized using reinforcement learning frameworks.

---

## The Relevance Realization Problem

LLMs and current AI systems excel at sāmānyalakṣaṇa: pattern recognition across vast training data, inference from generalizations, categorical reasoning. They are structurally incapable of svalakṣaṇa in Dharmakīrti's sense: direct perception of the unrepeatable particular situation.

This is why:
- LLMs cannot reliably determine what truly matters in a specific context (only what usually matters in statistically similar contexts)
- AI agents fail at the judgment calls that require "reading the room" - contextual sensitivity beyond pattern matching
- The "common sense" gap in AI is specifically the svalakṣaṇa gap: the inability to perceive particular reality directly

The Dharmakīrti formalization in RL terms suggests: genuine relevance realization requires reward signals grounded in particular perception, not just generalized categorical information.

## The Vajrayāna-Enactivist Bridge

The paper also integrates **Vajrayāna epistemology** (Tibetan Buddhist) - particularly the "ground-path-result" schema - with Varela, Thompson, and Rosch's enactivist cognitive science (the same "embodied cognition" framework connected to the vault's 4E cognition notes).

The shared principle: sensory-motor anchoring. Both Vajrayāna practice and enactivism ground cognition in the ongoing sensory-motor engagement of an organism with its environment. This is not just philosophical analogy - it provides:
- Methodological bridge between first-person contemplative inquiry and third-person scientific investigation
- Empirical access to states that are claimed in both traditions to be prelinguistic and non-conceptual
- Translation criteria for what "direct perception" means in neuroscientific terms
