## Core Insight

The surge in Spanish nationalist displays following the Catalan independence referendum (2017) was driven not by changing individual preferences but by a **rapid transformation in perceived norms** - what people believed others in their community were doing and endorsing. This case demonstrates that social norms can redefine perceived reality quickly and can both **amplify and mitigate** polarization, depending on context. Crucially: many behaviors that appear to reflect genuine preference change are actually norm-following behavior with stable underlying preferences.

---

## Evidence

**Catalan Case (Social Inclusion, 2025)**:
- Following the 2017 Catalan independence referendum, Spanish nationalist display behaviors (flags, symbols) surged
- The surge was disproportionate to any measured change in individual preferences for Spanish identity
- Analysis: transformation in *perceived norms* (what one believed others endorsed) drove the behavioral surge
- **Individuals were not suddenly more Spanish nationalist in their private preferences - they perceived that displaying Spanish identity had become newly normative**

**Theoretical Framework (PMC, 2024)**:
- A theoretical model of polarization as gradual fragmentation identifies normative signaling as a third pathway to polarization beyond informational influence and social comparison
- Norm-driven polarization is particularly dangerous because: it is rapid, it is decoupled from preference change, and it is vulnerable to spiral dynamics
