## Core Framework

**Radical Collective Intelligence (Radical CI)** proposes that cognitive functions - not just their outputs - are constitutively distributed across collective entities and cultural institutions. This is stronger than the standard distributed cognition claim:

- Standard distributed cognition: individuals perform cognition, but the environment scaffolds it
- **Radical CI**: "Representations and processes necessary to perform cognitive functions are collective entities, not encapsulated by any individual"

Applied to the full spectrum of cognitive functions:
- **Memory**: What is "remembered" includes cultural archives, institutional practices, transmitted traditions - not just neural storage
- **Metacognition**: "Knowing what you know" is partially a social function - communities maintain epistemic norms that regulate individual belief assessment
- **Belief updating**: The norms and evidence standards by which beliefs change are institutionally maintained
- **Reasoning**: Logical and rhetorical norms that govern valid inference are culturally transmitted and collectively maintained
- **Problem-solving**: Solution strategies are inherited from cultural practices, not independently derived

## Andy Clark's Extension to Generative AI (2025)

Andy Clark - who with David Chalmers originally proposed the Extended Mind thesis in 1998 - now argues in *Nature Communications* that generative AI (LLMs) is becoming a genuine component of extended cognitive systems.

The original Extended Mind thesis used the famous "Otto and Inga" thought experiment: Otto (who has Alzheimer's) uses a notebook that functions as his external memory, analogous to Inga's biological memory. The functional equivalence justifies treating the notebook as part of Otto's cognitive system.

Clark's 2025 extension argues:
- LLMs satisfy the functional equivalence criteria more completely than notebooks
- They provide dynamic, responsive cognitive support across multiple cognitive functions
- They raise new questions about **cognitive autonomy and dependency** not present with notebooks
- The extended mind with generative AI is qualitatively different: the AI co-constructs responses rather than storing fixed information

**The dependency question**: When cognitive functions are genuinely distributed into AI systems, what happens to those functions when AI is unavailable? This is the same question asked of any extended mind component - but with AI, the stakes are higher because the extension is richer.

## Noesology - Proposing a New Unified Discipline

A 2025 *Journal of Artificial and Augmented Intelligence* paper proposes **Noesology** as a new scientific discipline unifying:
- Human intelligence (cognitive science)
- Artificial intelligence (AI/ML)
- Collective intelligence (social science, organizational theory)
- Evolutionary intelligence (evolutionary biology)
- Complex systems theory (emergence, self-organization)

Defining collective intelligence as: "the aggregated cognitive abilities of a group of individuals or machines working together"

This dissolves the human/AI/collective boundary in intelligence research - all are instances of the same general phenomenon studied by the same discipline.
