## Core Framework

The **ontological turn** in anthropology and psychology proposes that cultures do not merely have different perspectives on the *same* reality - they may literally "inhabit fundamentally different realities." This is not relativism about values or preferences but ontological pluralism about what exists.

The distinction is philosophically significant:
- **Epistemological diversity**: Different methods and frameworks for knowing one shared reality
- **Ontological diversity**: Different realities, each equally real within its constitutive practices and relationships

**The paradigm case**: When a person in a spiritual-reality-centered culture reports seeing "a ghost," Western psychology responds by:
- Explaining it as visual hallucination (epistemological move: applying Western ontology to interpret the report)
- Pathologizing the experience (clinical move: treating non-Western ontology as disorder)

Within the indigenous framework, the experience may mean "presence of the ancestor" - epistemically valid, practically significant, constitutive of the real social world in which the person lives. The "ghost" is not a misperception of physical reality but an encounter with a different category of real entity in a different ontological framework.

## The Mi'kmaq "Two-Eyed Seeing" Approach

The *Etuaptmumk* (Mi'kmaq, Indigenous North American) tradition offers a practical model for navigating ontological plurality without collapsing it:

**Two-Eyed Seeing**: Using indigenous and Western knowledge systems simultaneously - looking through the indigenous eye for some purposes and the Western eye for others, without requiring resolution of the ontological incompatibility.

This contrasts with:
- **Assimilationism**: Indigenous knowledge is valid only when translatable into Western scientific terms
- **Separatism**: Indigenous knowledge is only valid within indigenous contexts, Western science is only valid within Western contexts
- **Relativism**: All knowledge systems are equally valid for all purposes

Two-Eyed Seeing is pragmatic ontological pluralism: acknowledging that different knowledge systems engage different realities effectively, without requiring a meta-ontology that ranks them.

## Why This Is Philosophically Challenging

Mainstream cognitive science operates on a background assumption of **ontological monism**: there is one physical reality, and all cultural differences are differences in how that reality is perceived, interpreted, or valued. Even sophisticated cross-cultural psychology (including the WEIRD critique) operates within this framework - the goal is to understand *universal* human cognition by using more representative samples.

Ontological pluralism challenges this at the foundation:
- The project of "universal cognitive science" may be an ethnocentric project
- "Cognitive processes" are not universal functions that vary in content across cultures but may be genuinely different across ontological frameworks
- Cross-cultural psychology may be systematically misunderstanding non-WEIRD cognition by forcing it into a Western ontological framework

## The "Pluriversal Psychology" Vision

The APA 2025 special issue on Decolonial Psychology frames the project as building **pluriversal psychology** where "many worlds can not only coexist but also develop together."

This is different from multiculturalism (many cultures in one world) - it asserts that the diversity of ontological frameworks is a cognitive and epistemic resource, not a problem to be resolved through translation to a universal framework.

**Practical implication for AI**: If different cultures inhabit different ontological realities, then AI systems that operate within a single ontological framework (e.g., materialist-physicalist Western ontology) will systematically misprocess queries that presuppose different ontologies. This is not correctable by adding cultural training data - it requires ontological pluralism in the AI architecture itself.

## Relationship to Buddhist Epistemology in the Vault

This framework connects to Buddhist insights about the constructed nature of reality. Buddhism argues that what we experience as "reality" is a construction of conceptualization - dependent origination, not independent substance. This is consistent with ontological pluralism: different conceptual frameworks (cultures) construct different experienced realities.

The Buddhist perspective adds a layer: even the ontological frameworks themselves are constructed, dependent, ultimately empty of inherent existence. This is neither monism (one real reality) nor naive pluralism (all frameworks equally real) but a dynamic middle path - acknowledging that frameworks construct real experienced realities while recognizing the constructed nature of the frameworks themselves.
