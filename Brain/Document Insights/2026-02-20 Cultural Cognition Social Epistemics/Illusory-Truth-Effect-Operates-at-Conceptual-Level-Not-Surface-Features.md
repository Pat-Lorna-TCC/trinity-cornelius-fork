# Illusory Truth Effect Operates at Conceptual Level - Paraphrasing Does Not Break It

## Core Insight

The **illusory truth effect** - the well-established finding that repeated claims are rated as more truthful, regardless of their accuracy - operates at the **conceptual level**, not at the level of surface features like wording or source identity. A 2025 study by Pillai and Fazio demonstrates that changing the wording of a claim (paraphrasing) or changing the attributed source does not break the effect. What matters is familiarity with the *underlying concept*, not recognition of the specific phrasing.

---

## Evidence

**Study Design**: Experimental paradigm testing illusory truth with paraphrased claims across wording variations and source changes
**Key Results**:
- Repeated claims rated as more truthful regardless of wording changes (paraphrase condition)
- Repeated claims rated as more truthful regardless of source changes
- **Supporting conceptual-level familiarity as the mechanism**, not surface feature recognition
**Publication Context**: 2025, referenced in the *Frontiers in Social Psychology* integrative review of misinformation psychology

---

## Why This Matters

The standard understanding of illusory truth was that **familiarity** (perceptual fluency from exact-match recognition) made claims feel true. The paraphrase finding overturns this:

- The mechanism is not "I recognize these exact words"
- The mechanism is "I have a sense of familiarity with this *idea*"
- Conceptual representation, not verbal representation, is what becomes "fluent"

**Practical implications for misinformation**:
1. **Corrections cannot rely on source-switching**: "That claim is false, and here is the original source" doesn't work because conceptual familiarity persists regardless of source
2. **Paraphrased misinformation is as dangerous as verbatim repetition**: Content farms that rephrase the same false claims are not reducing illusory truth effects
3. **AI-generated misinformation at scale**: LLMs can generate infinite paraphrases of the same false concept, all independently building conceptual-level illusory truth
4. **The "liar's dividend"**: Even if specific AI-generated fakes are debunked, the repeated conceptual exposure to the idea (e.g., "this election was stolen") may independently increase perceived truth
