## Core Insight

Wisdom is not a stable personality trait that people either have or don't have - it is an emergent process that fluctuates substantially within the same individual across different situations. This represents a fundamental paradigm shift: wisdom is something you DO, not something you ARE.

Grossmann's lab provides a formal network model in which wisdom emerges from dynamic interactions among cognitive, affective, and metacognitive components, rather than from a static underlying disposition.

---

## Evidence

**Study Design**: Multiple converging studies using ecological momentary assessment and situation-specific measurement
**Key Results**: Wisdom-related cognition - including intellectual humility and perspective-taking - varies systematically across situations and cannot be adequately captured by a single trait score
**Replication**: Two converging papers from Grossmann's lab (2025), building on a cluster of earlier work
**Implication for measurement**: Standard self-report wisdom scales (e.g., the 3D-WS) systematically underestimate situational variability. Ecological momentary assessment is now advocated.
**Citation**: Grossmann (2025), European Review of Social Psychology; Journal of Experimental Psychology: General

---

## Why This Matters

This finding has three cascading implications:

1. **Measurement**: Trait-based wisdom scales miss the point - you need to measure wisdom IN specific contexts
2. **Intervention**: Context design matters as much as dispositional training. Changing your environment may be as important as changing yourself
3. **Identity**: You cannot "become" wise in a global sense - you can only cultivate the conditions under which wise reasoning reliably emerges
