# Self-Distancing Eliminates the Solomon Paradox in Daily Life

**Source**: EMA study (2025). "The frequency, form, and function of self-talk in everyday life." *Scientific Reports / PMC*. https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12592353/ (12,966 surveys from 208 participants over two weeks)

## Core Insight

People reason MORE wisely about other people's problems than their own (Solomon's Paradox - the self-other asymmetry in wisdom). Self-distancing - using your own name and non-first-person pronouns in self-talk - eliminates this asymmetry and produces laboratory-level wisdom benefits in the real-world conditions of daily life.

---

## Evidence

**Study Design**: Ecological momentary assessment - 12,966 surveys from 208 participants over two weeks. Examined distanced vs. immersed self-talk in actual daily situations.

**Key Findings**:
- Distanced self-talk (using own name and non-first-person pronouns) is LESS COMMON than immersed self-talk across all situation types
- When used, distanced self-talk produced significant regulatory benefits
- Laboratory mechanism confirmed in daily life: Solomon's Paradox holds outside the lab

**What self-distancing enhanced**: Dialecticism, intellectual humility, cooperation, bipartisan thinking - the core components of wise reasoning

**Mechanism (Grossmann-Kross)**: People reason more wisely about others' problems because psychological distance reduces the emotional self-involvement that triggers defensive reasoning. Self-distancing synthetically creates this distance while keeping the person engaged with their own situation.

---

## The Buddhist Observer Perspective

The phenomenology of self-distancing maps directly onto Buddhist "witnessing awareness" - observing one's experience from a third-person perspective rather than being fused with it. The "observer self" in contemplative traditions is not mysticism; it is a documented cognitive mode with measurable regulatory benefits.

This is a rare case where a contemplative technique has been empirically operationalized and confirmed in daily life conditions.

**Key difference from suppression**: Self-distancing does not suppress emotion or involvement - it shifts the perspective on the situation. The person remains engaged but from a slightly removed vantage point. This is why it works where pure "detachment" often fails.

---

## Practical Protocol

Self-distancing in practice:
1. Refer to yourself by name in internal monologue ("What should [Name] do here?")
2. Use second or third person: "You are facing X. What would a wise person do?"
3. Ask: "What would I advise a friend in this exact situation?"

This is low-cost, instantly applicable, and ecologically validated. Unlike most psychological interventions, it works in the messy conditions of real life.
