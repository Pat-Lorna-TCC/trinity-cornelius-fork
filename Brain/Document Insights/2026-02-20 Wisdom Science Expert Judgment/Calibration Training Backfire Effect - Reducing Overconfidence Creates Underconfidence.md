# Calibration Training Backfire Effect - Reducing Overconfidence Creates Underconfidence

**Source**: Martin et al. (2024-2025). "Calibration Feedback with the Practical Scoring Rule Does Not Improve Calibration of Confidence." *Futures & Foresight Science* (N1 = 610, N2 = 871); Kelly & Mandel (2024) commercial calibration training study for intelligence analysts
**Document Type**: Experimental research (two studies)
**Extracted By**: AI (document-insight-extractor agent)
**Extraction Date**: 2026-02-20
**Session**: 2026-02-20 Wisdom Science Expert Judgment

## Core Insight

Calibration training - designed to reduce overconfidence - can paradoxically induce underconfidence as a side effect. Training programs that focus exclusively on "be less confident" solve one calibration problem while creating the opposite one. Effective calibration requires BIDIRECTIONAL targeting that addresses both overconfidence and underconfidence simultaneously.

---

## Evidence

**Kelly & Mandel (2024)**: Commercial calibration training for intelligence analysts
- Effective for improving 90% confidence interval estimates
- **Detrimental** for binary event calibration, where underconfidence was EXACERBATED

**Martin et al. (two experiments)**: N1 = 610, N2 = 871
- Calibration feedback with the Practical Scoring Rule failed to consistently improve calibration
- Showed evidence of asymmetric effects across question types

**What does work**: The classic DARPA-sponsored forecasting tournament study (Developing Expert Political Judgment, *Judgment and Decision Making*) showed that trained forecasters maintained performance edges over controls across a full year - but gains required structured protocols, not just feedback alone.

**Promising direction**: The Practical Scoring Rule (modified logarithmic scoring rule) shows promise for balanced calibration addressing both failure modes, but results have not yet replicated robustly.

---

## Why This Is Counter-Intuitive

The popular framing of calibration is: "people are overconfident, so training them to be less confident improves calibration." This study shows that framing is wrong in a systematically important way.

Calibration is a two-dimensional problem: you can fail by being too confident OR too uncertain. Training that treats it as one-dimensional creates new failures while fixing existing ones.

This has a practical implication for wisdom development: teaching intellectual humility in a one-sided way ("doubt yourself more") can create underconfidence and epistemic paralysis rather than the accurate calibration that genuine IH represents.
