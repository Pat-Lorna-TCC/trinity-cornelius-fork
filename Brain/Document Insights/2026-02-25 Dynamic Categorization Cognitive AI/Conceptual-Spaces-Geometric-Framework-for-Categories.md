## Core Framework

Categories are **regions in multidimensional quality space** built from geometrical structures based on quality dimensions (color, size, temperature, etc.). Similarity between concepts is spatial proximity in this geometric space. Theory remains active in cognitive theories of similarity with 2024-2025 updates.

**Key Innovation**: Provides mathematical foundation for representing categories geometrically rather than symbolically.

## Mechanism: How Geometric Representation Works

**Quality Dimensions** (examples):
- **Color**: Hue, saturation, brightness (3D space)
- **Taste**: Sweet, sour, salty, bitter, umami (5D space)
- **Emotion**: Valence, arousal (2D space)
- **Shape**: Geometric properties (multi-D space)

**Category Formation**:
- **Prototype**: Center point in quality space
- **Category region**: Convex region around prototype
- **Boundaries**: Where regions of different categories meet
- **Similarity**: Distance in geometric space

**Example - "Red"**:
- Region in 3D color space
- Prototypical red at center
- Fuzzy boundaries with orange, pink, maroon
- Distance from prototype = "redness" degree

## Why Geometric Representation Matters

**Advantages over symbolic**:
1. **Gradient membership**: Not binary in/out, but degree of membership
2. **Natural similarity**: Spatial proximity = conceptual similarity
3. **Interpolation**: Can generate intermediate concepts
4. **Dimensionality reduction**: Compress complex concepts into manageable spaces

**Cognitive plausibility**:
- Matches how humans judge similarity
- Explains prototype effects
- Accounts for fuzzy category boundaries
