# Meaning Predicts Longevity Better Than Life Satisfaction

**Source**: MIDUS data analysis. "Which Predicts Longevity Better: Satisfaction With Life or Purpose in Life?" https://midus.wisc.edu/findings/pdfs/2842.pdf
**Document Type**: Longitudinal Research Analysis
**Extracted By**: AI (document-insight-extractor agent)
**Extraction Date**: 2026-02-21
**Session**: 2026-02-21 Meaning Making Purpose Neuroscience

---

## Core Insight

Purpose in life predicts mortality more robustly than life satisfaction alone. This is a counterintuitive and practically significant finding: feeling that life is meaningful is a stronger longevity predictor than feeling that life is good. You can be satisfied but purposeless (hedonic contentment without eudaimonic engagement) and this provides less protection than having purpose even under difficult circumstances.

---

## Evidence

**MIDUS Longitudinal Data**:
- Purposeful individuals: **46% lower mortality risk**
- **33% less likely** to develop sleep problems
- Purpose outperforms life satisfaction on mortality prediction
- Effect holds after controlling for standard confounds

---

## The Distinction That Matters

**Life Satisfaction**: "How good do I feel about my life?" (hedonic dimension - pleasure/comfort assessment)
**Purpose in Life**: "Is my life directed toward something that matters?" (eudaimonic dimension - engagement with valued goals)

The finding suggests the brain/body is more responsive to **meaning-engagement** than to **pleasure-assessment**. Living with direction and purpose - even through difficulty - is more biologically protective than comfort without direction.

**Aristotle's Insight, Empirically Validated**: Eudaimonia (living in accordance with one's best nature, engaged with meaningful activity) is more health-protective than hedonia (pleasure and comfort). This was philosophical intuition for 2,400 years; MIDUS data makes it empirical.
