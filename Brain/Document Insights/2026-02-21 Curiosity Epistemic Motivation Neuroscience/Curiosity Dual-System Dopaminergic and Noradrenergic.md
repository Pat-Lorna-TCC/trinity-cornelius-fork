## Core Framework

Curiosity is not a unitary construct - it operates through at least two distinct neural systems that complement each other:

| System | Type | Trigger | Neural Circuit | Function |
|--------|------|---------|----------------|----------|
| System 1 | Epistemic / Context-based | Prior knowledge creates information gap | SN/VTA → hippocampus → nucleus accumbens + caudate | Resolves prediction errors from existing knowledge |
| System 2 | Perceptual / Novelty-based | Genuinely novel stimuli with no prior context | Locus Coeruleus → norepinephrine projections | Detects novelty, regulates exploration-exploitation |

**Key Distinction**: System 1 requires prior knowledge to generate curiosity (the "information gap" effect - you must know enough to know what you don't know). System 2 fires for genuinely unfamiliar stimuli regardless of prior knowledge. Both systems ultimately serve the brain's drive to build accurate predictive models.

## Context & Acceptance

**Field Acceptance**: Emerging consensus - formally distinguished in 2024 Nature Reviews Neuroscience but not yet in all textbooks
**Supporting Evidence**: Multiple fMRI studies, lifespan neuroimaging (neuromelanin-sensitive MRI for LC integrity), computational modeling
**Aging Differentiation** (bioRxiv 2025, N=lifespan sample): The two systems have distinct aging trajectories:
- LC (System 2) structural integrity declines with age
- Dopaminergic curiosity-memory enhancement (System 1) remains relatively preserved in older adults
- This explains why older adults lose novelty-seeking but not knowledge-gap-driven curiosity
