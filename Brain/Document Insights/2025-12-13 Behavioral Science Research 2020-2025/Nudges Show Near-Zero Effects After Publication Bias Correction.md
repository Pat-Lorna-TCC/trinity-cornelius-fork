# Nudges Show Near-Zero Effects After Publication Bias Correction

**Source**: Hu et al. (2025), "Assessing Nudge Impact: A Comprehensive Second-Order Meta-Analysis," Journal of Behavioral Decision Making
**Document Type**: Second-Order Meta-Analysis
**Extracted By**: AI (document-insight-extractor agent)

## Core Finding

The most comprehensive second-order meta-analysis of nudge effectiveness (synthesizing 13 meta-analyses, 1,638 primary studies, ~30M participants) found that nudge interventions show a small aggregated effect size of d=0.27, which drops to near-zero (d=0.004) after correcting for publication bias. Additionally, most component meta-analyses were rated as low or critically low methodological quality.

---

## Evidence

**Study Design**: Second-order meta-analysis (meta-analysis of meta-analyses)
**Sample Size**: 1,638 primary studies across 13 meta-analyses, approximately 30 million participants
**Key Results**:
- Uncorrected aggregated effect size: d=0.27 (95% CI [0.16, 0.38])
- After publication bias adjustment: d=0.004 (essentially zero)
- Methodological quality: Most meta-analyses rated low or critically low
**Citation**: Hu, Y., et al. (2025), Journal of Behavioral Decision Making
**Replication**: Confirms concerns raised by earlier 2021 PNAS meta-analysis detecting moderate publication bias

---

## Context

This finding represents a **critical challenge to nudge theory's practical effectiveness**. While earlier research suggested small-to-moderate effects, the 2025 analysis reveals that publication bias (tendency to publish positive results) dramatically inflated reported effectiveness.

**Implications for Practice**:
1. Realistic expectations: Nudges produce minimal real-world impact
2. Resource allocation: Cost-benefit analysis must use d=0.004, not d=0.27
3. Methodological rigor: Need for preregistered studies and publication of null results
4. Context dependency: Focus on decision structure interventions (strongest effect type)

**Note on Decision Structure**: Earlier PNAS meta-analysis (2021) found decision structure interventions (organization of choice alternatives) consistently outperform decision information or decision assistance, with food choices particularly responsive.
