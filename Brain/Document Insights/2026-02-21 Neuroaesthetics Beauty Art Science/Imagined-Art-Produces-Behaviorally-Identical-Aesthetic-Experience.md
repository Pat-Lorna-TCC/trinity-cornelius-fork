# Imagined Art Produces Behaviorally Identical Aesthetic Experience - Neurally Distinct

**Source**: Kathofer, M., Lamm, C., Leder, H., Crone, J.S. "Aesthetic Experiences Across Visual Perception and Mental Imagery: Behaviorally Indistinguishable, Neurally Distinct." *iScience*, Vol. 28, No. 6, article 112588, May 2025. DOI: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12148595/
**Document Type**: Empirical Research (University of Vienna)
**Extracted By**: AI (document-insight-extractor agent)
**Extraction Date**: 2026-02-21

## Core Insight

When people vividly imagine previously seen artworks, their aesthetic ratings and emotional responses are statistically indistinguishable from actually seeing them. Yet the underlying neural patterns are measurably different. This creates a striking dissociation: **identical aesthetic experience through different neural routes**.

Highly vivid imagers show the smallest behavioral gaps between imagined and perceived aesthetic experience.

---

## Evidence

**Study Design**: Within-subjects design with 34 participants alternating between direct perception and reimagination of faces and artworks
**Sample Size**: N = 34
**Key Results**:
- Aesthetic ratings for imagined artworks: statistically indistinguishable from direct perception
- Emotional responses to imagined artworks: statistically indistinguishable from direct perception
- Neural substrates: measurably distinct between perception and imagery conditions
- Individual differences: vividness of mental imagery predicts size of behavioral gap (higher vividness = smaller gap)

---

## Why This Is Radical

Standard models of aesthetic experience assume the artwork itself is necessary - beauty inheres in the object and requires perceptual contact. This finding suggests instead that:

1. **Aesthetic experience is primarily about internal neural organization**, not external stimulus properties
2. **Memory of an artwork can sustain its full emotional impact** - the aesthetic relationship persists after exposure ends
3. **Art therapy benefits may arise from imagination alone** - suggesting art therapy for people who cannot physically access art (hospitalized, homebound, incarcerated) may be as effective as direct engagement
4. **The qualia of beauty may be more constructive than receptive** - the brain builds aesthetic experience; the artwork is the occasion, not the cause

---

## Philosophical Implication

This finding supports a **constructivist account of aesthetic experience**: beauty is not in the object but in the brain's organization in response to the object (or to memory/imagination of it). The artwork functions as an invitation to a neural state, not as the container of aesthetic value.

This has deep implications for where we locate "art" - in the object, in the experience, or in the neural architecture that art cultivates over time.

---

## Practical Implications

- **Art-based medicine**: Patients who cannot attend museums or concerts may benefit from guided aesthetic imagery using previously meaningful artworks
- **Memory of beauty**: The emotional impact of great artworks does not fade proportionally with distance from the experience - it can be re-activated through imagination
- **Aesthetic training**: Cultivating vivid mental imagery may be as important as accessing physical art environments
