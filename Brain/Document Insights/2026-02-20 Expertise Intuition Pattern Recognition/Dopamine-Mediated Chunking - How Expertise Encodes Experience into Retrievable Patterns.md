## Core Insight

Chunking - the process by which expertise packs multiple items into single retrievable units - is learned through dopamine-mediated reinforcement in the prefrontal cortex-basal ganglia circuit. This means: (1) working memory limits in expertise domains are not anatomical constraints but **learning constraints** that can be modified through training; (2) chunking strategies are explicitly trainable, not just passively acquired; (3) conditions that disrupt dopamine signaling (extreme stress, extreme reward pressure) will disrupt expert chunking and degrade expertise.

## Evidence

**Study Design**: Neural network model of prefrontal cortex-basal ganglia circuits (computational) + analysis of clinical populations
**Publication**: *eLife*, 2025 (Brown University, Soni & Frank)
**Key Model Findings**:
- The model shows how chunking allows networks to "reuse the same prefrontal populations to store multiple items," creating resource-like constraints
- Adaptive chunking is gated by **dopaminergic signals** into striatal medium spiny neurons
- Working memory limitations stem from **learning constraints**, not anatomical capacity
- The model predicts working memory difficulties in Parkinson's disease, ADHD, and schizophrenia through altered dopamine signaling

**Synaptic Theory (2024 bioRxiv)**: Short-term synaptic plasticity enables "chunking neurons" that selectively gate groups of stimulus-responsive neurons, allowing hierarchical representations and exceeding basic capacity limits.

## The Counter-Intuitive Finding on WM Limits

The standard assumption: working memory capacity is ~4 items ± individual variation, and this is a fundamental anatomical constraint. The Soni & Frank finding: **WM limitations are learning constraints**. The "4 item limit" is an artifact of untrained, unchunked representation.

Experts who have chunked domain information can hold the equivalent of far more raw elements because their chunks are compressed representations of multiple elements. The limit is not the number of chunks but the chunking capacity itself - which is trainable.

**Clinical implication**: WM difficulties in Parkinson's (reduced dopamine), ADHD (altered dopamine signaling), and schizophrenia (dopamine dysregulation) are explained by disrupted chunking at the dopaminergic gating level.

---

## Expert Chunking (De Groot to 2025)

Chase and Simon's (1973) classic chess chunking work showed experts encode larger "chunks" - but only for regular (not random) positions. Modern updates confirm: domain-specific procedural knowledge drives chunking, not general memory capacity.

The 2025 model provides the mechanism: dopamine prediction errors at corticostriatal synapses drive the selective binding of co-occurring elements into retrievable units. This is why:
- Chess patterns chunk for regular positions (learned together through dopamine reinforcement)
- Chess patterns don't chunk for random positions (never reinforced together)
- Expertise is domain-specific (chunking is trained in the exact statistical structure of the domain)

---

## Training Implications

1. **Chunking strategies can be explicitly trained** - not just acquired passively through experience
2. **Training should optimize for dopaminergic gating**: spaced practice (allows synaptic reset), prediction error generation (slightly beyond current capacity), appropriate reward signals
3. **Stress disrupts chunking**: Conditions of extreme reward pressure alter dopamine signaling and can disrupt otherwise automatic expert pattern retrieval
4. **ADHD and expertise training**: The dopamine mechanism explains why ADHD medications (which regulate dopamine) improve working memory performance - they restore chunking capacity
