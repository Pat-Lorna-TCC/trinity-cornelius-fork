## Core Insight

Chess masters' superior memory is constrained to regular chess positions - it does not generalize to other domains. Not even IQ distinguishes chess masters from other chess players. Domain expertise is built from domain-specific representations that cannot be trivially transferred. Even within broad domains, transfer is minimal: **poetry-writing training did not improve story-writing creativity** - two apparently related sub-domains of "writing" failed to cross-pollinate.

---

## Evidence

**Study Design**: Cross-domain transfer studies, within-domain sub-specialization research, chess expertise memory studies
**Key Results**:
- Chess masters' memory advantage disappears with randomly arranged chess pieces (not regular positions) - the advantage is position-specific, not general spatial memory
- IQ does not distinguish chess masters from other players at expert level
- Poetry training does not transfer to story writing within "creative writing"
- Esports players show within-expertise transfer limitations (different game = different expertise)

---

## The Maximal Adaptation Problem

The deeper the expertise, the more finely tuned the cognitive tools become to specific task conditions. **Domain changes are especially damaging to experts** because their performance rests on subtle task affordances that novices simply haven't encoded yet - making novices more adaptable to rule changes.

An expert has developed exquisitely calibrated perceptual-cognitive tools for the exact conditions of their domain. Change those conditions and those tools are not just neutral - they can actively mislead, triggering the wrong patterns where novices have no patterns to trigger at all.

---

## Sub-Domain Specificity

Even within broad domains, transfer is minimal:
- Different surgery types: Experience in one procedure provides limited benefit for different procedures
- Different game types: Chess expertise provides minimal advantage in other strategy games
- Different creative writing modes: Poetry training does not improve story writing

**Implication**: The domain scope for which expertise claims are valid is far narrower than commonly assumed. A "experienced professional" claim should specify the precise domain conditions under which the experience was accumulated.

---

## Perceived Similarity as Bottleneck

Transfer requires not just objective similarity between tasks but **perceived similarity** - experts must recognize that their skills apply. This recognition itself requires meta-cognitive expertise about one's own domain. Experts can fail to transfer not because the skills don't apply but because they don't recognize the isomorphism.

---

## Practical Implications

1. **Hiring**: Domain experience claims should specify task conditions, not just field
2. **Consulting**: Expert consultants from adjacent domains may have less transferable knowledge than assumed
3. **Career planning**: Switching domains within a field may require rebuilding expertise nearly from scratch
4. **AI training**: AI models trained on specific domain distributions face similar transfer limitations
5. **Cross-functional teams**: Domain experts cannot assume their pattern libraries apply in adjacent problems
