## Core Framework

The 2024-2025 scientific literature has decisively moved beyond the "cold cognition vs. hot emotion" dichotomy that dominated 20th-century psychology. Emotions are not noise in the cognitive system - they are part of the signal. The right question is no longer "do emotions help or hurt cognition?" but rather "which cognitive processes are enhanced, and which are impaired, by which emotional states?"

## The Divergent Effects Model

Emotion's effects on cognition are systematically divergent, not uniform:

| Cognitive Process | Emotion Effect | Mechanism |
|------------------|---------------|-----------|
| **Item memory** | ENHANCES | Amygdala-hippocampal synergy for salient items ("flashbulb memory" effect has robust neural substrate) |
| **Relational/contextual memory** | IMPAIRS | You remember the shocking news but not when/where you heard it |
| **Attention (task-irrelevant emotion)** | IMPAIRS | Attentional capture causes performance decrements |
| **Attention (task-relevant emotion)** | ENHANCES | Emotional content increases engagement |
| **Working memory** | IMPAIRS | Emotional information competes with task content for limited resources |

---

## The Amygdala-Hippocampus Model

Two competing models of amygdala-hippocampal interaction are being reconciled:

- **Antagonistic model** (older): Amygdala inhibits hippocampus under high arousal, impairing relational encoding
- **Agonistic model** (newer): Synergistic engagement under moderate arousal enhances both item and contextual memory

**Resolution**: Arousal intensity may determine which model applies:
- Moderate emotional arousal recruits both structures cooperatively
- Extreme arousal triggers the antagonistic suppression pattern

This means the same emotional state that helps you remember something vividly may simultaneously prevent you from remembering the context around it.

---

## Implications

**Design Implications**:
- Designing learning environments, therapeutic contexts, and high-stakes decision environments requires specifying WHICH aspect of cognition matters, not just asking about emotion in general
- Emotional content in teaching can enhance memory for the material itself while impairing integration with related facts
- Stress/arousal in decision contexts may produce confident but context-poor choices

**Counter to Rational Agent Model**:
- The old question "should I trust my gut or my head?" is now scientifically obsolete
- The real question: "what does this specific emotion, at this intensity, in this context, tell me about this specific cognitive task?"
