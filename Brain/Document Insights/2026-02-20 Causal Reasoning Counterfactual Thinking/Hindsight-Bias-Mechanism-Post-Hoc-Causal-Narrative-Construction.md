# Hindsight Bias Is Post-Hoc Causal Narrative Construction - Not Memory Failure

## Core Insight

Hindsight bias ("I knew it all along") is not primarily a memory distortion - it is the product of the brain's normal causal reasoning machinery operating in reverse. The **Causal Model Theory (CMT)** proposes that "creeping determinism" (the retrospective sense of inevitability) is driven by the causal reasoning process itself.

Counterintuitively, **thinking more carefully about causality can *increase* hindsight bias**, not reduce it. When you elaborate the causal chain linking antecedents to the outcome, you increase the perceived inevitability of the outcome - the opposite of what deliberate reasoning should produce.

---

## The Mechanism: Reverse Causal Inference

After learning an outcome, the brain engages normal causal reasoning - but now working backward from a known end-state:

1. **Known outcome** triggers causal search: "Why did this happen?"
2. **Causal reasoning** constructs the best narrative linking prior events to the outcome
3. **Narrative construction succeeds** because causal reasoning is powerful enough to find plausible connections
4. **Narrative feels inevitable** because the causal chain seems to explain the outcome completely
5. **Conclusion**: "Of course this was going to happen" - the sense of predictability is the result of narrative coherence

This is why understanding a story (having a complete causal model) makes it feel predictable in hindsight - the coherent causal model creates the illusion of inevitability.

---

## The Counterfactual Paradox

Normally, counterfactual thinking should weaken hindsight bias (by showing how things could have gone differently). The research reveals the opposite can occur:

**Mechanism of paradoxical strengthening**:
- Constructing a counterfactual ("What if X hadn't happened?") requires building a complete causal model
- Building a complete causal model strengthens the perceived causal link between events
- A stronger causal link makes the actual outcome feel *more* inevitable (because the cause was so powerful, even without it the outcome might have happened)
- Result: Counterfactual elaboration can increase, not decrease, perceived inevitability

**The exception**: Counterfactuals that highlight the *contingency* of outcomes (showing many different outcomes were equally possible) do weaken hindsight bias. The key is whether counterfactual thinking reveals causal complexity or causal necessity.
