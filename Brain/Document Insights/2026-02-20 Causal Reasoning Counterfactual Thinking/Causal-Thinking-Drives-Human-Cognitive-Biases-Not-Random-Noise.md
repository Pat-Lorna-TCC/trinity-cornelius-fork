## Core Framework

The classical framing of cognitive biases treats them as failures of rational reasoning - malfunctions to be corrected. A more nuanced framework emerging from causal cognition research: **most major cognitive biases are not random errors but systematic, predictable products of efficient causal reasoning operating under information constraints**.

Under this framework:
- Biases are not defects but features of a well-calibrated causal reasoning system
- The same mechanism that enables efficient causal attribution also produces characteristic errors in specific conditions
- Understanding the causal mechanism behind each bias reveals when it will appear and how to correct for it

## Four Major Biases Explained by Causal Mechanism

### 1. Hindsight Bias
**Causal mechanism**: Post-hoc causal narrative construction
**Why it occurs**: Normal backward causal inference from known outcome creates the feeling of inevitability
**When it's worst**: When the causal chain is coherent and complete (good causal explanation = strong hindsight)
**Correction**: Focus on the space of outcomes that *could* have occurred given initial conditions, not the outcome that did

### 2. Fundamental Attribution Error (FAE)
**Causal mechanism**: Asymmetric causal attribution under information asymmetry
**Why it occurs**: We lack situational context for others, so our causal model defaults to dispositional causes (the only causes we can observe); we have rich situational context for ourselves
**When it's worst**: High stakes, negative outcomes, unfamiliar people
**Correction**: Actively seek situational information before attributing causes to person-level factors

### 3. Narrative Fallacy
**Causal mechanism**: Confabulated causal stories about random sequences
**Why it occurs**: The brain's causal reasoning system is always "on" - it finds causal patterns even when none exist
**When it's worst**: Retrospective analysis of complex events (markets, elections, historical events)
**Correction**: Pre-specifying prediction frameworks BEFORE outcomes are known; base rate thinking

### 4. Confirmation Bias
**Causal mechanism**: Causal model confirmation-seeking
**Why it occurs**: Efficient causal model updating uses existing causal models as priors; disconfirming evidence is processed as "noise" by default
**Why it's adaptive**: Most existing causal models are approximately correct; updating too readily on each disconfirming piece of evidence would be inefficient
**When it's worst**: Identity-linked beliefs (where the causal model is part of self-concept); high prior confidence

## The Unifying Principle

All four biases share a common structure: **the causal reasoning system performs efficient inference given the information available to it, and produces errors when that information is systematically incomplete or biased**.

This is identical to the principle behind machine learning failures: a model trained on biased data will produce biased outputs. The brain's causal reasoning system, trained on a lifetime of experience from a particular vantage point, produces biased outputs when that vantage point systematically omits important information.

**Implication**: Reducing cognitive biases requires addressing the information asymmetry that drives them, not trying to override the causal reasoning system itself. This aligns with the existing vault finding that debiasing training shows only 19% transfer - because it targets the reasoning system rather than the information environment.

## Graded Causation and Moral Attribution

A 2025 Erkenntnis paper introduces a subtle principle for moral attribution:

**Sufficiency beats necessity in moral responsibility**:
- Agent A who was *sufficient* for an outcome (could have caused it alone) bears more responsibility than...
- Agent B who was *necessary* but not sufficient (could not have caused it alone)

This contradicts intuitive moral accounting but follows from causal logic: the sufficient agent had the most causal power concentrated in their action.

**Why this matters for decision-making**: When assigning blame (to others or yourself), check for sufficiency, not just necessity. Many cases of excessive self-blame involve mistaking "necessary condition" for "sufficient cause."
