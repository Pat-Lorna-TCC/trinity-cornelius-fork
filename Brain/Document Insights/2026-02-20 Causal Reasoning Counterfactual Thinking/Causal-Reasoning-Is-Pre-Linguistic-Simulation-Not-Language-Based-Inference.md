# Human Causal Reasoning Is Pre-Linguistic Simulation - Not Language-Based Inference

## Core Framework

A convergence of developmental psychology and neuroscience evidence supports a clear conclusion: human causal reasoning is fundamentally **simulation-based** (constructing mental models and running forward predictions), not **language-based inference** (deriving causal conclusions from linguistic propositions).

Evidence:
1. Causal reasoning appears before language is complete (24-30 month toddlers)
2. The neural substrate for causal reasoning (hippocampal simulation + logic network) is distinct from language areas
3. Disrupting hippocampal simulation (TMS) impairs causal reasoning; disrupting language areas does not
4. Cross-cultural causal reasoning shows universal underlying mechanisms despite linguistic diversity

This framework has a critical implication: **training AI systems on language alone cannot produce genuine causal reasoning** because causal cognition does not run on language as its substrate.

## The Simulation Architecture

Human causal reasoning uses a specific computational architecture:

1. **Representation**: Causal variables and their relationships are represented as manipulable mental models (not propositions)
2. **Inference**: Running a mental simulation forward from initial conditions (not deriving logical conclusions)
3. **Counterfactual**: Modifying the mental model (changing a causal variable) and re-running the simulation
4. **Generalization**: Abstracting the causal structure of a simulation into a schema applicable to new domains

This architecture explains capabilities that language-based inference cannot:
- Abstract causal generalization to novel objects (toddler experiments)
- Discovering unobserved causal variables ("there must be something else causing this")
- Reasoning about causal interventions ("what would happen if I did X?")

## The "Learning From Doing" Foundation

The earliest forms of causal learning in infants (before 24 months) are grounded in **sensorimotor interaction** - learning that actions cause effects. This action-effect learning creates the template for all subsequent causal cognition:

- Perception of causal structure begins with **own actions** affecting the world
- This provides the intuitive understanding of **intervention** (doing something to cause an effect)
- Abstract causal reasoning later builds on this foundation

Pearl's "do-calculus" (Rung 2 reasoning) maps directly onto this developmental origin: the "do(X)" operator formalizes what infants learn through direct action. The brain's causal reasoning capacity is built around the concept of action-caused-effect.

**AI implication**: Embodied AI systems that learn through interaction (reinforcement learning, robotics) may develop more genuine causal representations than language models that observe text about actions and effects.

## Cross-Cultural Universality

Despite significant cross-cultural variation in *which types* of causal explanations are preferred (teleological vs. mechanistic, dispositional vs. situational), the **underlying causal reasoning mechanisms** appear universal:
- All cultures distinguish causes from correlations
- All cultures support counterfactual reasoning
- All cultures' children develop causal inference at similar ages

The variation is in the *default deployment* of causal reasoning (which rungs are habitually used, which explanations are preferred) - not in the underlying simulation capacity. This universality supports the pre-linguistic, simulation-based nature of the capacity: it precedes cultural/linguistic shaping.

## Buddhist Resonance - Direct Perception Before Conceptualization

Buddhist epistemology distinguishes between:
- **Direct perception** (pratyaksha): Immediate, non-conceptual knowing
- **Inference** (anumana): Conceptual, language-mediated knowing

The finding that causal reasoning is simulation-based (pre-linguistic) rather than language-based resonates with Buddhist insight that direct cognitive capacities precede and ground the conceptual overlay that language creates. The toddler's causal simulation capacity is a form of direct cognitive engagement with causal structure - it works *before* and *beneath* language.

Buddhist practitioners describe meditative direct perception as accessing something beneath conceptual thought. This may correspond to the same simulation-based cognitive architecture that underlies causal reasoning.
