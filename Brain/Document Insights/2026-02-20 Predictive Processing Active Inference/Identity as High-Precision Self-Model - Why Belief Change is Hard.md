## Core Framework

The FEP provides the most mechanistically precise account of why identity and belief change is so difficult:

**Identity = a cluster of very high-precision self-model predictions**

These are not just beliefs about the world - they are beliefs about what kind of predictor "I" am. When someone's identity includes "I am a conservative" or "I am an entrepreneur" or "I am a person who doesn't use AI," these self-descriptors are **high-precision prior predictions** about how they will interpret information and how they will act.

To change these beliefs requires generating prediction errors that overcome their precision weight. The higher the precision (the more central to identity), the larger the prediction error must be to trigger updating.

## The Formal Account

From the 2025 Bayesian Brain Theory review:
- Hierarchical beliefs encoded across cortical layers
- Belief updating via synaptic plasticity (spike-timing dependent)
- Higher-level beliefs (identity) = more deeply embedded in hierarchy = require repeated updating across many layers to change
- Monoaminergic precision signals (dopamine) enforce high precision on high-level beliefs

**Why deep beliefs change slowly:**
The generative model is hierarchical. Surface-level beliefs (opinions) sit at lower levels of the hierarchy - they update relatively quickly. Identity-level beliefs (core values, self-concept) sit at the top of the hierarchy. Prediction errors from lower levels must propagate up through many layers, each dampened by precision weights, before reaching the top. By the time they arrive, the error signal is often too weak to trigger updating.
