## Core Framework

The Active Intersubjective Inference (AISI) framework proposes that the **self is not a fixed entity but an active inference process** - specifically, the brain's continuous inference about how others perceive and respond to us. The self is constituted through second-order self-models extracted from social interactions.

In normal functioning:
- Social interactions provide ongoing evidence about one's social standing, safety, worthiness
- The self-model updates continuously from this evidence
- The self is literally a running inference engine, not a stable structure

In MDD and PTSD after trauma:
- Traumatic experiences introduce corrupted data into the inference process
- Prior beliefs about safety, self-worth, and predictability are distorted by high-precision trauma-encoded priors
- These high-precision priors dominate over subsequent updating evidence
- The corrupted self-model becomes self-perpetuating: it filters subsequent social interactions to confirm its predictions

## The Synapse as Battleground

The *Entropy* paper frames depression specifically as a **synaptopathy through active inference**:
- Depression arises from alterations in excitatory-inhibitory (E/I) balance
- E/I imbalance corrupts precision-weighting at the synaptic level
- Creates a vicious cycle: world appears consistently worse than predicted → negative prediction errors dominate → model locks in negative priors → world continues to appear worse than predicted

From active inference perspective, depression is **not a random failure** but an emergent property of a system that has learned a correct model of an adverse environment and is now over-generalizing it.

## Why the Therapeutic Relationship IS the Therapy

This framework provides the deepest computational account of why therapeutic alliance predicts treatment outcomes better than technique adherence:

**The relationship provides evidence inconsistent with the patient's entrenched prior model.**

A therapist who is consistently warm despite the patient's expectation of hostility; who does not judge despite the patient's expectation of shame; who maintains safety despite the patient's expectation of threat - this is not "nice support," it is **persistent disconfirming evidence** that forces the self-model's prior beliefs to update.

The techniques (thought records, exposure exercises) provide episodic evidence. The relationship provides continuous, high-frequency evidence. Over months, the sheer weight of evidence eventually exceeds the precision of the trauma-encoded priors.
