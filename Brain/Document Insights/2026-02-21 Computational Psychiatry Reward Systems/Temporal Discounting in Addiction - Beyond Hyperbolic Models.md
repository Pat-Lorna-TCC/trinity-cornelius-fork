## Core Findings

**Beyond Simple Hyperbolic Discounting**

Standard addiction models use hyperbolic discounting (high discount rate = more impulsive). The 2024-2025 literature identifies this is insufficient:

1. **The cocaine study (2024)**: A novel model measuring **delay discounting AND choice sharpness** explains impulsive choices in cocaine use disorder better than hyperbolic models alone. Choice sharpness (the steepness of the preference function) is a separable parameter.

2. **The Jellinek Curve (2025)**: All phases of addiction (initiation, escalation, vicious cycles, recovery) are modeled using generalized hyperbolic discount functions deformed by **Weber-Fechner and Stevens' Power laws** (laws of subjective time perception). This explains why:
   - Relapse occurs even after long abstinence: the subjective memory of drug value is encoded more precisely than the memory of abstinence duration
   - Escalation follows predictable mathematical curves
   - Recovery has characteristic relapse windows

3. **Transdiagnostic (N=731)**: Temporal discounting links to **two distinct symptom dimensions**:
   - Anxious-depression dimension (via discount rate)
   - Inattention-impulsivity-overactivity dimension (via magnitude sensitivity)
   These are distinct mechanisms, not the same phenomenon.

## The Weber-Fechner Time Distortion Insight

Weber-Fechner law: subjective perception is proportional to the logarithm of stimulus intensity. Applied to time:
- Future rewards feel further away than they are (logarithmic time compression)
- The subjective distance to a reward tomorrow is disproportionately large
- This creates apparent impulsivity even with objectively normal discount rates

In addiction, this distortion is amplified:
- Drug rewards are immediate (no time distortion penalty)
- Natural rewards require sustained effort over time (maximum distortion penalty)
- The mathematical asymmetry, even without unusually high discount rates, makes drug use appear computationally rational
