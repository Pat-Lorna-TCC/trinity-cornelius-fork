## The Gap

Computational psychiatry has succeeded in building a unifying theoretical framework (active inference, precision weighting) but faces a critical tension: the same mathematical structure (free energy minimization) applies to all disorders, but clinical value requires **disorder-specific parameter maps and individual-difference models**.

Current specific gaps:
1. **Lab vs. real-world validity**: Most computational psychiatry uses brief lab tasks; behavior in controlled settings may not reflect non-stationary, context-dependent patterns of real psychiatric illness
2. **Individual differences**: Computational parameters vary enormously between individuals with the same diagnosis - this is both the promise and the challenge
3. **Biomarker validation gap**: No current computational or neural biomarker meets APA clinical reliability standard (80% sensitivity/specificity/accuracy simultaneously)
4. **Head-to-head model comparisons**: Too many competing computational frameworks without systematic cross-validation against each other on the same datasets
5. **Missing domain coverage**: PTSD specifically, ASD through precision weighting lens, bipolar computational models, schizophrenia negative symptoms

---

## Why This Matters

The field is in a peculiar position: strong theoretical unification (active inference explains everything in principle) combined with weak clinical translation (no single biomarker is reliable enough for clinical use). The gap is between the mathematical elegance of the unified model and the practical requirements of clinical diagnostics.

The ML-psychiatric population problem illustrates this: studies mixing psychiatric and non-psychiatric populations produce inflated predictions because formal diagnosis overshadows subtle disorder-specific computational signatures. Clinical utility requires precision, not just statistical signal.

---

## What We Know (Related Research)

- Unified theory: Active inference provides coherent mechanistic accounts across all disorders
- Clinical translation: Computational phenotyping, nEV biomarkers, closed-loop neuromodulation entering clinical testing
- Missing: Head-to-head comparisons showing which model best predicts treatment response for which specific disorder and patient subtype

---

## Practical Implications for the Knowledge Base

This gap matters for applying computational psychiatry insights:
- The theoretical frameworks in this session's notes are genuinely valuable as conceptual models
- Clinical application requires disorder-specific validation that is still underway
- The frameworks should inform understanding of mechanisms, not yet clinical decision-making
