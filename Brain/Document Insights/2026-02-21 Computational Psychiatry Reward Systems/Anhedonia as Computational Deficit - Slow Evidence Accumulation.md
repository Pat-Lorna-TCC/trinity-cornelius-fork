## Core Insight

Anhedonia - the signature symptom of depression - is NOT "low pleasure" or a broken hedonic system. It is a specific computational deficit: **slower evidence accumulation and elevated decision thresholds for reward-seeking actions**. The anhedonic brain demands more evidence before committing to effort, processes reward signals more slowly, and shows blunted reward prediction errors - making it appear unmotivated when it is in fact running a miscalibrated inference engine.

---

## Evidence

**Study 1 - Young People (2025)**:
- **Sample Size**: N=155 (ages 16-25)
- **Key Results**: As consummatory anhedonia increased, reward learning accuracy decreased; anhedonia correlates with poorer effort modulation as a function of reward magnitude - not simply with low hedonic experience
- **Design**: Computational modeling of reward learning task

**Study 2 - Adolescent MDD HDDM (2024)**:
- **Sample Size**: 70 MDD patients vs 54 controls
- **Key Results**: Hierarchical drift diffusion modeling revealed slower drift rates (evidence accumulation speed) in MDD - the brain takes longer to accumulate evidence that an action is worth pursuing
- **Method**: Hierarchical Drift Diffusion Modeling (HDDM)

**Study 3 - Remitted Depression (2025)**:
- **Key Results**: Even in remission, individuals showed reduced hard-task choices overall, but retained heightened sensitivity to high-value, high-probability rewards - motivational deficits persist after depressive episodes resolve

**Neural Biomarker**:
- Larger reward prediction error (RPE) signals in the ventral striatum predicted **reduced anhedonia 6 months later** - the brain's remaining RPE capacity is a prognostic biomarker

---

## Why This Matters

The traditional framing ("I don't enjoy things") is phenomenologically accurate but computationally misleading. The real deficit is in the *inference machinery* linking evidence about reward to decisions about effort. This has direct treatment implications:

- **Dopaminergic augmentation** (bupropion, stimulants) specifically targets vigor and evidence accumulation speed
- **SSRIs** work better for rumination and punishment sensitivity, not motivational anhedonia
- High-value, high-probability rewards can still engage effort even in anhedonic individuals - targeting these first in therapy may rebuild the reward inference system
