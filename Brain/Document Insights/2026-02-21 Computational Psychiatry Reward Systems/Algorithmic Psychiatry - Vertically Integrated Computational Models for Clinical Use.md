## Core Framework

"Algorithmic psychiatry" is proposed as a new clinical paradigm using computation as a **bridging language across biological scales** - from molecular biology through neural circuits to cognition and behavior. The key argument: interventions at any single biological level (e.g., pharmacology at receptor level) have effects that propagate across all levels, and current psychiatry lacks the tools to model these cross-scale effects.

The four components:
1. **Hierarchical predictive processing** as the unifying computational framework
2. **Computational phenotyping** for individualized patient classification
3. **Computationally-grounded therapies** (e.g., "SlowMo" for delusional reasoning)
4. **Spatially-targeted intervention** (focused ultrasound enabling region-specific pharmacology)

## The Unifying Mechanism (Schizophrenia as Test Case)

In schizophrenia, hierarchical predictive processing explains both hallucinations AND delusions simultaneously through a single mechanism:
- **Reduced precision in sensory predictions** (bottom-up noise)
- **Heightened precision in cognitive beliefs** (top-down overconfidence)
- Combined effect: the brain generates vivid prior predictions (hallucinations) and resists revising them with incoming sensory evidence (delusions)

This framework provides the first mechanistic account that unifies positive symptoms (hallucinations, delusions) with negative symptoms (avolition, anhedonia) under a single computational model.

## Clinical Translation Proof Points

**SlowMo therapy**: Technology-assisted therapy targeting fast vs. slow thinking patterns in delusional reasoning. Shows clinical efficacy as a proof of concept that computationally-grounded therapies work in practice.

**Focused ultrasound**: Enables spatially-targeted pharmacological interventions at specific brain regions (beyond systemic drug delivery), closing the loop between computational phenotyping and targeted treatment.

**Computational phenotyping**: Uses task-based measures to identify individual patient subtypes that predict treatment response - making "precision psychiatry" operationally real.
