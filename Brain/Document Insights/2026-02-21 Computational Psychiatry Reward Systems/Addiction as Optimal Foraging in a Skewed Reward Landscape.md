## Core Framework

The standard "hijacked reward system" model of addiction - drugs take over dopamine circuits and override rational choice - is computationally incomplete. A more accurate framework: **the addicted brain is rationally exploiting the highest short-term value source available in its subjective reward landscape**.

The key insight: addiction is not only about the drug's high absolute reward value. It is about the **relative value** of drugs compared to all other available rewards. In the addicted person's environment, social connection, achievement, and sensory pleasures have often been devalued (through tolerance, social withdrawal, or genuine environmental poverty). The drug's relative value is therefore even higher than its absolute value suggests.

Treatment implications:
- Blocking the drug's rewarding effects (naltrexone, partial agonists) is necessary but insufficient
- Increasing the absolute value of competing rewards is equally critical
- Environmental change is often required alongside pharmacological intervention

## Context and Acceptance

**Field Acceptance**: Emerging - supported by computational foraging models; Heinz et al. (2024) and Robbins et al. (2024) both argue habitual behavior in addiction is dimensional (not categorically distinct from goal-directed behavior) and is modifiable by cognition
**Mathematical Backing**: Jellinek curve paper models all phases (initiation, escalation, vicious cycles, recovery) using generalized hyperbolic discount functions deformed by Weber-Fechner and Stevens' Power laws - explaining why relapse occurs even after long abstinence periods when the subjective "memory" of drug value remains encoded

## Why the Addict's Brain is Not Broken

| Standard Model | Foraging Model |
|---------------|----------------|
| Drug hijacks reward circuits | Drug wins a comparative value competition |
| Rational choice is overridden | Rational choice is operating in distorted landscape |
| Fix: block drug effects | Fix: raise value of alternatives AND manage drug salience |
| Brain is malfunctioning | Brain is functioning correctly on wrong inputs |
