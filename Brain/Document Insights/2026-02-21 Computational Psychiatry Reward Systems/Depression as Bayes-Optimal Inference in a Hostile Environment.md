## Core Framework

Using active inference and Bayesian decision theory, researchers have formalized a counterintuitive but mathematically rigorous claim: **depression can be a Bayes-optimal response** to an accurately perceived negative environment. An agent with correct prior beliefs that its social environment is hostile, unrewarding, and unpredictable will rationally reduce effort, withdraw, and adopt a passive cognitive style. This is not pathology - it is correct inference.

Depression becomes maladaptive only in two conditions:
1. The prior beliefs persist after the environment actually improves (prior rigidity)
2. The system cannot update priors based on new evidence (precision weighting failure)

## Radical Treatment Implication

The paper states directly: "Reversing maladaptive depression amounts to repairing the environment, such that the prior beliefs of a phenotype are fit for purpose."

This reframes the purpose of antidepressants: they likely work not by fixing a broken serotonin system but by **lowering the evidence threshold for prior revision** - making the brain more responsive to new environmental evidence. This explains why SSRIs work best when combined with therapy (which provides the new evidence to update on), and why they often fail when used in isolation in unchanged negative environments.

## How the Brain Gets Stuck

The transition from adaptive to maladaptive depression follows a specific computational failure sequence:
1. Agent accurately perceives hostile environment - rational withdrawal begins
2. Environment improves, but prior model does not update (precision weighting rigidity)
3. The brain continues to generate predictions consistent with the old model
4. New positive experiences are discounted as prediction errors (too surprising to be trusted)
5. Loop reinforces itself - the depressive attractor becomes stable

This is structurally identical to the Uncertainty-Dopamine-Belief Loop - the brain stays in its known (if painful) model rather than risking the uncertainty of updating.
