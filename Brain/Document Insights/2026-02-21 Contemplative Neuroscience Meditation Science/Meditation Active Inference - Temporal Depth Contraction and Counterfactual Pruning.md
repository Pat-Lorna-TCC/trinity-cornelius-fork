## Core Finding

Meditation produces two specific computational changes within the Active Inference (Free Energy Principle) framework that explain the characteristic phenomenology of present-moment experience:

1. **Temporal depth contraction**: Meditation shortens the brain's temporal predictive window - the brain generates predictions that extend less far into past and future
2. **Counterfactual pruning**: Meditation reduces the number of alternative scenarios (policies) the brain simulates, explaining why meditators report simplified, vivid, present experience

---

## Temporal Depth Contraction - Explained

The Bayesian brain normally generates predictions that span:
- Near future (seconds): motor preparation, word completion, etc.
- Medium future (minutes to hours): social interactions, task plans
- Far future (days to lifetime): goals, aspirations, feared outcomes
- Past (memories): context, identity narrative, learned associations

**Normal waking consciousness** uses deep temporal models - the brain is simultaneously computing consequences at multiple timescales, giving experience a narrative, extended quality ("I am the same person who was here yesterday and will be here tomorrow").

**Meditation progressively contracts the temporal depth**:
- FA: Some reduction in far-future modeling; attention anchored to present breath
- OM: Further reduction; monitoring present moment without anticipation
- Non-dual / advanced: Near-complete collapse to the immediate present
- Cessation: Possibly a zero-depth state where the generative model itself temporarily halts

**This explains why meditators report** "everything seems immediate, direct, and vivid" - because the brain's predictive machinery is no longer extending itself through time, leaving only the present sensory reality.

---

## Counterfactual Pruning - Explained

Active inference normally involves **policy simulation**: the brain continuously simulates multiple possible courses of action (policies) and their likely outcomes before selecting behavior.

**This policy simulation is computationally expensive and phenomenologically present** as:
- Mental rehearsal (running through what to say)
- Worry (simulating negative futures)
- Planning (evaluating options)
- Rumination (simulating past alternatives)

**Meditation reduces policy simulation**:
- Fewer active policy branches
- Reduced counterfactual depth (less "what if" computation)
- This reduces the cognitive "noise" of competing simulated futures
- Leaving experience more streamlined, vivid, and simplified

**The meditator's reported "clarity" and "simplicity"** is the subjective experience of a brain with fewer active counterfactual threads running.

## The Unified Account

Combined, temporal depth contraction + counterfactual pruning explains:

| Buddhist Experience | Computational Account |
|--------------------|----------------------|
| "Here and Now" | Temporal depth contracted to immediate present |
| "No self" | Self-model's temporal extent (past narrative + future planning) = reduced |
| "Vivid direct experience" | Fewer competing simulations; primary sensory precision increased |
| "Bliss of non-doing" | Relief from continuous policy simulation |
| "Equanimity" | Reduced precision on future-negative simulations |
| "Cessation" | Generative model temporarily halted; no temporal depth = no experience |

## The Minimal Theory of Consciousness

The Friston, Seth, Hohwy (2025) paper builds a full minimal theory of consciousness from active inference that explains:
- Normal waking consciousness (deep temporal models, active policy simulation)
- Altered states from meditation (progressive reduction of both)
- Minimal states near the boundary of consciousness (cessation approach)
- Psychedelic states (altered precision weighting, different from meditation's reduction)

The "minimal" consciousness is defined as the simplest generative model that can maintain viability - and meditation progressively approaches this minimum.

## Implications

**For practice**: This model predicts that practices which more directly contract temporal depth should produce stronger present-moment phenomenology. Practices like breath-focused FA anchor attention in something that is inherently brief (a single breath) - an effective temporal depth contractor.

**For psychopathology**: Depression and anxiety involve the opposite - excessive temporal depth (ruminating on past, worrying about future). The active inference account explains WHY meditation helps: it is the computational inverse of the rumination pattern.

**For AI**: The model suggests that more capable cognitive systems have deeper temporal models and more policy branches. Meditation's value for humans may be in periodic model-simplification that reduces computational overhead and noise.
