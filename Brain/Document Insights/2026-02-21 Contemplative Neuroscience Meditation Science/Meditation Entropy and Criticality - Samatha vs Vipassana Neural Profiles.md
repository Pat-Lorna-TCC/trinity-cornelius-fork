# Meditation Entropy and Criticality - Samatha vs Vipassana Neural Profiles

## Core Finding

Both Samatha (focused attention/calming) and Vipassana (open monitoring/insight) meditation **increase neural signal complexity** compared to resting state - but through different mechanisms that position the brain differently relative to the critical point (edge of chaos).

---

## The Complexity Profiles

**MEG study of expert Buddhist monks** (Pascarella, Thölke et al., 2025):

**Multiple complexity metrics measured**:
- Long-range temporal correlations (LRTC)
- Deviation from criticality coefficient (DCC)
- Lempel-Ziv complexity (LZc)
- Higuchi fractal dimension (HFD)
- Spectral entropy

**Key finding**:

| Metric | Samatha (FA) | Vipassana (OM) | Direction |
|--------|-------------|----------------|-----------|
| Overall complexity | Increased vs rest | Increased vs rest | Both higher than rest |
| DCC (deviation from criticality) | Less change | DECREASED (closer to criticality) | Vipassana = more critical |
| Criticality position | Ordered (below critical point) | Closer to edge of chaos | Different regions of complexity landscape |

---

## What This Means for Practice Theory

**Samatha increases complexity while remaining in an ordered regime**:
- The brain becomes more complex but maintains structure (lower DCC than Vipassana)
- Ordered complexity: coherent, stable attention sustained
- The concentration and stability of focus corresponds to an ordered (but not simple) neural regime

**Vipassana pushes toward criticality (edge of chaos)**:
- The brain moves toward the phase transition between order and chaos
- Critical dynamics: maximal information processing capacity, highest sensitivity to inputs
- Open, non-reactive awareness corresponds to the most flexible (most critical) neural regime
- This supports the insight function: at the critical point, the system is maximally sensitive to previously unnoticed patterns
