# Guilt vs Shame Neural Dissociation - Anterior Mid-Cingulate Cortex Tracks Harm Severity

## Core Insight

Guilt and shame are neurobiologically distinct moral emotions with different triggers, neural signatures, and behavioral consequences. A 2025 fMRI study combining computational modeling with interpersonal game design provides the clearest dissociation to date:

- **Guilt** responds primarily to **severity of harm caused** (inadequate valuation of others) → activates **anterior mid-cingulate cortex (aMCC)**, which detects conflict between social norms and actual behavior
- **Shame** responds primarily to **responsibility for harm** (threat of social devaluation) → activates **parahippocampal gyrus (PHG)** and ACC

Both emotions activate the **anterior insula (aINS)**, which processes multiple negative moral emotions including guilt, shame, and disgust.

---

## Evidence

**Study Design** (eLife, 2025):
- Combined fMRI with computational modeling
- Novel interpersonal game to independently vary harm severity and responsibility attribution
- Dissociated guilt-driven vs. shame-driven altruistic behavior

**Key Neural Findings**:
- **aMCC** (anterior mid-cingulate cortex): consistently activated during guilt - conflict detection between social norms and actual behavior
- **aINS** (anterior insula): processes multiple negative moral emotions (guilt, shame, disgust) - general negative moral affect processor
- **PHG** (parahippocampal gyrus): shame-specific
- **ACC** (anterior cingulate cortex): shame
- **mPFC** (medial prefrontal cortex): overlapping activity for both guilt and shame

**Computational Insight**: Study provides high-quality empirical support for **functionalist theory of emotions** - emotions are functional states that track specific environmental and social features, not just undifferentiated affect

## The Guilt/Shame Distinction and Behavior

| | Guilt | Shame |
|--|-------|-------|
| Trigger | Harm severity (I caused this) | Social devaluation threat (others know) |
| Self-focus | Behavior-focused ("I did bad") | Identity-focused ("I am bad") |
| Social consequence | Reparative motivation | Avoidance/withdrawal motivation |
| Neural signature | aMCC (norm violation detection) | PHG + ACC |
| Altruistic outcome | Greater reparative prosocial behavior | Withdrawal from social engagement |

This distinction matters clinically: moral injury treatment must address guilt and shame separately, as they have different mechanisms and require different interventions (guilt → repair/restitution; shame → self-compassion/identity work).
