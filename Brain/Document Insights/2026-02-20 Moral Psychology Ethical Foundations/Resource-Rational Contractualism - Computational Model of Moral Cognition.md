## Core Framework

Fiery Cushman's Resource-Rational Contractualism proposes a "triple theory" of moral cognition integrating:
1. **Rule-based contractualism** (what rules would rational agents agree to follow?)
2. **Resource constraints** (given limited computational capacity, which rules are tractable?)
3. **Virtual bargaining** (moral judgment as simulated negotiation between stakeholders)

This framework replaces the emotion/reason dichotomy with a more precise question: given that moral agents have bounded rationality, what *computational shortcuts* best approximate optimal moral reasoning?

**Model-Free vs. Model-Based Moral Learning** (empirically dissociated):
- **Model-free**: Tracks which behaviors reliably produce good/bad outcomes through reinforcement (habit-based ethics, fast)
- **Model-based**: Constructs causal models of the world to simulate consequences (deliberative ethics, slow but accurate)

## Context & Acceptance

**Field Acceptance**: Emerging - represents the leading edge of computational moral psychology
**Supporting Evidence**: Multiple papers from Cushman Lab (Harvard), Tenenbaum (MIT), Gershman (Harvard) published or in press in top journals 2024-2025
**Key Empirical Findings**:
- Moral judgment sensitive to bargaining power: those with more leverage held to higher moral standards (Journal of Experimental Psychology: General, 2025)
- Model-free and model-based contributions to moral learning empirically dissociated (CogSci, 2025)
- Conditions under which moral rules are "over-ruled" explained by virtual bargaining (Cognition, 2025)

## Why This Matters: The Field is Maturing

The shift from intuition-based to computational accounts of morality is analogous to the shift from descriptive to mechanistic accounts of any scientific domain. This enables:
- **Testable predictions** from first principles rather than post-hoc description
- **Individual differences** predicted from computational parameters (e.g., model-free vs. model-based ratio)
- **Development** explained as shifting parameter weights over the lifespan
- **Clinical applications**: moral injury, antisocial behavior, addiction may reflect specific computational deficits
