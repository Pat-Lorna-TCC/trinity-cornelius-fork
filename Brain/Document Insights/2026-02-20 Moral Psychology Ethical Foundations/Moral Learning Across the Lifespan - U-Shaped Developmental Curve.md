# Moral Learning Across the Lifespan - U-Shaped Developmental Curve for Intention vs Outcome

**Source**: Lockwood, P., Van den Bos, W., & Dreher, J.C. "Moral Learning and Decision-Making Across the Lifespan" - Annual Review of Psychology, Vol. 76, pp. 475-500 - January 2025

## Core Insight

Sensitivity to **intention vs. outcome** in moral judgment follows a **U-shaped developmental curve**: it is most similar (least differentiated) in the earliest and oldest years of life, with maximum differentiation occurring in middle age. This is a counterintuitive finding: we might expect moral sophistication to increase monotonically with age, but instead it follows a non-linear developmental trajectory.

The key mechanisms underlying moral development are: shifts in **theory of mind** (ability to model others' intentions) and shifts in **model-free vs. model-based moral learning ratios** across the lifespan.

---

## Evidence

**Study Design**: Annual Review of Psychology synthesis across lifespan neuroscience and moral development literature
**Key Findings**:
1. Intention-outcome sensitivity U-curve: most similar in early childhood + oldest age, most differentiated in middle adulthood
2. Theory of mind development underlies shifting moral judgment across age
3. Model-free/model-based learning ratios shift across lifespan (model-based peaks in young adulthood, declines with age)
4. Computational approach provides mechanistic accounts and generates testable hypotheses

**What This Explains**:
- Young children judge by outcome (hurt/didn't hurt), not intention
- Adolescents and young adults develop full intention-based moral judgment
- Older adults show some return to outcome-based weighting (possibly due to reduced model-based learning capacity or theory of mind decline)

---

## Why This Is Significant

The finding challenges linear models of moral development (e.g., Kohlberg's stage theory implies continuous progression). Instead:
- Moral sophistication is not simply accumulated - it peaks and then partially declines
- The oldest adults may share some moral processing characteristics with very young children
- Interventions that work for one age group may not transfer to others

**Cognitive Mechanism**: Model-based moral learning (deliberative, consequence-simulating) peaks when general cognitive capacity is highest, then declines as cognitive resources are reduced in older age - creating the U-shape in intention sensitivity.
