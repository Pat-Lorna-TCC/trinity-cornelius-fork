# Harm Perception as Universal Moral Anchor - Dyadic Morality Theory

**Source**: Gray, K. & Pratt, S. "Morality in Our Mind and Across Cultures and Politics" - Annual Review of Psychology, Vol. 76, pp. 663-691 - January 2025; Gray, K. "Outraged: Why We Fight About Morality and Politics" (Pantheon, 2025)

## Core Framework

Kurt Gray's Theory of Dyadic Morality (TDM) argues that ALL moral cognition - across liberals, conservatives, and diverse cultures - is anchored in a single harm-based template: an intentional agent causing damage to a vulnerable patient (A→P). Two moral universals emerge:

1. **What is harmful seems wrong** (harm → immorality)
2. **What is wrong seems harmful** (immorality → harm, via "dyadic completion")

The second is the counterintuitive finding: when something is perceived as *morally wrong*, the mind automatically generates a victim - even if no obvious harm exists. Purity violations (e.g., consensual but taboo acts) are condemned because people *imagine harm*, not because harm-independent sacred values are triggered.

## Context & Acceptance

**Field Acceptance**: Emerging - stands in productive tension with MFT's pluralism
**Supporting Evidence**: Cross-cultural empirical research on dyadic completion across diverse populations; Annual Review of Psychology Vol. 76 synthesis
**Alternative Theory**: Moral Foundations Theory (Haidt) - posits that conservatives genuinely respond to harm-independent foundations (purity, authority, loyalty); Gray argues even these ultimately track perceived harm

---

## The Challenge to Moral Foundations Theory

Gray's framework directly challenges MFT's political psychology applications:
- MFT claims conservatives have "additional" moral foundations beyond harm (purity, authority, loyalty)
- Gray counters: these foundations are condemned because people *perceive or imagine harm* flowing from their violation
- If correct, moral disagreements across cultures and politics may be more tractable: the disagreement is about **what counts as harm**, not about fundamentally different moral worlds

**The Practical Implication**: Political bridging should focus on competing harm narratives, not on translating between incommensurable foundations. Both sides believe in harm prevention - they just have different models of what constitutes harm.
