## Core Insight

High moral reasoning is associated with **increased activity in the brain's reward system** (dopamine circuits) - suggesting that higher-level moral development is driven by intrinsically motivated prosocial behavior, not merely by fear of punishment or social enforcement. This is a paradigm shift from enforcement-based models: morality is experienced as rewarding, not just obligatory.

Critically: dopamine dysregulation in active addiction correlates with maladaptive *deontological rigidity* (rigid rule-following), while long-term recovery involves more *flexible moral reasoning strategies*. This inverts the common assumption that addiction produces moral relativism - instead, it produces moral inflexibility.

## Evidence

**Addiction and Moral Judgment Study** (Journal of Drug Issues, 2025):
- Active addiction: dopamine dysregulation → maladaptive deontological rigidity
- Long-term recovery: dopamine normalization → more flexible, context-sensitive moral reasoning
- Finding inverts common narrative that addiction destroys moral compass - instead, it distorts and rigidifies it

**Lifespan Moral Learning Review** (Annual Review of Psychology, 2025 - Lockwood, Van den Bos, Dreher):
- Model-free learning (reinforcement-based habit ethics) and model-based learning (consequence simulation) shift across development
- Sensitivity to intention vs. outcome peaks in early childhood and oldest age (U-shaped developmental curve)
- Dopamine modulates value-based and reinforcement learning underlying moral choices

**Reward System Activation with High Moral Reasoning**:
- Prosocial motivation (not punishment fear) activates reward circuits
- Suggests moral virtue is intrinsically rewarding when properly developed
