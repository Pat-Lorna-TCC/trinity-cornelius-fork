## Core Framework

Philip Tetlock's sacred values framework explains why certain moral commitments function as **absolutes that resist rational tradeoffs**. Sacred values are characterized by:
1. Categorical rather than cost-benefit reasoning
2. Backfire effect: monetary offers for sacred value tradeoffs produce *more* resistance, not less
3. Strong emotional response to "taboo tradeoffs" (combining sacred values with secular/monetary considerations)
4. Cross-cultural robustness: sacred value protection appears in all studied cultures

**Neural signature of sacred value violations**: Outright violations produce increased BOLD signals in the **left anterior temporal lobe and bilateral amygdalae**; right amygdala activity correlates with moral disgust ratings in taboo tradeoff conditions.

## The Backfire Effect Mechanism

When offered money to consider violating a sacred value:
1. The offer is perceived as an insult (monetizing the priceless)
2. Resistance *increases* (the offer signals the other party doesn't understand what's at stake)
3. Emotional response escalates (moral outrage, cleansing behavior)

This explains why purely economic solutions fail in:
- Political negotiations involving territorial/religious sacred spaces
- Organizational attempts to compensate for moral injury through salary increases
- Any context where what is violated is an identity-constituting commitment
