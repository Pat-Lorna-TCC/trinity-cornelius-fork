# MFT Predicts Attitude Positions Better Than Moral Convictions - Foundations vs Conventions

**Source**: Teas, P.E., Hanson, B.E., Leal, A., Novak, L.M., & Skitka, L.J. "Values in Context: The (Dis)connections Between Moral Foundations and Moral Conviction" - Personality and Social Psychology Bulletin - 2025
**Document Type**: Multi-study empirical paper (5 studies, N=3,327)
**Extracted By**: AI (document-insight-extractor agent)
**Extraction Date**: 2026-02-20
**Session**: 2026-02-20 Moral Psychology Ethical Foundations

## Core Insight

Across five studies (N=3,327), Moral Foundations Theory better predicts what **attitude positions** people hold than whether they hold those attitudes as genuine **moral convictions** (as opposed to merely strong preferences or conventional values). Some MFT foundations may reflect conventional social values rather than truly moral commitments with the categorical, sacred quality that moral convictions possess.

This is a significant empirical challenge to MFT: if a "moral foundation" doesn't reliably predict whether someone treats an issue as *morally* important (vs. just politically preferred), the foundation may be measuring something other than morality.

---

## Evidence

**Study Design**: Five studies, N=3,327
**Key Finding**: MFT foundation scores predict *which* positions people take (liberal vs. conservative) better than they predict *whether* people treat those positions as moral absolutes (moral conviction strength)

**The Moral Conviction Construct** (Skitka's research program):
- Moral convictions are attitudes held as moral absolutes - not merely strong preferences
- They resist compromise, activate deontological reasoning, and produce outrage at violation
- They are categorically different from strong conventional preferences

**The Implication**: A high purity/sanctity score might predict that someone opposes certain behaviors for *social conformity* reasons (conventional value) without necessarily experiencing that opposition as a moral conviction with the categorical resistance and outrage potential of a sacred value.

---

## Why This Is an Important Nuance

Not all strong political preferences are moral convictions. The distinction matters for:
- Predicting who will compromise (conventional values can be traded; moral convictions cannot)
- Understanding when outrage responses will be automatic vs. calculated
- Designing intervention programs (moral reframing works differently for conventional preferences vs. genuine moral convictions)
- Interpreting cross-cultural MFQ-2 results (Ghana finding: some apparent foundations may be social conventions, not universal moral architecture)
