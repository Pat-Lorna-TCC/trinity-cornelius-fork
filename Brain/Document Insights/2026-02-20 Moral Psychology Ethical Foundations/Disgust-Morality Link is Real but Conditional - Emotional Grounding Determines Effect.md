# Disgust-Morality Link is Real but Conditional - Emotional Grounding Determines Effect

**Source**: Landy, J. "What Does Disgust Have to Do With Moral Judgment?" - WIREs Cognitive Science - 2025; Salvo, G., Ottaviani, C., & Mancini, F. "Disgust and moral judgment: Toward a neurocognitive framework for reconciling conflicting findings" - Personality and Individual Differences - 2025

## Core Insight

The long-running controversy about whether disgust causally influences moral judgment has reached a convergence point: **incidental disgust only reliably influences moral judgment when decisions are emotionally grounded** (activating insula and anterior cingulate cortex) rather than rationally derived. This boundary condition resolves conflicting empirical findings by identifying the neural mechanism distinguishing emotional from utilitarian moral appraisals.

Additionally: disgust is primarily associated with **purity/sanctity-based moral judgments**, not harm/care or fairness judgments. This provides empirical precision to Moral Foundations Theory's purity domain.

---

## Evidence

**Review Consensus** (WIREs Cognitive Science, 2025 - Landy):
- Opposing camps now ripe for convergence
- Disgust sensitivity is primarily associated with purity/sanctity-based moral judgments
- Incidental disgust (contextual, irrelevant to judgment) has weaker and more conditional effects than trait disgust sensitivity

**Neurocognitive Framework** (Personality and Individual Differences, 2025 - Salvo, Ottaviani, Mancini):
- Incidental disgust reliably influences moral judgment only when decisions are **emotionally grounded**
- Neural signature: insula + anterior cingulate cortex activation
- When decisions are rationally derived, incidental disgust loses its effect
- Provides the neurobiological mechanism explaining previously conflicting results

**Behavioral Immune System (BIS) Framework**:
- Disgust evolved to detect pathogen threats
- This system *overgeneralizes* to social and moral contexts
- Drives avoidance of outgroup members and norm violators (even when no actual pathogen risk)
- Explains why disgust-inducing rhetoric is effective at generating moral condemnation

---

## The Overgeneralization Mechanism - A Key Insight

The BIS overgeneralization is particularly important: the same neural mechanism that protected our ancestors from pathogens now fires when we encounter moral violations, outgroup members, or anything perceived as "contaminating" social norms. This creates:

1. A biological basis for moral disgust reactions (not just cultural)
2. A vulnerability to manipulation: framing outgroup members as "disgusting" taps the same threat-detection hardware as actual pathogen avoidance
3. Differential political activation: purity/disgust-sensitive individuals will be more susceptible to moral rhetoric that frames social threats in contamination terms
