## Core Insight

Awe produces a strong, reliable increase in prosocial behavior (r=0.40 across 30 papers, 91 effect sizes, N=27,407 - the largest synthesis to date). The mechanism: awe **diminishes the individuated self** ("me-centric" cognition), attunes people to larger entities (communities, nature, humanity), and triggers "we-minded" social tendencies including cooperation, affiliation, and collective action.

This is not merely a pleasant feeling - awe produces a distinct physiological signature: **sympathetic-parasympathetic coactivation** (simultaneously activating arousal AND calming systems), distinct from joy or neutral conditions.

## Evidence

**Meta-Analysis** (Basic and Applied Social Psychology, December 2024):
- 30 papers, 91 effect sizes, N=27,407
- r = 0.40, SE = 0.04 (strong effect, small standard error)
- Sharing and volunteering: strongest prosociality effects
- Helping: weakest effect
- Effect stronger with higher percentage of female participants

**Moral Elevation in Children** (Behavioral Sciences, September 2025):
- Witnessing moral virtue → prosocial behavior in children
- **Sympathetic-parasympathetic coactivation** - physiological signature distinct from joy and neutral
- This autonomic signature marks awe as a biologically distinct state, not just an emotion label

**Awe and Collective Action** (2025 Review):
- Awe diminishes individuated self → attunes to larger entities → triggers "we-minded" tendencies
- Cooperation, affiliation, and collective action all increase
- Sequential pathway in adolescents: awe → nature connectedness → moral identity → pro-environmental action

## The Self-Diminishment Mechanism

The prosocial effect of awe is mediated by **self-diminishment** - the temporary dissolution of the me-centric perspective. This creates a direct conceptual bridge to the vault's Buddhism-neuroscience cluster:

- Buddhist "no-self" (anatta) is a trained version of the spontaneous awe experience
- Flow states also involve self-diminishment (DMN deactivation)
- Awe provides the *natural trigger* for self-transcendence that Buddhism seeks through practice and flow seeks through optimal challenge

**The Three Pathways to Prosocial Self-Transcendence**:
1. Awe (spontaneous, triggered by vastness or beauty)
2. Flow (performance-triggered, sustained)
3. Buddhist practice (intentional, trained)

All three produce similar self-diminishment and prosocial orientation through different routes.
