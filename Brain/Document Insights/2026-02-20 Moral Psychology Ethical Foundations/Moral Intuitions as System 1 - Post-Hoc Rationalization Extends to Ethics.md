## Core Insight

Moral judgment operates primarily through System 1 (fast, automatic, emotional) with System 2 providing post-hoc rationalization - the same pattern already documented in economic and identity-related decisions. The 2024-2025 research adds crucial nuance: the classical dual-process model (Greene's emotion vs. reason dichotomy) is being replaced by a **dynamical systems account** where moral processing involves overlapping, continuous systems with relative rather than absolute functional specialization.

The key empirical finding: under cognitive load (System 2 depletion), people find harmful-but-beneficial actions (e.g., sacrificing one person to save five) *less* acceptable - consistent with intuitive moral aversion dominating when deliberative resources are unavailable.

## Evidence

**Study 1** (Philosophical Psychology, December 2024):
- Argues Greene's framework depends on modular account of mind now challenged by dynamical systems approaches
- Non-exclusive dual-process theories (relative specialization) better fit evidence than exclusive dichotomy (absolute modules)

**Study 2** (Frontiers in Psychology, February 2025):
- Cognitive load reduces acceptance of harmful-but-beneficial actions
- Under load: simpler mental representations disconnect harmful actions from beneficial consequences
- Consistent with System 1 intuitive processing dominating under resource depletion

**Cushman Lab Reframing (2025)**:
- Proposes model-free vs. model-based moral learning as better framework than emotion/reason dichotomy
- Model-free: tracks reliable behavioral outcomes through reinforcement (habit-based ethics)
- Model-based: constructs causal models to simulate consequences (deliberative ethics)
