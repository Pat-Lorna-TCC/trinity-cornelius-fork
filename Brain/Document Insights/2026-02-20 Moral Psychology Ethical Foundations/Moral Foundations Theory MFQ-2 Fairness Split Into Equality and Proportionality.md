# Moral Foundations Theory MFQ-2 - Fairness Split Into Equality and Proportionality

**Source**: Atari, M., et al. "Moral Foundations Questionnaire-2" - moralfoundations.org; Dogruyol et al. (2024), Winkelkotte et al. (2025), Frisari et al. (2025), Iranian validation (2025)

## Core Insight

The original Moral Foundations Theory Fairness foundation has been empirically split into two distinct constructs in the updated MFQ-2: **Equality** (intuitions about equal treatment and equal outcomes) and **Proportionality** (intuitions about reward proportional to merit or contribution). This revision has now been validated across 25+ populations including Turkey, Ghana, Iran, and Italy - making it the most cross-culturally validated update to the theory.

---

## Evidence

**Study Design**: Multi-study validation using factor analysis, exploratory structural equation modeling, network psychometrics, and Item Response Theory
**Sample Sizes**: Turkey N=large (2024); Ghana N=1,049 (2025); Iran N=392 + N=989 (2025); Italy confirmed (2025); 25+ populations total
**Key Results**:
- Six-factor structure (Care, Loyalty, Authority, Purity + Equality + Proportionality) confirmed in Turkey, Iran, Italy
- Ghana validation found partial fit - Western conceptualizations may miss non-Western moral realities
- Equality/Proportionality distinction maps onto liberal/conservative moral differences more precisely than original single Fairness foundation
- Liberals emphasize Equality; conservatives emphasize Proportionality
- Improved internal consistency over MFQ-1 in all validated populations
**Important Caveat (2025)**: Across five studies (N=3,327), MFT predicts *attitude positions* better than whether people hold those attitudes as genuine *moral convictions* - some foundations may reflect conventional rather than truly moral values

---

## Why This Matters for Political Psychology

The Equality/Proportionality split is not just academic. It explains why:
- Liberals and conservatives can both claim to value "fairness" yet mean opposite things
- Disagreements about taxation, redistribution, and meritocracy feel like moral conflicts (because they are - but they're conflicts between two *different fairness foundations*)
- Cross-partisan persuasion fails when both sides invoke "fairness" as shared ground but have incompatible underlying intuitions

This is the empirical backbone of the finding that moral disagreements often stem from different foundations being activated, not from one side lacking morality.
