## Core Framework

Multiple 2025 papers converge on the argument that the next frontier for AI capability is not **larger individual models** but **structured collectives of models**. Classical scaling laws relating performance to parameter count and compute are **insufficient for tasks requiring debate, division of labor, and long-horizon coordination**. The "societies of models" paradigm treats AI development as a collective intelligence design problem rather than a single-model capability problem.

Three interaction regimes are emerging in these societies:
1. **Competition**: Models with different objectives competing for resources/influence
2. **Collaboration**: Models with shared objectives pooling capabilities
3. **Coordination**: Models with different roles executing interdependent tasks

**Why Scaling Laws Alone Fail**:
- Debate requires adversarial interaction not present in individual model training
- Division of labor requires specialization that homogeneous models resist
- Long-horizon coordination requires persistent state across multiple reasoning agents
- Error correction requires diverse perspectives, not a single very large perspective

## Three Interaction Regimes and Their Implications

**Competition** produces pressure for capability improvement and diverse strategy development - analogous to market competition producing efficient allocation. Risk: defection, adversarial optimization against human values.

**Collaboration** produces capability pooling and specialization - analogous to division of labor. Risk: homogenization if agents converge to similar approaches.

**Coordination** produces complex task completion beyond individual agent capability - analogous to organizational hierarchy. Risk: coordination overhead growing faster than capability benefit.

**Key Design Question**: Which regime is appropriate for which task type? Current multi-agent system design largely defaults to collaboration, ignoring the potential of structured competition and the necessity of true coordination for complex tasks.
