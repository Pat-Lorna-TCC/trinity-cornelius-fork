# Reformers Beat Experts - Rewarding Directional Contribution Over Individual Accuracy

**Source**: Wang, G. et al., "Individual incentives that promote collective intelligence" - PNAS, 2025. DOI: 10.1073/pnas.2516535122
**Document Type**: Research Paper (PNAS)

## Core Insight

Rewarding "reformers" - those whose contributions move collective predictions in a better direction, regardless of their personal accuracy - consistently outperforms rewarding "experts" (those with the highest individual accuracy). This counter-intuitive finding challenges decades of talent management orthodoxy and reveals that **what matters is not who is right, but whose contribution improves the group's direction**.

---

## Evidence

**Study Design**: Mathematical theory of collective intelligence aggregation, empirically tested across three incentive schemes
**Key Results**:
- **Expert incentives FAIL**: Rewarding individually accurate people destroys diversity through social imitation cascades - others copy the "expert," collapsing independence
- **Niche expert incentives**: Accurate but fragile - specialists in underrepresented domains help but the system is vulnerable to environmental change
- **Reformer incentives SUCCEED**: Preserves diversity and helps collective systems recover from environmental change; the individually least accurate member may be the most valuable contributor if their directional error moves the group toward accuracy

**Analogy**: Prediction markets and stock trading already approximate the reformer model (profit comes from moving collective beliefs in the right direction, not just being individually accurate). Scientific peer review more closely resembles niche experts.

**Replication**: Mathematical derivation with empirical testing; PNAS 2025

---

## Why This Is Counter-Intuitive

Standard organizational logic says: identify your best performers, reward them, let others imitate them. This is the meritocratic default. The research shows this logic **actively destroys the collective intelligence it tries to optimize** by:
1. Creating social imitation cascades (diversity collapse)
2. Homogenizing predictions (eliminating error cancellation)
3. Making the system brittle to environmental change

The reformer insight inverts this: **the value of a contribution is relational and directional, not absolute**. Someone can be a poor predictor individually yet systematically improve the group whenever they contribute.

---

## Applications

**Prediction markets**: Already approximate reformer incentives - calibrate against this finding
**Scientific teams**: Should evaluate contributions by their effect on collective accuracy, not individual publication records
**Organizational design**: Performance reviews that reward "best performers" may destroy team collective intelligence
**AI ensembles**: Diverse agents whose individual accuracy is low may be essential for ensemble robustness - do not prune based on individual benchmarks alone
