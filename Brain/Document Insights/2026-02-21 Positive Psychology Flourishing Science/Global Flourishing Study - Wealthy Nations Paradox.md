# Global Flourishing Study - Wealthy Nations Paradox

**Source**: VanderWeele, T.J. et al. - "The Global Flourishing Study: Study Profile and Initial Results on Flourishing" - Nature Mental Health (2025). PMC available.
**Document Type**: Longitudinal Cohort Study (Wave 1 of multi-wave panel)

## Core Insight

The largest cross-cultural flourishing study ever conducted ($43.4M, 202,898 participants, 22 countries) found that wealthier nations (Sweden, USA) score paradoxically *lower* on meaning, relationships, and character than lower-income nations (Indonesia, Philippines, Israel) despite scoring higher on financial security. GDP is an empirically invalid proxy for human flourishing.

---

## Evidence

**Study Design**: Longitudinal panel study, nationally representative samples
**Sample Size**: 202,898 participants across 22 countries (Wave 1); 128,868 (Wave 2, 62% retention)
**Key Results**:
- Indonesia: highest composite flourishing (8.10/10)
- Japan: lowest composite flourishing (5.89/10)
- USA and Sweden: high financial security, low meaning/relationships/character scores
- Childhood abuse effect on adult flourishing: -0.34 (persistent across cultures)
- Excellent childhood health: +0.46 effect on adult flourishing
- Religious attendance (weekly+): 7.67 flourishing vs. 6.86 for never-attending
**Replication**: Wave 2 data (April 2025) broadly consistent with Wave 1
**Citation**: VanderWeele et al., Nature Mental Health, 2025

---

## The Paradox Mechanism (Speculative - Not Yet Confirmed)

Why do wealthier nations underperform on meaning and relationships? Candidate mechanisms under investigation:
- **Individualism trade-off**: Economic development correlates with individualism, which may erode relational bonds
- **Hedonic adaptation**: Financial security removes survival motivators but creates no substitute for meaning
- **Social fabric erosion**: Market economies may crowd out community and religious institutions that supply meaning

**Note**: The GFS documents the paradox but does not yet establish the causal mechanism.
