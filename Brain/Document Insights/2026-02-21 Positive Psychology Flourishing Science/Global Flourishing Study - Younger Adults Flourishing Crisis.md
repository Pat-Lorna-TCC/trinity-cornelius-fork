# Global Flourishing Study - Younger Adults Flourishing Crisis

## Core Insight

Prior research predicted a U-shaped relationship between age and happiness (high in youth, dip in middle age, recovery in old age). The Global Flourishing Study across 22 countries found that flourishing for ages 18-49 is essentially *flat* before increasing in later life - meaning younger generations may be measurably worse off than previous generations at the same age, a finding consistent across all 22 nations studied.

---

## Evidence

**Study Design**: Cross-national longitudinal panel, nationally representative
**Sample Size**: 202,898 across 22 countries
**Key Results**:
- Ages 18-49: flat flourishing trajectory across all 22 countries
- Ages 50+: clear increase in flourishing
- This pattern contradicts the previously expected U-shape curve
- Pattern holds across high-income and low-income nations
- Convergent with separate data: 1 in 5 young adults globally experience loneliness (Holt-Lunstad, World Psychiatry, 2024)
**Citation**: VanderWeele et al., Nature Mental Health, 2025

---

## Why This Diverges from Prior Research

Older studies (used to support U-shape) had several limitations:
- Cross-sectional designs that confounded age effects with cohort effects
- Smaller, less representative samples
- Did not distinguish between hedonic well-being and comprehensive flourishing (meaning, relationships, character)

The GFS used nationally representative samples in 22 countries with a comprehensive flourishing inventory - making the flat trajectory finding robust.
