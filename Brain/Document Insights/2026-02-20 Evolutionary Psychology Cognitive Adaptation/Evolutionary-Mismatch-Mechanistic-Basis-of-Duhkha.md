## The Synthesis

Buddhism identifies craving (tanha) and ignorance (avidya) as the roots of suffering (duhkha). Evolutionary psychology now provides a **mechanistic neurobiological account** of why this is true - and why it is not merely philosophical.

**The Evolutionary Mechanism of Duhkha**:
1. Craving arises because reward systems evolved to drive pursuit of adaptive goals (food, status, mates, safety) in environments where those goals were **genuinely scarce and difficult to obtain**
2. Modern environments provide **supernormal versions** of these stimuli at minimal effort (processed food, social media likes, pornography, gambling)
3. The optimization engine (dopamine RPE) cannot distinguish between "best available in environment" and "artificially amplified"
4. This creates a permanent mismatch between **the intensity of craving** and the **adaptive value of its satisfaction**

**This is the evolutionary mechanism behind the Buddhist observation that desire leads to suffering**: The craving does not terminate upon satisfaction because the dopamine optimization engine immediately recalibrates to seek what is better. Modern environments provide satisfaction at every level but not cessation of wanting.

## The Lisa Feldman Barrett Layer

The 2024 HBES keynote by Lisa Feldman Barrett ("the brain's job is to predict the body's future needs") aligns with both the active inference framework and Buddhist concepts of the fabricated self:

- The brain **constructs experience** rather than passively receiving it
- This construction is optimized for **ancestral fitness**, not contemporary wellbeing
- The "self" that suffers is itself a construction - a prediction the brain makes about who is doing the wanting
- Buddhist practice is technology for disrupting this construction (seeing the self as a prediction, not a fixed entity)

**The convergence**: Predictive processing, evolutionary psychology, and Buddhist philosophy all arrive at the same insight - suffering arises from a self-system optimized for a world that no longer exists.

Both frameworks suggest the same solution to the mismatch:

| Buddhist Practice | Evolutionary Mechanism |
