## Core Framework

Social systems are not simply external selection pressures on cognition - **they arise from the decisions individuals make about who to interact with and how to behave**. This creates a coevolutionary feedback loop:

- Social living generates cognitive uncertainty requiring intelligence (to navigate complex social dynamics)
- But social networks also provide cognitive resources that reduce uncertainty (distributed knowledge, collective problem solving)
- Neither cognition nor sociality can be explained without the other
- The "social brain hypothesis" (social complexity drives brain evolution) is therefore only half right - it is bidirectional

## Why This Is Counterintuitive

The standard account: social complexity (large groups, political alliances, coalition dynamics) created selection pressure for larger brains and more sophisticated cognition. This is the "Machiavellian intelligence hypothesis."

The bidirectional account adds: the social groups that individuals choose to form are themselves shaped by their cognitive capacities. More intelligent individuals form different social networks (more complex, more flexible) that then create different selection pressures on subsequent generations. **The cognitive landscape and the social landscape co-create each other.**

**Implication for evolutionary psychology**: You cannot read off social structure from cognitive architecture, nor cognitive architecture from social structure. The two coevolve in cycles.
