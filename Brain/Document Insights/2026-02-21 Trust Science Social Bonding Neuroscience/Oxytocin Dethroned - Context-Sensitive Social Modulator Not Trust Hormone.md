## Core Insight

Twenty years of research culminate in a decisive paradigm shift: oxytocin is NOT a universal "trust hormone." The original 2005 Kosfeld et al. *Nature* finding - that intranasal oxytocin increases trust - has failed to replicate in a registered replication by some of the original authors, finding no effect under identical conditions. The 2025 scientific consensus reframes oxytocin as a **context- and person-sensitive social neuromodulator** with effects strongest in low-trust individuals and primarily operating on *caution calibration* rather than generalized trust.

---

## Evidence

**Study Design**: Multiple including registered replication study + high-powered preregistered bioRxiv preprint (n=359) + animal model (rhesus macaques with amygdala infusion)

**Key Results**:
- **Failed replication**: Kosfeld et al. (2005) original finding did not replicate under identical conditions
- **Population-specific effect**: Intranasal OXT increases trust behavior by ~15-17% ONLY in individuals with low baseline trust disposition (n=359, 2025 bioRxiv). For typical-trust individuals, no effect
- **Modulates caution, not trust**: Oxytocin correlates positively with *caution* (belief that vigilance is needed in high-uncertainty situations) rather than with generalized trust
- **State-dependent prosocial modulation**: BLA oxytocin infusion in rhesus macaques showed effects ONLY during high prosocial states - sustaining choices and counteracting performance decline via amygdala-prefrontal coordination, not a simple trust signal
- **In-group favoritism**: From an evolutionary perspective, oxytocin motivates in-group cooperation while simultaneously DECREASING trust toward out-group members
- **Social memory**: Oxytocin signaling in the medial amygdala is critical for social recognition - ability to identify familiar individuals

**Replication**: Original 2005 claim failed registered replication; 2025 preprint (n=359) specifically designed to resolve controversy

---

## Paradigm Shift

The "take oxytocin to trust more" narrative is scientifically defunct.

**Previous model**: Oxytocin = universal trust activator
**Current model**: Oxytocin = context-sensitive social salience modulator that:
1. Amplifies attention to social cues
2. Adjusts anxiety/caution thresholds contextually
3. Works primarily in low-trust individuals (where there is room to move)
4. Promotes in-group prosociality while potentially increasing out-group vigilance

**Why this matters for AI design**: Attempts to pharmacologically increase trust (or simulate OXT effects) will be ineffective in typical-trust populations. Trust is built through repeated interaction, not neurochemical shortcut.
