## Core Insight

Two 2024-2025 findings extend dopamine's role in trust: (1) Dopamine encodes prediction errors for AVERSIVE outcomes (not just rewards), extending the RPE framework to include betrayal and social violation as negative dopaminergic events. (2) Policy complexity suppresses dopamine responses - meaning complex social environments (bureaucracies, multi-party trust relationships, institutional trust) systematically reduce the clarity of social prediction error signals, potentially degrading trust learning.

---

## Evidence

### Finding 1: Dopamine Encodes Aversive Prediction Errors (*Science Advances* 2024)
**Study**: Striatal dopamine signals in temporal-difference learning manner
**Key Finding**:
- Dopamine responds to aversive feedback in a temporal-difference learning manner, not just to rewards
- 2024-2025 research shows dopamine encodes generalized prediction errors - including for valueless stimuli
- Trust violations represent a *negative social prediction error* - the received behavior (betrayal) is worse than predicted (trustworthiness) - this engages dopamine prediction error circuits

**Implication for trust**: Trust violations trigger dopaminergic depression below baseline, not merely neutral absence of reward. This makes betrayal neurologically costly in the dopamine system, providing a mechanism for why betrayal is so impactful.

### Finding 2: Policy Complexity Suppresses Dopamine (*Journal of Neuroscience* 2025)
**Study**: Dopamine responses under varying policy complexity conditions
**Key Finding**:
- Dopamine responses are suppressed when the behavioral policy (set of decision rules) is complex
- Complex social environments reduce the clarity of social prediction error signals

**Implication for institutional trust**: Institutions are complex - their behavior results from many interacting factors (policies, personnel, politics, resource constraints). This complexity suppresses dopaminergic social learning signals, making it harder to update trust in institutions through direct feedback. Institutional trust building may require simpler, cleaner feedback mechanisms than interpersonal trust.

## Integration with Existing Dopamine Framework

The existing knowledge base has:
- RPE as the basic mechanism of dopamine-driven learning
- Dopamine as the universal thread connecting neuroscience, identity, behavior change

New extensions from this research:
1. **Trust learning is dopaminergic** - building and breaking trust uses the same RPE circuits as all other dopamine-driven learning
2. **Betrayal = strong negative RPE** - not just absence of reward but below-baseline response
3. **Institutional trust is harder to learn** because policy complexity suppresses the RPE signal clarity

This creates a unified theory: all social learning (including trust) is dopamine RPE learning, but the signal quality varies by social complexity.
