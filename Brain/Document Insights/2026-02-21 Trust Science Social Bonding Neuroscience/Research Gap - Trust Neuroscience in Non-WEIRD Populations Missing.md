## The Gap

Most trust neuroscience research has been conducted in Western, Educated, Industrialized, Rich, Democratic (WEIRD) populations. Cross-cultural validity of neural trust models is largely untested. The cultural differences in trust architecture are behavioral - collectivist vs. individualist patterns are well-documented - but whether the NEURAL MECHANISMS (amygdala rapid appraisal, TPJ mentalizing, vmPFC reputation updating) operate identically across cultures is unknown.

---

## Why This Matters

**Known cultural behavioral differences in trust**:
- Individualist cultures: Trust is predominantly calculative and institution-based, extending to strangers
- Collectivist cultures: Trust is primarily personal, rooted in relationship history, family ties, and in-group loyalty
- 2025 cross-national study (Canada, USA, China, Taiwan): Self-construal predicts generalized trust differently across cultures
- Seven-nation organizational study: Higher institutional trust in individualist societies; collectivist societies concentrate trust within smaller, denser networks

**Unknown neural mechanisms**:
- Does the amygdala's 100ms trustworthiness response use the same facial features across cultures?
- Do TPJ mentalizing parameters differ by cultural background (individualist vs. collectivist inference styles)?
- Do vmPFC reputation-updating dynamics differ in contexts where institutional vs. personal trust is primary?
- How does cross-cultural marriage between trust systems affect neural processing?

---

## What We Know (Related Research)

- **Domain A (Neural)**: Neural trust circuits identified primarily in Western samples (US, European labs)
- **Domain B (Cultural)**: Robust behavioral and self-report differences in trust architecture across cultures
- **Missing**: Whether neural mechanisms implementing these culturally-different trust architectures are the same or different

**Additional gap**: Social pain circuit research - are the ACC/insula activations for social rejection uniform across cultures, or does cultural context modulate which social events constitute "rejection"?

---

## Research Implications

If neural trust mechanisms differ by culture:
- AI trust design must be culturally adapted at the neural level, not just behaviorally
- Therapeutic trust interventions developed in WEIRD contexts may be less effective in non-WEIRD populations
- Organizational trust design (which currently assumes Western norms) may fail in global contexts for mechanistic reasons, not just surface cultural reasons

If neural trust mechanisms are universal:
- Cultural behavioral differences emerge from the same neural hardware operating with different "priors" (culturally transmitted trust expectations)
- Cultural trust design can focus on updating priors rather than redesigning neural mechanisms
