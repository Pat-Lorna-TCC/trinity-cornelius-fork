## Core Insight

Only 8% of US adults reported "great trust" in science as of June 2025 - a decline of nearly **24 percentage points** from the pre-pandemic period. This represents a collapse of the epistemic foundation of democratic science communication. Institutional trust is neuropsychologically distinct from interpersonal trust: it is extended to abstract entities, is more susceptible to media-mediated signals, and erodes differently - in "shells" (hollowing out rather than abrupt collapse). Mental health problems, socioeconomic disadvantage, and childhood adversity all predict lower institutional trust at age 23.

- Mental health problems predict lower institutional trust at age 23
- Socioeconomic disadvantage predicts lower institutional trust at age 23
- Childhood adversity predicts lower institutional trust at age 23
- 20 social and mental health predictors across childhood, adolescence, and adulthood examined

**Neuropsychological Basis**:
- Trust and distrust around institutions are neuropsychologically distinct processes
- Trust and distrust can co-exist around the same institution (not simply opposites)
- Institutional trust is complementary organizing principles, not a single dimension

## How Institutional Trust Differs from Interpersonal Trust

| Dimension | Interpersonal Trust | Institutional Trust |
|-----------|--------------------|--------------------|
| Target | Individual person | Abstract entity/organization |
| Inference type | Intentions of specific agent | Organizational character |
| Updating mechanism | Direct interaction | Media-mediated signals |
| Erosion pattern | Event-driven | Gradual hollowing out |
| Susceptibility | Betrayal events | Sustained narrative damage |

**Key insight**: Institutions rarely collapse outright - they erode in shells. Trust withdraws incrementally, hollowing out the institution while its formal structures remain intact.

## Science Trust Recommendations (2025)

From *Social and Personality Psychology Compass* 2025:
1. Independent peer review for policy-relevant science
2. Transparent communication of how evidence integrates with policy decisions
3. Compromised autonomy and reduced transparency significantly undermine public trust
4. Multi-stakeholder approaches: media literacy education, algorithmic design for civic discourse
