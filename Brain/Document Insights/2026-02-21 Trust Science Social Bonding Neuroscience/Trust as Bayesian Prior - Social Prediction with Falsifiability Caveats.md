## Core Framework

Trust can be understood as a **prior probability distribution** over another agent's future behavior - a Bayesian prior that gets updated through social interactions. High-trust priors predict prosocial behavior; low-trust priors predict defection. Betrayals are large negative social prediction errors that force prior updating. However, a 2025 paper raises serious empirical concerns about the falsifiability of Bayesian brain claims - this framework should be used as descriptively useful but mechanistically unconfirmed.

**STATUS: Theoretically compelling but empirical falsifiability is under challenge**

## The Trust-as-Prior Framework

### How Trust Operates as a Bayesian Prior

**Prior formation**:
- Each person (or institution) has a prior probability distribution: "How likely are they to act prosocially?"
- High trust = prior centered near 1.0 (expect prosocial behavior)
- Low trust = prior centered near 0.0 (expect defection or harm)
- New relationships start with prior based on base rates (e.g., cultural distrust of strangers) + first impressions

**Prior updating (Bayesian updating)**:
- Each interaction provides evidence: partner acted prosocially (likelihood shifts prior toward trust) or defected (shifts toward distrust)
- The strength of update depends on **precision** (inverse variance): high-certainty evidence updates more than ambiguous evidence
- Precision is encoded by monoaminergic neuromodulators: dopamine (reward prediction), serotonin (aversive prediction), norepinephrine (uncertainty/arousal)

**Betrayal as large negative prediction error**:
- Betrayal = received behavior (defection) is drastically worse than predicted (trust)
- This creates a very large negative prediction error
- A single strong betrayal can dramatically shift the prior because it is high-salience evidence
- This explains asymmetric trust dynamics: betrayals move priors more than equivalent positive events

### Distrust as Low-Precision Prior

If trust operates as a precision-weighted prior:
- **Distrust** = low-precision, high-uncertainty state that prevents effective social prediction
- The person cannot form stable expectations about the other agent's behavior
- This creates cognitive burden (continuous vigilance) and prevents efficient social cooperation
- Rebuilding trust = accumulating enough positive prediction errors to raise precision of the trust prior

## The Falsifiability Challenge (2025)

**CRITICAL CAVEAT**: A 2025 paper in *European Journal of Applied Physiology* - "The Myth of the Bayesian Brain" - challenges the framework's empirical falsifiability:

- The framework's remarkable flexibility in accommodating diverse findings raises concerns about whether it generates genuinely testable predictions
- Key challenge: Empirical evidence that uncertainty manipulation does NOT always modulate neural gain contradicts core predictions
- The framework can accommodate most experimental findings by adjusting precision parameters post-hoc (unfalsifiable adjustment)

**Assessment**: The Bayesian/trust-as-prior framework is:
- **Descriptively powerful**: Provides coherent account of trust building, betrayal, and repair
- **Mechanistically uncertain**: The specific precision-weighting implementation claims are under challenge
- **Heuristically useful**: Even if wrong mechanistically, the Bayesian update intuition guides sensible predictions about trust dynamics
