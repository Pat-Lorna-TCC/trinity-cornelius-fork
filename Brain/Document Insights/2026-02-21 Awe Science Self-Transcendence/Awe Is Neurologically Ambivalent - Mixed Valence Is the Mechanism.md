# Awe Is Neurologically Ambivalent - Mixed Valence Is the Mechanism

**Source**: Lee J, Han DD, Oh SY, Cha J. "Awe is characterized as an ambivalent affect in the human behavior and cortex." *Communications Psychology* (Nature), 3(1):123, 2025. DOI: https://www.nature.com/articles/s44271-025-00299-2
**Document Type**: Empirical Research - EEG-VR Study
**Extracted By**: AI (document-insight-extractor agent)
**Extraction Date**: 2026-02-21

## Core Insight

Awe is not a primarily positive emotion - it is a neurologically distinct *ambivalent* (mixed-valence) state. The intensity of subjective awe in virtual reality was predicted not by purely positive feelings but by the duration and intensity of mixed feelings. At the neural level, frontoparietal channels most strongly differentiate awe's ambivalent signature, with competing signals integrated through the insula cortex.

---

## Evidence

**Study Design**: EEG-VR experiment using contrastive learning (CEBRA deep learning) and Hidden Markov modeling
**Sample Size**: N = 43 (VR-EEG awe study)
**Key Results**:
- Awe intensity predicted by ambivalent (mixed) feelings, not purely positive ones
- Ambivalent feelings represented as spatially distinct from positive and negative affect in individual latent neural spaces
- Frontoparietal channels most implicated in distinguishing valent states during awe-inducing VR
- Multiple EEG band powers differentiated awe from other states
- Insula cortex integrates the competing positive-negative signals

**Replication**: Single study; extends foundational fMRI research on awe and DMN suppression

---

## Significance: Resolves a Longstanding Definitional Debate

Prior research treated awe as positive or complex-positive. This study directly challenges that assumption. Awe is characterized by the co-presence of wonder AND threat/incomprehension - the tension between these is not incidental but constitutive of the awe experience.

**Practical Implication**: Awe-induction protocols should NOT artificially suppress the threatening, destabilizing, or overwhelming elements of awe. The "fear" component - the sense of being overwhelmed or dwarfed - is part of what makes awe work neurologically. Sanitizing awe into purely positive experience may reduce its efficacy.

**Therapeutic Implication**: This means awe-based interventions should be designed to hold the tension of mixed affect, similar to how psychedelic therapy holds the difficult elements of the experience rather than eliminating them.
