# Awe-Prosociality Meta-Analysis - r=0.40 Across 27,407 Participants

**Source**: "The Relationship Between Awe and Prosocial Behavior: A Three-Level Meta-Analysis." *Basic and Applied Social Psychology*, Vol. 47, No. 3, 2024. DOI: 10.1080/01973533.2024.2433721
**Document Type**: Three-level Meta-Analysis
**Extracted By**: AI (document-insight-extractor agent)
**Extraction Date**: 2026-02-21
**Session**: 2026-02-21 Awe Science Self-Transcendence

## Core Insight

A landmark 2024 three-level meta-analysis of 30 papers, 91 effect sizes, and 27,407 individuals confirms a strong positive correlation (r = 0.40) between awe and prosocial behavior. Both dispositional (trait) awe and state awe predict prosocial action. Critically, this relationship holds for both positive AND negative awe experiences - consistent with the finding that awe's mixed-valence nature is the operative mechanism.

---

## Evidence

**Study Design**: Three-level meta-analysis
**Sample Size**: 30 papers, 91 effect sizes, N = 27,407
**Key Results**:
- r = 0.40 (SE = 0.04) - strong positive correlation
- Both dispositional and state awe positively associated with prosocial behavior
- Both positive and negative awe experiences correlated with prosocial behavior
- Sharing and volunteering showed strongest correlations; helping behaviors weakest
- Female percentage moderates the relationship (higher female proportion = stronger link)
- Cultural individualism does NOT moderate the relationship (awe-prosociality is cross-cultural)

**Parallel meta-analysis** (*Current Psychology*, 2024) found that age and cultural background moderates the dispositional awe-prosociality link specifically.

---

## Key Finding: Cultural Universality

The finding that cultural individualism does not moderate the awe-prosociality link is significant. It suggests the awe mechanism for expanding self-concern operates across individualist and collectivist cultures. This is consistent with the "expanded self" reframe: awe does not require a collectivist starting orientation - it expands self-boundaries regardless of cultural baseline.

---

## The Mechanism: Social Connectedness, Not Small Self

A separate 2025 study (Luo & Yuan, *Scientific Reports*) provides mechanistic precision: social connectedness - not the small self per se - mediates awe's effect on ethical behavior. This refinement suggests awe improves prosocial behavior primarily by making people feel *part of* a social world, not by making them feel cosmically diminished.

This distinction matters for intervention design:
- Social framing of awe experiences (not just solitary vastness) may maximize prosocial effect
- Collective awe contexts (concerts, rituals, nature groups) may be more prosocially potent than solitary awe
