## Core Insight

Buddhist anatta (no-self) practice and awe-induced self-transcendence converge on the same neural endpoint - loosened self-models through DMN disruption - but through fundamentally different timescales and routes. Buddhist practice requires sustained attention training over years; awe requires only encountering vastness. Both expand the self-boundary, increase prosociality, and reduce self-referential rumination. The key distinction is depth and duration: awe produces a temporary no-self adjacent state; deep anatta practice aims at permanent insight into the constructed nature of self.

---

## The Parallel Mechanisms

From the 2025 Frontiers in Psychology Buddhist meditation paper: "A growing body of research suggests that what we commonly regard as a fixed 'self' is better understood as a dynamic construct that can be broken down and reassembled over time. Through consistent contemplative practice, the self-model can reorganize in non-linear ways, occasionally giving rise to experiences of 'no-self' (anatta), where the usual boundaries of identity are perceived differently."

This is the same process described for awe at a compressed timescale:
- Both target the DMN self-model
- Both reduce the felt sense of fixed, bounded selfhood
- Both expand identity to broader categories
- Both produce prosocial aftereffects

| Feature | Buddhist Anatta Practice | Awe Experience |
|---------|-------------------------|----------------|
| Route | Sustained attention training | Environmental vastness encounter |
| Timescale | Years → permanent insight | Minutes → days/weeks of aftereffect |
| DMN effect | Gradual reorganization | Acute suppression |
| Self-boundary | Progressive dissolution | Temporary softening |
| Prosociality | Long-term character change | Immediate behavioral effect |
| Accessibility | Requires commitment, teacher | Available anywhere (nature, music, art) |
| Depth of no-self | Complete (in deep practice) | Partial (expanded self, not dissolved) |

## The "Expanded Self" vs. "No Self" Distinction

This is theoretically precise: Buddhist anatta aims at genuine insight that *no* fixed self can be found anywhere. Awe produces an "expanded self" - the self-boundary widens to include others and larger categories, but a sense of self persists. This is not a failure of awe but a point on the same continuum:

```
Ordinary self (rigid, isolated)
        ↓ (awe)
Expanded self (permeable, socially connected)
        ↓ (deep meditation)
No-self (boundaries dissolved; only awareness remains)
        ↓ (anatta insight)
Post-insight: Self as tool, not essence
```

Awe may be the secular, accessible first step on this continuum - producing enough boundary softening to increase prosociality and reduce suffering without requiring the full commitment of contemplative practice.

---

## Implications for Accessibility of Wisdom

Buddhist insight traditions took millennia to develop and require significant practice commitment. The discovery that awe - available through nature walks, music, and art - produces neurologically parallel states raises an important possibility: the experiential wisdom that traditions guarded behind years of practice may be more democratically accessible than previously understood.

This does not trivialize contemplative practice - the depth and permanence of anatta insight through sustained practice is genuinely different from brief awe. But it suggests that the *direction* of transformation available through practice can be sampled experientially through awe, potentially motivating deeper practice.
