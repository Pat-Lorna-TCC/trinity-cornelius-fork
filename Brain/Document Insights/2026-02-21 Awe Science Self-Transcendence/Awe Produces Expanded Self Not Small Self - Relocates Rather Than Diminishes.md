# Awe Produces Expanded Self Not Small Self - Relocates Rather Than Diminishes

**Source**: Edwards ME, Mendenhall K, Sanders C, King LA. "Small but Still Significant: Awe and the Self." *Personality and Social Psychology Bulletin*, 2025. DOI: 10.1177/01461672251359336
**Document Type**: Empirical Research - Within-person experimental studies
**Extracted By**: AI (document-insight-extractor agent)
**Extraction Date**: 2026-02-21
**Session**: 2026-02-21 Awe Science Self-Transcendence

---

## Core Insight

The "small self" effect in awe research does not mean feeling *insignificant* - it means feeling smaller in relation to something vast while simultaneously widening the self-boundary to include others and universal categories. Awe *relocates* the self in a larger context; it does not destroy or diminish it. Post-awe, people more readily identify with global citizenship, include others in the self-concept, and endorse broader social identity.

---

## Evidence

**Study Design**: Four within-person experiments
**Key Results**:
- Awe shrinks metaphorical self-size but does NOT produce feelings of personal insignificance
- Participants reported greater significance across all conditions (not uniquely in awe conditions)
- Post-awe: increased identification with universal categories (global citizenship), others included in self-concept, broader social identity endorsed
- Awe binds self to social world - facilitates rather than undermines personal significance

---

## The Reframe: Small Self vs. Expanded Self

The dominant framing of awe as creating a "small self" has been partially misleading. Better framing:

| Prior Framing | Corrected Framing |
|---------------|-------------------|
| Awe makes self small | Awe expands the self-boundary |
| Ego contraction | Identity expansion |
| I am diminished | I am part of something vast |
| Insignificance | Recontextualization |
| Self destruction | Self relocation |

This distinction matters clinically: awe does not produce nihilism ("nothing matters because I am small") but recontextualization ("I matter as part of something larger").
