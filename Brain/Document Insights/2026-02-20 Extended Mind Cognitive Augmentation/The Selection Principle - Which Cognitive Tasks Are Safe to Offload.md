## Core Framework

Not all cognitive offloading to AI is equal. The **Selection Principle** provides the decision criterion:

**Safe to offload** (procedural/retrieval-based tasks):
- Arithmetic calculations
- Fact lookup and retrieval
- Formatting and structural organization
- Routine procedure execution
- Information aggregation across sources
- Analogous to calculator, search engine, or reference book use
- Human retains the capacity to do this work; it is not developmentally valuable per se

**Risky to offload** (integrative reasoning tasks):
- Synthesis across disparate concepts
- Argumentation and position construction
- Conceptual connection and analogy
- Novel problem framing
- Judgment calls requiring value weighting
- These are the tasks humans must practice to develop cognitive capacity
- Offloading these depletes the cognitive capital that enables expertise

---

## Why the Distinction Matters: The Development Criterion

The key addition to existing offloading literature is a **developmental criterion** alongside the functional criterion:

**Functional criterion** (traditional): Is this task cognitive? If yes, external performance counts as extended mind.

**Development criterion** (new): Does internal performance of this task contribute to cognitive development? If yes, offloading has a developmental cost that must be included in the value calculation.

Calculators pass the development criterion for most people (arithmetic fluency has limited developmental value for non-mathematicians). AI-generated synthesis fails the development criterion: the ability to synthesize, argue, and reason is foundational to intellectual competence at all levels.

---

## The Paradox for Knowledge Workers

This principle creates a specific paradox for knowledge workers: the tasks they are most tempted to offload to AI (synthesis, writing, analysis) are precisely the tasks that develop their core professional capabilities. Offloading these tasks produces immediate productivity gains while silently depleting the cognitive capital that makes them valuable.

The "hollowed mind" is most dangerous for knowledge workers because:
1. Their work outputs still look competent (AI produces quality work)
2. Their underlying capabilities decline (they did not do the cognitive work)
3. The gap is only visible when AI is unavailable or when genuinely novel problems arise

---

## The Epistemic Litmus Test

Three questions to determine whether to offload a task:

1. **Would I learn something by doing this myself?** If yes, the developmental cost of offloading is high.
2. **Does this task build judgment?** Judgment-building tasks (synthesis, evaluation, construction) should not be offloaded. Judgment-applying tasks (retrieval, calculation) can be.
3. **Is AI better than me at this type of task?** If AI is better AND the task doesn't build developmental capacity, offloading is rational. If AI is better AND the task builds capacity, offload selectively and practice independently.
