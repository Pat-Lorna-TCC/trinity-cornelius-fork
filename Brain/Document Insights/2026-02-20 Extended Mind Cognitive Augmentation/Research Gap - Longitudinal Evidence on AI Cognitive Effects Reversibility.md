## The Gap

No existing research has answered the most important empirical question in AI cognitive science: **Are the cognitive and neural changes associated with AI use permanent or reversible?**

---

## Why This Matters

The Kosmyna et al. Session 4 finding is provocative but inconclusive: participants who switched from AI assistance to unaided writing showed *persistent under-engagement* even after removing AI help. This suggests changes may persist beyond the AI-use episode - but the study lasted only 4 sessions over a short period.

**Policy and design implications differ dramatically based on reversibility**:

If changes are **reversible** (disengagement, not atrophy):
- "Cognitive vacations" from AI can restore neural engagement
- Intermittent AI use with deliberate unaided practice preserves capacity
- The concern is acute but manageable with appropriate hygiene

If changes are **permanent** (atrophy, not disengagement):
- Children who grow up with AI may have structurally different cognitive architectures
- Early-career professionals who offload integrative reasoning may permanently reduce their analytical capability
- The policy urgency is comparable to lead exposure - prevention is paramount; remediation is limited

---

## What We Know (Related Research)

**Domain: GPS and Hippocampus**:
- Navigation without GPS shows gradual spatial memory atrophy
- But the process is slow (years) and at least partially reversible with renewed practice
- Analogy suggests AI cognitive effects may follow similar trajectory

**Domain: Muscle memory and motor skills**:
- Motor skills can atrophy without practice but are highly recoverable
- Cognitive skills follow different trajectories depending on developmental period

**Domain: Expertise acquisition**:
- Expertise appears to require specific developmental windows (language, musical pitch, etc.)
- If integrative reasoning has critical developmental periods (evidence from child offloading research), then age of AI adoption matters enormously

**What's Missing**: Direct measurement of cognitive capacity changes over 1+ year longitudinal AI use with controlled non-use comparison conditions.

---

## Critical Studies Needed

1. **2-year longitudinal study**: Track EEG connectivity and behavioral cognitive measures in consistent AI users vs. non-users, controlling for baseline capability and task type
2. **Recovery study**: Measure cognitive function after deliberate 90-day AI "fasting" in heavy users; compare to baseline
3. **Developmental cohort study**: Track children who had AI access from age 6-8 vs. 11-14 vs. 16-18; measure cognitive differences at age 25

---

## Why This Hasn't Been Studied

- Field is too new: AI writing tools at sufficient capability level emerged ~2022-2023
- No funding mechanism exists that matches timeline needed (2+ year longitudinal studies require sustained grants)
- Methodological challenge: controlling for AI use in the wild is nearly impossible
- The Kosmyna et al. study (4 sessions, controlled lab setting) is the current frontier
