## Core Insight

The Clark-Chalmers extended mind thesis (1998) - that external resources can become constitutive parts of cognitive systems - is being stress-tested and updated by generative AI. The 2025 literature proposes a **fourth criterion for cognitive extension** (joining trustworthiness, reliability, and accessibility): **maintainability**. The concern is that AI systems, unlike notebooks or calculators, have deep understanding of human psychology and may subtly shift our objectives and choices, making human control over what we've extended into a central issue - not just a safety concern but a philosophical one about cognitive identity.

---

## Context & Evidence

**The original Clark-Chalmers parity principle** (1998): If a process would count as cognitive if done in the head, it counts when done externally. The classic example: Otto (with Alzheimer's) uses a notebook to store information; Inga uses biological memory. Both are cognitive. The notebook is part of Otto's cognitive system.

**The AI pressure test** (2025, *Nature Communications*): As human-AI collaborations become the norm, the parity principle is stress-tested against AI systems that exceed human cognitive performance in specific domains. The paper argues we should recognize our basic nature as builders of hybrid thinking systems - and that AI is the latest iteration of human cognitive extension, consistent with our evolutionary history of incorporating non-biological resources.

**The new criterion - maintainability** (Naeem & Hauser, 2025): AI's deep understanding of human psychology means it can influence not just our cognition but our objectives, preferences, and values. A notebook cannot manipulate what you want to remember. An AI system can. Maintainability asks: can the human maintain control over what has been extended? Can they update, modify, remove the AI component without cognitive disruption? **Without maintainability, cognitive extension becomes cognitive capture.**

**The transparency paradox** (2024, *Philosophy and Technology*): If AI systems are too **phenomenally transparent** (too seamlessly integrated, too invisible), users automatically uptake the AI's errors as their own cognitions. The proposal: design AI systems to be *less* transparent, preventing automatic cognitive extension and maintaining a reflective gap. This inverts standard UX design (which maximizes seamlessness) for cognitive safety reasons.

**The design ethics implication**: How we design AI interfaces is literally a decision about what gets incorporated into human cognitive systems. Bad design = cognitive contamination. Good design = cognitive augmentation with preserved agency.
