# Meaning Cannot Exist Inside a Brain - The Relational Nature of Significance

## Core Insight

Enactivism makes a claim that sharply divides it from all computational cognitive science: meaning is not a property of internal representations stored inside brains. Meaning is fundamentally **relational** - it exists in the history of interactions between an organism and its world. The word "apple" does not mean anything because of a neural pattern that maps to an internal concept. It means what it does because of enacted social practices and organism-environment interaction histories. Isolate the brain in a vat and there is no apple-meaning, only neural patterns without significance.

---

## Context & Evidence

**The computational alternative**: In traditional cognitive science, meaning is a property of internal representations. Neural patterns "represent" external objects or categories. Meaning is thus containable inside the skull - it is the relationship between mental representations and what they stand for.

**The enactivist critique**: This view inverts the correct relationship. Significance is not first internal and then projected onto the world. It is first relational and only then partially internalized through habit and memory. A hungry organism's interaction with an apple constitutes the apple's meaning as food. Change the organism's needs and the apple changes its meaning. The meaning was never "in" the organism - it was in the organism-situation coupling.

**2024-2025 evidence from participatory sense-making**: The enacted institutions framework (De Jaegher & Di Paolo, *Synthese* 2024) extends this claim to language and social institutions. Words mean what they do because of the enacted social practices in which they are embedded - not because of internal semantic representations. Institutions have meaning because they are patterns of participatory sense-making, not because individuals mentally represent institutional rules.

**Evidence from psychiatric applications**: The 2024-2025 enactive psychiatry literature argues mental illness is better understood as "a disturbance of the ecology of the brain" - disruption of meaning-making at the organism-environment interface, not merely a malfunction of internal information processing.

---

## Field Acceptance

**Widely accepted within enactivism**: This is core to autopoietic enactivism (Varela, Thompson, Maturana) and radical enactivism (Hutto & Myin).

**Contested across cognitive science**: Computationalism, predictive processing, and most mainstream cognitive neuroscience treat internal representations as the locus of meaning.

**The debate has not been resolved** - both camps claim empirical and philosophical advantages.
