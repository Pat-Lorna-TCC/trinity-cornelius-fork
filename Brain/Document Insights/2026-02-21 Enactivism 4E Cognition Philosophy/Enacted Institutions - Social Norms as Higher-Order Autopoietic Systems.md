## Core Insight

Institutions are not merely behavioral patterns or sets of rules that constrain individual cognition. They are **higher-level autonomous systems enacted by patterns of participatory sense-making** - problem domains that patterns of social interaction have brought forth and now sustain. An institution exists to maintain "letting be" - a balance between underdetermination and overdetermination of individual behavior by the collective. Because institutions are enacted cognitive systems, institutional dysfunction can be analyzed using the same tools as individual cognitive dysfunction.

---

## Context & Evidence

**Standard equilibrium theory vs. enactivist theory**: Standard institutional theory (economics, sociology) treats institutions as behavioral equilibria or as rule systems that constrain choices. These theories lack an adequate account of agency - they cannot explain why individuals participate in institutions or why social norms have motivational power.

**The enactivist alternative**: Institutions are brought forth through participatory sense-making (the enactive account of how meaning arises in social interaction). Once enacted, institutions achieve a form of higher-level autonomy - they are not reducible to the individuals participating in them. The institution is a problem-domain that generates its own norms, its own standards of health and pathology.

**The "letting be" principle**: Institutions maintain a productive balance:
- Too little determination = coordination failure (individuals cannot orient within the institution)
- Too much determination = oppression (individuals lose the autonomy required for genuine participation)
- Optimal: Normative structure that enables action without specifying it completely

**The motivational power of social norms**: Previous institutional theories could not explain why social norms motivate behavior without external enforcement. The enactivist account: norms motivate because they are constitutive of the sense-making practices through which agents understand themselves and their situations. Violating norms is not just breaking a rule - it is disrupting the sense-making framework within which the agent's actions have meaning.

**Canguilhem's health/pathology distinction applied to institutions**: Just as biological health is the capacity to create new norms in response to new conditions, institutional health is the capacity for normative flexibility - adapting norms in response to changing circumstances without dissolving the institutional identity. Institutional pathology is normative rigidity or collapse.
