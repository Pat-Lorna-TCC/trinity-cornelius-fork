# Social Practices Constitute Not Just Influence Cognitive Capacities

## Core Insight

The standard view treats social practices as external factors that influence cognitive development - shaping what you know, how you process information, and what schemas you apply. The enactivist view is ontologically stronger: certain cognitive capacities are **literally constituted by** participation in social practices. Without language, there is no abstract reasoning - not because language is a useful "tool" for reasoning, but because the capacity for certain reasoning **only exists as a form of languaging**. The capacity cannot be separated from the practice through which it is enacted.

---

## Context & Evidence

**The standard view (Influence Model)**: Social and cultural practices are external inputs to individual cognitive systems. The individual mind remains the unit of cognition. Culture shapes but does not constitute.

**The enactivist Constitution Claim**: Some cognitive capacities are not possessed by individuals who then participate in practices - they only exist in the participatory activity itself. This is not just a strong form of social constructivism; it is a claim about ontological constitution.

**Language as the core case**: The claim is not merely that language influences how we think (linguistic relativity - Sapir-Whorf hypothesis). It is that **some cognitive capacities only exist as forms of languaging** - the activity of using language in social interaction. Isolated from the practice, the capacity does not exist; it is not merely dormant.

**2024 enactivist linguistic relativity** (Rodríguez Jordá & Di Paolo): This paper proposes an enactivist account of linguistic relativity. The traditional debate is whether language influences thought (strong: determines; weak: biases). The enactivist account: language does not merely influence thought; certain forms of thought are modes of languaging - they are enacted in and through language use, not merely expressed by it. This is a constitutive claim, not a causal one.

**Social norms and motivational power** (2024 *Synthese*): The enacted institutions paper explains why social norms motivate behavior without external enforcement - because norms are constitutive of the sense-making practices through which agents understand their situations. Violating a norm is not just breaking a rule; it disrupts the sense-making framework within which the agent's actions have meaning. This explains the powerful phenomenology of social transgression: it feels wrong not just instrumentally but experientially.

**Implications for culture and cognition**: Cultural practices are not "settings" within which universal cognitive machinery operates. Different cultural practices constitute different cognitive capacities. The "universal human mind" with culturally variable inputs is not the right picture. There are genuinely different cognitive ecologies constituting genuinely different capacities.

## Field Acceptance

**Contested**: This is a strong constitutive claim that goes beyond most cultural psychology and sociolinguistics.

**Philosophically supported within enactivism**: The constitution claim follows from core enactivist premises.

**Partially supported by linguistic relativity research**: The empirical evidence for strong linguistic constitution of thought is debated.
