# Science Has a Blind Spot About Experience - Thompson Frank Gleiser

## Core Insight

Science has a "blind spot": it cannot account for the experience of doing science. The entire scientific method depends on human observation, measurement, and interpretation - all of which are experiential acts. But the scientific worldview that has emerged from this method treats experience as either an epiphenomenon (causally inert byproduct) or a complication to be explained away. This creates a self-undermining problem: the method depends on experience, but the worldview produced by the method cannot adequately account for the method's own foundation. Enactivism is part of the solution because it treats experience as cognitively constitutive, not peripheral.

---

## Context & Evidence

**The blind spot**: Science is performed by conscious, experiencing beings who use experience (perception, inference, communication) to build models of reality. The models science produces increasingly eliminate the role of experience in explaining reality. The blind spot is this gap: science cannot see its own experiential foundation without undermining its claim to objective, perspective-independent knowledge.

**Why this matters**: The hard problem of consciousness is not just a philosophical puzzle - it represents science's inability to account for the very activity through which science is conducted. As long as science cannot account for experience, it is using a tool it does not fully understand.

**The enactivist response**: Enactivism proposes that experience is not a peripheral add-on to cognitive science but constitutive of it. The organism-environment coupling that enactivism describes is already experiential - sense-making is experience. There is no purely objective standpoint from which to describe cognition, because description is itself a form of cognition.

**Thompson's trajectory**: This book represents the culmination of Thompson's project begun with Varela and Rosch in *The Embodied Mind* (1991). He argues, with cosmologist Gleiser and astrophysicist Frank, that the problem extends across all sciences - physics, cosmology, and biology all face versions of the same blind spot.

**Connection to "Shaky Groundlessness" critique** (2025): A phenomenological critique argues that enactivism itself faces this problem - it attempts to describe consciousness from within consciousness while claiming a natural-scientific standpoint. The "Shaky Groundlessness" paper argues enactivism lacks a proper distinction between natural and transcendental viewpoints, creating self-inconsistency.

## Field Acceptance

**Philosophically important**: The argument is rigorous and represents decades of work from serious cognitive scientists and philosophers.

**Contested by mainstream science**: The claim that science cannot ignore experience is accepted by philosophers of mind but resisted by empirical scientists who see it as reintroducing subjectivism.

**Not yet resolved**: Neither the problem nor the enactivist solution has reached consensus.
