# Critical 4E Cognitive Science - Oppressive Scaffolding Constitutes Cognition

## Core Insight

Standard 4E cognitive science celebrates how agent-environment interactions scaffold and extend cognition, making us smarter. Critical 4E asks: what happens when the scaffolding is **oppressive**? The answer is deeply unsettling: oppressive scaffolding does not merely prevent action or block access - it **constitutes (forms) cognitive capacities**. A disabling environment does not just constrain a person's existing cognition; it shapes the cognitive capacities that person has. The environment is not a neutral background that cognition occurs against - it is partly what cognition is.

---

## Context & Evidence

**The standard 4E view**: Cognitive tools, social practices, and environments extend and scaffold cognition positively. The standard examples are the notebook, the smartphone, the chess clock - external resources that become part of the cognitive system.

**The critical extension**: Toro, Rietveld & Kiverstein (2025) introduce the concept of **material oppression**: ableism structures the sociomaterial environment to fit able-bodied persons and exclude others, leading to pervasive experiences of "I-cannot" and, in severe cases, to **pathological embodiment** - where a person cannot enact activist affordances (the ability to resist, reorganize, advocate) due to perceived risk of catastrophic reaction.

**The disability paradigm shift**: Van Grunsven (2025) argues that a quasi-Cartesian picture of the human body shapes our sociotechnical niches in an ableist manner. Disability is not primarily a property of a person's brain or body - it is a property of a **person-environment system**. This is stronger than the standard social model of disability: because the environment is cognitively constitutive (not just constraining), a disabling environment does not just prevent action - it cognitively forms the person within it.

**Empirical application**: Albright et al. (2025) applied this framework to counseling: Black persons with disabilities see fewer affordances and have reduced enactive ability due to oppressive systems. The intersectional experience of racism and ableism creates a systematically reduced affordance field - the person's cognitive capacities are literally diminished by their environment.

**The political dimension**: Cognition is political. The distribution of affordances is a justice issue. The cognitive niche is not neutral - it reflects and reproduces power relations. This makes environmental redesign not just a disability accommodation but a **cognitive justice issue**.
