# Probability Weighting Is an Inference Problem Not a Fixed Characteristic

## Core Framework

The non-linear probability weighting function from Prospect Theory (people overweight low probabilities, underweight high probabilities) emerges naturally from noisy perceptual signal encoding rather than reflecting a fixed distortion in preference structure. Probability perception is better understood as an inference problem: the brain encodes lottery probabilities with limited precision, and the resulting perceptual noise generates behavioral patterns that look like systematic probability distortion.

**Field Acceptance**: Emerging - represents the computational neuroscience frontier of behavioral economics; not yet fully accepted mainstream, but theoretically grounded and generating testable predictions.

---

## Key Components of the Framework

**1. Efficient Coding Model (NBER, 2024)**:
- Optimal encoding of lottery rewards is S-shaped (matching the observed probability weighting function)
- Low-probability events are optimally oversampled (which explains overweighting of small probabilities - it's not irrational, it's efficient under uncertainty)
- Subjects who attach higher weight to low probabilities exhibit valuations more sensitive to probability changes in that range - confirming that probability perception is dynamic, not fixed
- **Testable prediction**: Probability weighting should vary with context, experience, and information environment

**2. Risk Perception as Noisy Signal Processing (JEEA, 2024)**:
- Risk attitudes are influenced by: anticipation, time pressure, experience, salience, and availability heuristics
- These influences are expected from a noisy-signal model, not from stable preference theory
- Non-linear probability weighting emerges from coarse perceptual aggregation

**3. Individual Mixture of Risk Models (CEAR, 2025)**:
- Individuals use mixtures of two or more risk preference models rather than being consistently characterized by any single model
- The extent of mixing varies across individuals
- Challenges the assumption of stable, unitary risk preferences
- **Implication**: A single measurement of someone's risk preferences may not predict future behavior because people switch between models

---

## Why This Matters

Traditional behavioral economics treats probability weighting as a stable feature of individuals (you have a specific probability weighting function that defines your risk personality). This computational neuroscience model says:

1. The same person's probability weighting can change with context, experience, and salience
2. It's not a personality trait - it's a property of the decision environment
3. The "overweighting of low probabilities" (why people buy lottery tickets AND insurance) is not irrational - it's the correct response to perceptual uncertainty about whether a low-probability event will occur
