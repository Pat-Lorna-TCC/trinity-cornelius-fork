# Soft Commitment Devices Outperform Hard Financial Commitments

**Source**: Derksen, L., Kerwin, J.T. et al. - "Healthcare Appointments as Commitment Devices" - PMC/IZA Discussion Paper - January 2025
**Document Type**: Randomized Controlled Trial (Field Experiment)
**Extracted By**: AI (document-insight-extractor agent)
**Extraction Date**: 2026-02-21
**Session**: 2026-02-21 Behavioral Economics Intertemporal Choice

## Core Insight

Healthcare appointments (soft commitment) more than doubled HIV testing rates (+16 percentage points, 140% increase), substantially outperforming hard financial commitment devices (staking ~50% of daily median income). Most men who took hard commitments lost their investments. Soft commitment devices are cheaper, achieve higher take-up rates, and deliver better outcomes - upending the standard assumption that financial stakes are needed for self-control interventions to work.

---

## Evidence

**Study Design**: Randomized Controlled Trial (RCT)
**Sample Size**: 1,232 high-risk men in Zomba, Malawi
**Key Results**:
- Appointments (soft commitment): +16 percentage points HIV testing rate (140% increase)
- Take-up rate for appointments: 65%
- Take-up rate for financial commitment: 51%
- Hard commitment device stake: USD $1.38 (~50% of median daily income)
- Most men who took hard commitments lost their investments (did not follow through)
- Cost per additional HIV test:
  - Appointment: $2.69
  - Hard commitment device: $3.01
  - Cash payment: $11.00
- Appointment treatment effect: 4x larger than average hard commitment; 54% larger than the most successful prior intervention in this literature
**Replication**: Large-sample RCT; published in peer-reviewed venue (PMC)
**Citation**: Derksen, L., Kerwin, J.T. et al. (2025). PMC/IZA Discussion Paper. https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12687577/

## Why Soft Beats Hard: The Mechanism

Appointments overcome commitment problems without the risk of commitment failure. The hard commitment device creates a double problem:
1. **Lower take-up**: People correctly anticipate they might not follow through
2. **Failure tax**: When they don't follow through, they lose money AND don't get the health benefit
3. **Substitution**: The financial stake occupies cognitive/emotional resources without delivering the scheduling benefit

The appointment provides:
- **Temporal anchoring**: A specific time is allocated, reducing planning friction
- **Social accountability**: Breaking an appointment feels different from just not showing up
- **No downside risk**: Take-up is easier because there is no financial forfeit for failure
- **Reminder integration**: Appointment systems naturally include reminder calls (which drove much of the effect)

## Broader Evidence on Commitment Devices

From the same research landscape:
- **Friction reduction** counteracts present bias more reliably than financial incentives in many contexts
- **Planning prompts** (implementation intentions): robust effectiveness across domains
- **Temptation bundling**: Pairing desired and undesired activities effectively counters delay
- **Lottery-based incentives**: Leverage probability overweighting to motivate behavior
- Physical activity interventions: behavioral economics approaches show short-term effects but no long-term effects

---

## Implication for Behavioral Economics Toolkit

The standard hierarchy assumed:
1. Hard financial commitments (most powerful - skin in the game)
2. Soft commitments (planning, implementation intentions)
3. Framing / nudges (weakest)

The Malawi evidence inverts positions 1 and 2 for real-world deployment:
1. Soft commitments with reminders (appointment-style)
2. Temptation bundling / planning prompts
3. Hard financial commitments (high failure risk, limited real-world advantage)

**Policy implication**: Healthcare, financial savings programs, and educational settings should invest in appointment infrastructure over penalty-based commitment programs.
