# Decision Fatigue Is Belief-Mediated Not Glucose-Depleted

**Source**: BFI Working Paper (2025): "A Timeline of Cognitive Costs in Decision Making" (BFI WP 2025-69); ScienceDirect (2024): "Self-Control and Limited Willpower: Current Status"; JAMA Network Open (2024): Physician prescribing patterns
**Document Type**: Theoretical Review; Empirical Field Study
**Extracted By**: AI (document-insight-extractor agent)
**Extraction Date**: 2026-02-21
**Session**: 2026-02-21 Behavioral Economics Intertemporal Choice

---

## Core Insight

Decision fatigue effects appear real in field settings (physicians prescribe more unnecessary antibiotics late in shifts; judges make worse decisions late in sessions), but the mechanism is not glucose depletion as originally proposed. Stanford research shows decision fatigue primarily affects those who believe willpower is a limited resource - making this a belief-mediated, not metabolic, phenomenon. The ego depletion paradigm as originally formulated has failed to replicate, but contextually-valid, mechanism-agnostic decision fatigue remains empirically documented.

---

## Evidence

**Replication Status**:
- 2016 Registered Replication Report (N = 2,000+, 23 labs): No reliable evidence for ego depletion
- However: Longer, stronger manipulations in 2024 studies show more reliable effects when properly powered
- 2024 ScienceDirect review argues earlier challenges "largely failed" and replicability "has now been well established" with improved methods

**Field Evidence (JAMA Network Open, 2024)**:
- Physicians more likely to prescribe unnecessary antibiotics later in clinical shifts
- This finding persists despite theoretical uncertainty about mechanism
- Field evidence is more credible than lab ego depletion paradigm

**Belief-Mediated Mechanism (Stanford research)**:
- Decision fatigue primarily affects people who believe willpower is a limited resource
- People who hold an implicit theory that willpower depletes will show fatigue; those who believe it is expandable do not
- **Implication**: The "depletion" effect is partially a self-fulfilling prophecy

**Cognitive Cost Timeline (BFI Working Paper, 2025)**:
- Directly models how cognitive costs accumulate across a sequence of decisions
- Neurobiological accounts fall into three camps: depletive (glucose - weakest evidence), accumulative, and limited bandwidth
- Glucose accounts have the weakest empirical support of the three

---

## Why This Matters

The original ego depletion model justified specific interventions: eat sugar, take breaks, schedule important decisions early in the day. The belief-mediated model suggests:

1. **Mindset interventions**: Teaching people that willpower is not a limited resource may buffer against decision fatigue
2. **Belief-targeted approaches**: Rather than glucose/rest, target the implicit theory of willpower
3. **Field validity**: The physician prescribing finding means decision fatigue has real consequences even without a clean lab replication of the mechanism

The cognitive cost timeline model is more granular: fatigue accumulates differently depending on decision type, difficulty, and sequencing.
