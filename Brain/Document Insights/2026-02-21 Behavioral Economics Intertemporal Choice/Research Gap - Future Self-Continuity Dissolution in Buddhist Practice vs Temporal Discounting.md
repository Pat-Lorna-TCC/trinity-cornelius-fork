# Research Gap: Future Self-Continuity Dissolution in Buddhist Practice vs Temporal Discounting

## The Gap

No research has directly examined whether Buddhist meditation practice - which systematically cultivates reduced identification with a persistent self (anatta/no-self) - affects temporal discounting rates, and whether this contradicts the finding that future self-continuity reduces discounting. This is a genuine paradox at the intersection of contemplative science and behavioral economics.

---

## Why This Matters

The 2024-2025 behavioral economics literature has established that future self-continuity reduces temporal discounting: feeling more like your future self makes you more patient.

The vault's Buddhism-Neuroscience hub has established that meditative practice cultivates a lived sense that the self is an illusion - practitioners explicitly reduce their identification with a persistent, continuous self.

If these findings are both true, they generate a paradox:
- Behavioral economics says: strengthen self-continuity → less discounting → better long-term decisions
- Contemplative neuroscience says: dissolve self → less suffering → more wisdom
- Both are associated with better long-term outcomes (financial patience and well-being respectively)

**Are they achieving the same endpoint via opposite routes?**
Or is the behavioral economics finding domain-limited (financial decisions) while Buddhist self-dissolution operates at a different level (hedonic wellbeing)?

---

## What We Know (Related Research)

**Behavioral Economics Side**:
- Strong future self-continuity reduces temporal discounting (Yang et al. 2024)
- Identity instability specifically predicts present bias (Ott et al. 2025)
- Age-progressed self-visualization reduces discounting (multiple studies)

**Contemplative Neuroscience Side**:
- Meditative practice deactivates Default Mode Network (self-referential processing)
- Buddhist training reduces self-continuity as a deliberate goal (anatta practice)
- Long-term meditators show reduced narrative self-attachment
- Flow states involve PFC deactivation - temporary "selflessness" - with peak performance

**Missing: The Intersection**:
- No studies have measured temporal discounting in long-term meditators controlling for self-continuity
- No studies have examined whether the Buddhist "no-self" experience is the same construct as the behavioral economics "weak future self-continuity"
- No studies have tested whether meditation training changes temporal discounting rates

---

## Hypothesized Resolution

**Hypothesis**: Buddhist self-dissolution and behavioral economics self-continuity may be operating at different levels of the self-construct:

- **Narrative self** (autobiographical continuity): Buddhist practice reduces this; this is what behavioral economics measures
- **Functional motivation** (what drives behavior): Buddhist practice may actually increase clarity about long-term values, increasing functional orientation toward the future even while reducing narrative attachment

If this resolution is correct, we would expect:
- Meditators to show lower narrative self-continuity scores
- But equal or greater patience in temporal discounting tasks
- The mechanism would be values-clarity rather than self-identification

---

## Research Needed

**Critical Studies**:
1. Cross-sectional study: Measure future self-continuity AND temporal discounting in long-term meditators vs. controls
2. Longitudinal study: Track temporal discounting rates through a meditation training intervention
3. Mechanism study: Manipulate narrative self vs. values-clarity independently to test whether both affect discounting in the same direction

**What This Would Clarify**:
- Whether self-continuity and self-dissolution can coexist (different levels of self)
- Whether Buddhist practice provides a non-identity route to temporal patience
- Whether the behavioral economics and contemplative science findings are truly paradoxical or merely operating on different aspects of selfhood
