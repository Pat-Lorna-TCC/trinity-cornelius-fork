# Hyperbolic Discounting as Cognitive Artifact Not Exotic Time Preference

## Core Insight

What behavioral economists have measured as "hyperbolic discounting" and "present bias" may be substantially a cognitive artifact of decision complexity - not a genuine exotic time preference. When people must mentally aggregate delays, reward magnitudes, and discount rates simultaneously, bounded cognition produces noisy valuations that exhibit apparent hyperbolicity even when underlying preferences may be approximately exponential.

---

## Evidence

**Study Design**: Controlled experimental study with manipulation of decision complexity
**Sample Size**: 500 subjects, 18,000 decisions
**Key Results**:
- Partial correlation between atemporal anomalies and intertemporal anomalies: r = 0.34, suggesting a shared cognitive mechanism drives both
- When researchers artificially increased problem complexity, hyperbolic patterns increased commensurately
- "Mirror" tasks where rewards were shrunk (not delayed) produced identical hyperbolic patterns - ruling out time preference as the mechanism
- Structural estimates of present bias are strongly correlated with cognitive uncertainty and choice inconsistencies
- People who are nervous about making mistakes are twice as likely to follow expert advice to be patient
**Replication**: Based on 18,000 decisions; large-scale within-study replication
**Citation**: Enke, B., Graeber, T., & Oprea, R. (2024). "Complexity and Hyperbolic Discounting." HBS Working Paper 24-048 / NBER w31047.

---

## Why This Is Paradigm-Shifting

The canonical interpretation of hyperbolic discounting treats it as a stable preference structure - a fundamental feature of human psychology where people genuinely weight the present more than an exponential discount function would predict. This has underpinned decades of behavioral economics interventions (commitment devices, pre-commitment mechanisms, automatic enrollment defaults).

The Enke/Graeber/Oprea finding suggests this entire edifice may be built on a cognitive illusion: when the task is hard, people approximate - and approximation produces hyperbolic-looking data. The implication is that measured "present bias" in many studies is spuriously inflated by cognitive load rather than reflecting genuine time preference.

**The crucial test**: Mirror tasks (where rewards shrink rather than are delayed) produce identical patterns. If genuine time preferences drove the effect, delay tasks should look different from shrinkage tasks. They do not.

---

## Intervention Implications

If hyperbolic discounting is largely cognitive noise, then:

1. **Simplifying decision environments** (fewer options, clearer time horizons, reduced complexity) may be more powerful than incentive-based interventions
2. **Commitment devices** targeting the wrong mechanism - people aren't genuinely impulsive, they're cognitively overwhelmed
3. **Financial literacy programs** may work partly by reducing cognitive complexity rather than changing time preferences
4. **Expert guidance** can substitute for patient choices - the "nervous about mistakes" finding suggests people will behave patiently when uncertainty is removed
