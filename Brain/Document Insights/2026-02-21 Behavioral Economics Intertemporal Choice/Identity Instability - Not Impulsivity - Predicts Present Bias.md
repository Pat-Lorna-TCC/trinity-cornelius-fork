# Identity Instability - Not Impulsivity - Predicts Present Bias

**Source**: Ott et al. (2025) - "Identity Instability and Temporal Discounting in BPD Features" - Personality Science; Yang et al. (2024) - "The Effect of Future Self-Continuity on Intertemporal Decision Making" - Frontiers in Psychology
**Document Type**: Empirical Research / Clinical Psychology
**Extracted By**: AI (document-insight-extractor agent)
**Extraction Date**: 2026-02-21
**Session**: 2026-02-21 Behavioral Economics Intertemporal Choice

---

## Core Insight

Among 9 diagnostic features of borderline personality disorder, only identity instability was associated with higher temporal discounting rates - not impulsivity, not emotional dysregulation, not any other feature. This specificity is extraordinary: the mechanism connecting psychological disorder to impatience is identity incoherence across time, not the general trait of impulsivity that economists and psychologists typically assume.

Separately, high future self-continuity (feeling connected to one's future self) directly reduces temporal discount rates. Individuals with weak future self-continuity pursue immediate gratification because the "self" that will receive the delayed reward feels like a stranger.

---

## Evidence

**Study 1 (Ott et al., 2025)**:
**Study Design**: Clinical trait analysis examining which BPD features predict temporal discounting
**Key Result**: Among 9 BPD features, only identity instability predicted higher discounting rates. Impulsivity did not have a specific effect when identity instability was accounted for.
**Citation**: Ott et al. (2025). Personality Science. https://grinbandlab.org/wp-content/uploads/2025/09/ott_2025.pdf

**Study 2 (Yang et al., 2024)**:
**Study Design**: Experimental study manipulating future self-continuity
**Key Results**: High future self-continuity directly reduces temporal discount rates; weak self-continuity = future self as stranger
**Citation**: Yang et al. (2024). Frontiers in Psychology. https://doi.org/10.3389/fpsyg.2024.1437065

**Study 3 (ScienceDirect, 2025)**:
Factors beyond mere temporal connectedness - including perceived uncertainty about future self and overall temporal focus - modulate this relationship, making self-continuity a richer construct than simple "how similar will I be?"

**Age Evidence (Yi Lu, GSA 2024)**: Both past and future self-continuity increase with age. Older adults show stronger self-continuity and less discounting.

**Intervention Validation**: Age-progressed renderings of participants (increasing perceived self-continuity) decrease temporal discounting - a directly actionable intervention.

---

## Why This Is Paradigm-Shifting

Classical behavioral economics models treat present bias as driven by impulsivity, steep exponential discounting, or the cognitive mechanisms described by Enke et al. The identity-discounting connection opens a completely different causal pathway: **present bias is partly an identity problem, not a willpower or cognitive problem**.

This reframes behavioral economics interventions entirely:
- Traditional approach: Use commitment devices to constrain future choices
- Identity approach: Help people develop coherent self-narratives across time

The finding that it is specifically identity instability (not impulsivity) from the BPD cluster is particularly diagnostic - it rules out the simple "more impulsive = more present bias" account.
