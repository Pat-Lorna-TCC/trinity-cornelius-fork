## The Synthesized Insight

The 2024-2025 behavioral economics literature has produced convergent evidence for a single meta-pattern: the canonical behavioral anomalies (hyperbolic discounting, loss aversion, probability weighting) may be substantially cognitive/perceptual artifacts generated by bounded cognition under complexity - not stable preference structures. Multiple independent research programs are reaching this conclusion from different directions simultaneously.

**Confidence Level: High** - Based on multiple independent high-quality empirical papers converging on the same interpretation; the synthesis itself is speculative, but the individual pillars are well-supported.

## Why the Convergence Is Significant

These findings come from different research groups, different methods, and different phenomena. Yet they all reach the same conclusion: behavioral anomalies are not exotic preference structures - they are artifacts of:

1. **Bounded cognition under complexity** (hyperbolic discounting)
2. **Methodological artifacts from lab-based, unrepresentative sampling** (loss aversion)
3. **Noisy perceptual signal encoding** (probability weighting)
4. **Publication bias masking near-zero effects** (nudge effectiveness)

This convergence is methodologically diverse - it includes RCTs, meta-analyses, computational neuroscience modeling, and representative population surveys. When independent methods converge on the same revision to the standard theory, the signal is likely real.

## Implications for Intervention Design

If behavioral anomalies are cognitive artifacts, interventions should target:

1. **Cognitive environment design**: Simplify decision environments rather than incentivize behavior change (removes the cognitive noise that produces hyperbolic behavior)
2. **Framing heterogeneity**: Stop assuming universal loss aversion; some people respond better to gain framing
3. **Realistic effect size expectations**: Plan for near-zero effects at scale, not lab-grade effects
4. **Perceptual salience management**: Probability weighting is context-sensitive, so manipulate context

## Testable Predictions

**If this synthesis is correct, we would expect:**
1. Behavioral anomalies should be stronger in cognitively demanding situations and weaker in simple ones
2. Laboratory-measured loss aversion should not predict real-world financial behavior in representative samples
3. Nudge effectiveness should correlate with baseline cognitive load (more effective when load is low, target population is pre-motivated)
4. Interventions that simplify decisions should outperform incentive-based interventions

**Falsification criteria:**
- If hyperbolic discounting persists in maximally simplified intertemporal choice tasks
- If loss aversion is found at λ ≈ 2.0 in pre-registered, representative, unordered symmetric studies
