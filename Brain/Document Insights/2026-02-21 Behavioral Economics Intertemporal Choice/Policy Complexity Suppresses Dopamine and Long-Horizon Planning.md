## Core Insight

Dopamine responses to reward outcomes are suppressed by policy complexity - the complexity of the decision environment reduces the brain's ability to generate robust reward prediction error signals. This directly links the computational neuroscience of dopamine to the cognitive complexity account of hyperbolic discounting: complex decision environments don't just make the math harder - they suppress the neurochemical signals that encode the value of long-horizon planning.

---

## Evidence

**Study Design**: Experimental neuroscience with measurement of dopamine responses
**Key Results**:
- Dopamine responses to reward outcomes were suppressed when the decision policy was complex
- This is consistent with reinforcement learning models - complex policies produce noisier reward signals
- The finding directly connects to Enke et al.'s cognitive complexity work on intertemporal choice

## The Complexity-Simplicity Design Principle

If complexity suppresses the dopamine signals needed for long-horizon planning, then:

**For organizations**: Complex corporate incentive structures may produce present-biased behavior not because people are impulsive, but because the complexity of connecting current behavior to future reward suppresses the dopamine signal that would otherwise motivate long-horizon action.

**For policy**: Benefit programs with complex eligibility requirements don't just create administrative burden - they neurochemically undermine the capacity for future-oriented decision-making that would be needed to successfully navigate them.

**For personal finance**: Complex investment products (even optimal ones) may produce worse long-term behavior than simple sub-optimal products because the complexity suppresses the dopamine reinforcement needed to maintain the behavior.
