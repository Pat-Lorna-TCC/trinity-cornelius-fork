## Core Insight

Loss aversion is not stable - it intensifies dramatically under conditions of scarcity and stress. A real-world competitive industry study found a baseline loss aversion coefficient of λ = 4.2, rising to λ = 9.5 on slow days with smaller management teams. This context-dependence means loss aversion is not a fixed personality trait but a dynamic, state-dependent response that peaks when resources feel scarce.

---

## Evidence

**Industry Study (Journal of Risk and Uncertainty, 2025)**:
**Study Design**: Real-world competitive industry (natural field experiment)
**Key Results**:
- Baseline loss aversion coefficient: λ = 4.2 (substantially higher than laboratory λ ≈ 2.0)
- Under scarcity conditions (slow business days + smaller management teams): λ = 9.5
- The 2.25x amplification from scarcity/stress is the key finding
**Citation**: Journal of Risk and Uncertainty (2025)

## Reconciling Contradictory Loss Aversion Evidence

This finding helps reconcile the contradictory 2024-2025 literature:

1. **Chapman et al. (representative population)**: ~50% of US adults are loss tolerant - measured under normal conditions with normal stakes
2. **Brown et al. meta-analysis**: λ ≈ 2.0 across lab studies - measured in artificial lab settings, often with student samples under low-stress conditions
3. **Industry field study**: λ = 4.2-9.5 under real competitive pressure - measured under genuine stakes with real consequences

**The reconciliation**: Laboratory measurements underestimate loss aversion for people under real-world stakes and stress, while at the same time population surveys show that loss aversion is not universal. The resolution is:
- Loss aversion is context-dependent and stress-dependent
- It is not universal (50% are loss tolerant at baseline)
- But among those who are loss averse, it intensifies dramatically under scarcity/stress conditions

## Implications

**For financial decision-making**: The moments when good financial decisions are most needed (income stress, market downturns, business slowdowns) are exactly when loss aversion is most distorting
**For health behavior**: Scarcity creates a vicious cycle - resource constraints trigger loss framing, which impairs decision quality, which compounds resource constraints
**For organizational design**: Small management teams under business stress may make systematically worse risk-management decisions due to amplified loss aversion
