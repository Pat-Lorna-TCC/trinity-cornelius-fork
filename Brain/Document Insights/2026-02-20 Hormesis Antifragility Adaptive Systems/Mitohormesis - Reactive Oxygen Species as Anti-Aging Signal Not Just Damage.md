## Core Insight

The Free Radical Theory of Aging (FRTA) - the dominant 20th century aging model - is being fundamentally revised. While HIGH levels of reactive oxygen species (ROS) do accelerate aging and cause damage, LOW-TO-MODERATE levels of mitochondria-derived ROS function as **anti-aging signaling molecules** that trigger protective adaptations. This is mitohormesis: the biphasic dose-response in which mitochondrial stress at appropriate doses extends lifespan rather than shortening it.

The direct implication: antioxidant megadosing may be **counterproductive** by blunting the very signals that activate longevity pathways.

---

## Evidence

**The Biphasic ROS Dose-Response:**
- High ROS: oxidative damage, protein oxidation, DNA damage, accelerated aging (what FRTA described)
- Low-moderate ROS: activation of NRF2 antioxidant response, heat shock proteins, autophagy, ATF4, FGF21 signaling → net lifespan extension

**Key Molecular Mediators of Mitohormesis Benefits:**
| Mediator | Function | Anti-Aging Role |
|---------|----------|----------------|
| ATF4 | Transcription factor activated by mitochondrial stress | Activates stress response genes |
| FGF21 | Fibroblast growth factor, elevated by exercise + protein restriction | Lifespan extension in animal models |
| Mitochondrial heat shock proteins | Protein quality control | Maintain mitochondrial function under stress |

**Pharmacological Confirmation (C. elegans)**:
- MCU inhibitor mitoxantrone extends lifespan in aged nematodes
- Transiently elevates protective antioxidant enzymes in human cells
- Translational relevance suggested but not yet proven in humans

**Field Acceptance**: Widely established across multiple experimental systems; FRTA revision is mainstream in aging biology as of 2024

## The Antioxidant Supplement Problem

This finding challenges the popular health narrative around antioxidant supplementation:

**Popular belief**: More antioxidants = less oxidative damage = slower aging

**Mitohormesis finding**: Moderate ROS from exercise and metabolic stress ARE the signal to activate protective longevity pathways. Taking high-dose antioxidants (Vitamin C, Vitamin E, NAC) after exercise may blunt these protective signals.

**What the evidence shows:**
- Supplementing with antioxidants after exercise can reduce the fitness gains from exercise in multiple studies
- Antioxidant megadosing interferes with the mitohormetic signaling that exercise was designed to trigger
- The body's own antioxidant response (SOD, catalase, GPx) - triggered by the ROS signal - is better calibrated than exogenous antioxidant flooding

**"Exercise is medicine" now has a precise mechanistic explanation**: Exercise works partly because it generates moderate ROS in a recoverable, calibrated way that activates mitohormetic longevity pathways.

---

## The CACH Model Integration

Mitohormesis is a cellular implementation of the CACH (Catabolic-Anabolic Cycling) model:
- **Catabolic phase**: Exercise → mitochondrial ROS production → catabolic stress signals
- **Anabolic phase**: Rest/recovery → ROS levels normalize → mitohormetic pathways activated → NRF2, ATF4, FGF21 signaling → mitochondrial biogenesis, autophagy, anti-aging gene expression

The "anti-aging" effects of exercise, caloric restriction, and intermittent fasting converge on mitohormesis as a shared mechanism.
