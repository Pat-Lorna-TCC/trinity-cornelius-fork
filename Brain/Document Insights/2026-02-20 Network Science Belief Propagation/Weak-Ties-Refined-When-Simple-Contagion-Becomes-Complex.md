## Core Framework

Granovetter's 1973 "Strength of Weak Ties" theory (~70,000 citations) has been empirically validated but now requires a critical qualification: **weak ties accelerate simple contagion but actually slow complex contagion.** The original theory was correct within its domain (job mobility, novel information). It is wrong when applied to belief adoption, behavior change, and identity-linked information spread.

The unifying principle: **Read the original theory conditionally - "weak ties are strong *for simple information transmission*."**

---

## Where Weak Ties Work (Validated 2024)

**Job Mobility (LinkedIn Megastudy)**:
- Large-scale LinkedIn study confirmed the basic theory with a crucial nonlinear refinement
- **Inverted U-shape**: Moderately weak ties (not the weakest) increase job mobility most
- Strongest ties have the least effect on job mobility
- Weakest ties (very loose acquaintances) are less effective than moderately weak ties
- This refines, but validates, the core insight

**Novel Information Spread**:
- Weak ties bridge distinct clusters, providing access to non-redundant information
- First complete empirical verification achieved by resolving the "forbidden triad" paradox (Cambridge Network Science, 2024)
- The theory holds when the core assumptions (tie bridges between distinct clusters) are empirically met

---

## Where Weak Ties Fail - The Critical Boundary

**For Complex Contagion (Belief Change, Behavior Adoption)**:
- Complex contagion requires *reinforcement* from multiple independent sources
- Weak ties connect people to different clusters where they lack reinforcing connections
- A receiver exposed to a belief through a weak tie gets no follow-up reinforcement
- The belief enters an isolated context without the cluster density needed for adoption
- **Result: Weak ties SLOW diffusion of beliefs that require social reinforcement**

**For Online Social Support**:
- Studies find strong ties valued for ALL types of support, including informational support
- Weak ties are not uniquely superior even for novel information in digital contexts
- The network topology assumption (weak ties = bridges between distinct clusters) often breaks down online

**Cambridge Network Science 2024**: The structural advantage implied by weak ties "may not necessarily stand" when the core assumptions are not empirically met.

---

## The Unified Decision Rule for Network Strategy

| Contagion Type | Optimal Network | Tie Strength | Examples |
|----------------|-----------------|--------------|---------|
| Simple | Random, small-world | Weak to moderate | News, rumors, product awareness, facts |
| Complex | Clustered, modular | Strong, within-cluster | Belief change, behavior adoption, political affiliation, identity shifts |

**Practical implication**: Bridging campaigns (connecting people across network clusters) are appropriate for information spread but may actively impede belief change campaigns. For changing deeply held beliefs, you need to work within dense clusters, not bridge across them.

---

## Counter-Intuitive Extension

This means the rhetoric of "building bridges" and "creating connections across divides" is appropriate for information sharing but may be counterproductive for belief change. Dense communities with strong internal ties are the *required medium* for complex contagion. The ideal pathway for changing someone's political beliefs or health behaviors runs through their close community, not through exposure to diverse outside perspectives.
