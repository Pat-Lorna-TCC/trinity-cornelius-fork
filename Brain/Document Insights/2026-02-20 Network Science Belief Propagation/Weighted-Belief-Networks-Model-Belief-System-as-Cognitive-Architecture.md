# Weighted Belief Networks - A Model of Belief System as Cognitive Architecture

**Source**: Aiyappa et al. "Emergence of simple and complex contagion dynamics from weighted belief networks." *Science Advances*, April 2024. https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/sciadv.adh4439

## Core Framework

The Aiyappa et al. model provides a formal representation of what the vault has described intuitively as "belief systems" and "identity as a set of beliefs." In this framework:

- **Individual belief systems** are represented as **weighted networks** of concepts (nodes) connected by beliefs about relationships between concepts (edges)
- The **coherence** of a belief system is a structural property: how well-connected and mutually consistent the belief network is
- A **stable belief system** is one with high coherence - many interconnected, mutually reinforcing beliefs
- An **unstable belief system** has lower coherence - fewer connections, more isolated beliefs

---

## What This Model Adds to the Vault's Framework

The vault already has: "Identity is a set of beliefs about the world" and the Uncertainty-Dopamine-Belief Loop. This model provides the structural mechanism beneath those insights:

**Why beliefs cohere**: Belief networks develop coherence through reinforcement - beliefs that are consistent with each other are both more likely to be held and more likely to survive challenges, because they provide mutual support. The network structure IS the coherence.

**Why changing one belief is hard**: Changing a belief in a coherent network requires updating all the edges connected to that belief node. Each connected belief provides resistance because it would also need to change to maintain coherence. This is the structural basis for the dopamine-withdrawal mechanism the vault describes.

**Why some beliefs are harder to change than others**: Beliefs that are highly connected in the belief network (hubs of the belief network) are harder to change because they have more connections requiring updating. Political and religious identity beliefs are structurally central - many other beliefs connect through them.

## Implications for Belief Change Strategy

**Targeting peripheral beliefs** (low-connectivity in belief network):
- These are weakly connected to other beliefs
- Changing them does not threaten the coherent core
- Change is relatively low-cost (simple contagion dynamics)
- Example: Changing a belief about a specific fact that is not identity-linked

**Targeting central beliefs** (high-connectivity in belief network):
- These are connected to many other beliefs
- Changing them destabilizes the entire network
- Maximum resistance (complex contagion dynamics)
- Example: Changing a core identity belief (political affiliation, religious conviction)

**The wedge strategy**: Creating internal inconsistencies in a belief network may be more effective than direct challenges. If two beliefs in the same coherent network can be shown to contradict each other, the resolution requires updating at least one - creating an opening that doesn't exist when challenging from outside the network.
