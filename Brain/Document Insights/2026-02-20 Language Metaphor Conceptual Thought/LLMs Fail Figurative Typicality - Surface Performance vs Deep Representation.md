# LLMs Fail Figurative Typicality - Surface Performance vs Deep Representation

**Source**: "Can LLMs Interpret Figurative Language as Humans Do?: Surface-level vs. Representational Similarity." arXiv:2601.09041, January 2026.

## Core Insight

Although LLMs can form broad conceptual categories resembling human ones, they fail to capture **fine-grained figurative distinctions** - specifically, typicality effects in figurative language. Humans automatically treat some metaphors as "better examples" of a metaphorical category than others (typicality effects). LLMs do not exhibit these effects, suggesting their "understanding" of metaphor is achieved through statistical pattern matching rather than genuine conceptual mapping.

**Surface-level performance does not equal representational alignment.**

---

## Evidence

**January 2026 arXiv finding**: LLMs fail to capture typicality effects in figurative language. LLMs report inconsistent metaphor comprehension under syntactic manipulations - fragile internal representation.

**The diagnostic test**: If LLMs truly possessed human-like conceptual metaphor representations, they should show the same typicality effects humans show (some metaphors being "better examples" of a metaphorical category). They don't.

**Companion evidence (October 2025, arXiv:2510.04120)**: Current research lacks standardized evaluation metrics, making quantification of LLM metaphor performance across contexts difficult - the field itself cannot yet measure what it needs to measure.

---

## What Typicality Effects Reveal About Human Cognition

Typicality effects in conceptual structure reflect the **graded nature of sensorimotor experiences** that anchor abstract concepts. Humans don't experience all instances of "temperature" equally - warmth has a different experiential quality than hot, which is different from burning. These graded sensorimotor experiences create typicality gradients in abstract conceptual categories.

LLMs lack bodies. They lack the graded sensorimotor experiences that create typicality. Their "understanding" of conceptual metaphors is explicit knowledge (like consulting a grammar rule) rather than implicit competence (like speaking grammatically without knowing the rules).

**This is the signature of embodiment in cognition**: typicality effects are where the body's presence shows up in the mind's structure.

---

## The Explicit vs. Implicit Knowledge Distinction

The [[CMT Prompting Paradigm - Conceptual Metaphor Theory Improves LLM Reasoning]] finding and this finding together define an important boundary:

- **CMT prompting improves LLM reasoning**: when explicitly provided with source-to-target domain mappings, LLMs can reason more effectively (explicit knowledge triggered)
- **LLMs fail typicality**: without explicit prompting, LLMs cannot produce the automatic, graded responses that characterize genuine conceptual metaphor comprehension (implicit competence absent)

LLMs have explicit access to conceptual metaphor *knowledge* but lack the implicit *competence* that emerges from embodied experience.

---

## Implications for AI Capabilities Assessment

Aggregate agreement metrics (LLMs vs. humans on metaphor interpretation) mask failures at fine-grained levels. **The AI field has been measuring the wrong thing**. High accuracy on metaphor interpretation tasks through statistical pattern matching does not demonstrate human-like understanding.

This has implications beyond metaphor research:
- AI capabilities in any domain involving graded, embodied experience (emotion recognition, moral judgment, aesthetic evaluation) may be surface-performance without deep representational alignment
- Assessment methods that rely on aggregate accuracy rather than typicality and graded response patterns will systematically overestimate AI comprehension
