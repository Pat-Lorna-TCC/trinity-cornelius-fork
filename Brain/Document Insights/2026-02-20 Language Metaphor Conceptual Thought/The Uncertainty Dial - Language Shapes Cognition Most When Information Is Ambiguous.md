# The Uncertainty Dial - Language Shapes Cognition Most When Information Is Ambiguous

**Source**: "The Sapir-Whorf hypothesis and inference under uncertainty." LC Lab Berkeley (preprint). + Vaishnav (2025), "Thinking Through Language: Revisiting Linguistic Relativity in Cross-Cultural Communication." SSRN.

## Core Framework

Language effects on cognition are not constant - they are modulated by uncertainty. When sensory or contextual information is rich and unambiguous, language has minimal impact on thought. When information is ambiguous, incomplete, or emotionally charged, **linguistic framing becomes the dominant cognitive organizer**.

This resolves decades of inconsistent Whorfian evidence: the key variable is task uncertainty, not domain.

---

## Context and Acceptance

**Field Acceptance**: Emerging consensus - provides resolution to a 70-year empirical controversy
**Supporting Evidence**:
- Cross-linguistic spatial reasoning: Guugu Yimithirr (absolute directional) vs. English speakers (egocentric) - effects strongest in high-ambiguity navigation tasks
- Color categorical perception effects are strongest at category boundaries where discrimination is uncertain
- Qu & Miwa (2024, Cognitive Linguistics): Japanese speakers prioritize animacy over agency; English speakers prioritize agency - reflecting structural language differences in event encoding

**Key Empirical Patterns**:
- When information is clear and certain: language effects on cognition are minimal
- When information is ambiguous: language "fills in the gaps" and becomes the dominant organizer
- Emotional contexts heighten language effects (uncertainty about one's own emotional state)

---

## The Mechanism

Language does not override cognition - it functions as a **cognitive prosthetic** for uncertainty. The brain uses available information to construct meaning; when non-linguistic information is sparse, linguistic frames provide the scaffolding.

This is consistent with predictive coding: the brain is constantly generating predictions. When sensory evidence is strong, predictions are updated toward sensory data. When sensory evidence is weak, prior beliefs (including linguistic priors) dominate.

---

## Critical Implication: Where Framing Effects Will Be Strongest

This framework predicts framing effects will be most powerful in precisely the domains where humans most need cognitive support:
- Novel technologies (high uncertainty)
- Complex policy choices
- Health decisions
- Interpersonal conflict
- AI systems (high novelty, high uncertainty)

The domains where linguistic framing matters MOST are the domains where careful reasoning is MOST needed. This is not a coincidence - it is a fundamental property of how language relates to cognition.
