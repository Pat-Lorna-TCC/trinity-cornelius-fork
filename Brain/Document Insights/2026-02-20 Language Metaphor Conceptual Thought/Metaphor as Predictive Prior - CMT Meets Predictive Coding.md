## The Synthesis

Integrating Conceptual Metaphor Theory with predictive coding neuroscience yields a powerful unified framework: **metaphors are prior probability distributions** that the brain uses to predict upcoming information.

When a domain is framed as "battle," the brain pre-activates the battle schema, generating predictions about adversaries, tactics, victory conditions, casualties, and urgency. Incoming information is then processed *through* these predictions, with non-matching information generating prediction error and matching information being assimilated smoothly.

---

## Evidence Base for Each Component

**Predictive Coding Component** (confirmed research):
- Noureddine & Kuperberg (2024): The N400 (brain's semantic surprise signal) is best explained as Bayesian prediction error - language comprehension *is* Bayesian inference
- eLife 2024: Lifelong language experience shapes the brain's anticipatory mechanism at fundamental neural levels - language calibrates what the brain predicts
- SCIRP 2024: Explicit mapping of Predictive Processing Theory onto Conceptual Metaphor Theory, proposing metaphors function as prior beliefs in the predictive coding framework

**CMT Component** (theoretical framework, widely accepted):
- Kövecses (2025): Metaphors operate at hierarchical levels; changing one level cascades through the system
- Harris et al. (2024): Dead metaphors activate sensorimotor schemas at 250ms - consistent with automatic prior activation

**The Integration**: If metaphors are priors, then:
1. Metaphors generate predictions about incoming information
2. Matching information is processed with low effort (confirmation)
3. Non-matching information generates prediction error (surprise, resistance)
4. The brain is not "reading" language passively - it is testing hypotheses generated by activated metaphorical schemas

---

## Why Metaphors Are Both Cognitively Efficient and Cognitively Dangerous

**Efficient**: Priors enable fast, low-effort processing when incoming information matches the activated schema. This is why metaphors are universally useful - they provide rich predictive frameworks.

**Dangerous**: Strong priors suppress perception of information that doesn't fit. The brain does not simply receive information and then interpret it - it predicts what information should look like, and information that violates predictions is both harder to perceive and harder to integrate.

This explains:
- Why changing someone's conceptual frame is so cognitively disruptive (Kövecses 2025: requires restructuring across multiple hierarchical levels)
- Why metaphors can constrain scientific discovery (the MIT "paths of abstraction" research)
- Why framing effects persist even when people are aware of them (priors operate preconsciously at 250ms)
