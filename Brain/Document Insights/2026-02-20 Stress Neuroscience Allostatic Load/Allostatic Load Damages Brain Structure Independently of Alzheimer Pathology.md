# Allostatic Load Damages Brain Structure Independently of Alzheimer Pathology

**Source**: Palix et al. (2025). "Allostatic load, a measure of cumulative physiological stress, impairs brain structure but not beta-amyloid accumulation in older adults: an exploratory study." *Frontiers in Aging Neuroscience*, 17, 1508677. ## Core Insight

Allostatic load (AL) is negatively associated with **gray matter volume and white matter integrity** in frontal and temporal regions - and with poorer attentional performance - but shows **no association with beta-amyloid accumulation**. This critical distinction means stress-induced brain aging and Alzheimer's disease pathology are separate processes: stress primarily ages the brain structurally (gray/white matter, connectivity) rather than driving amyloid plaques.

---

## Evidence

**Study Design**: Multi-modal neuroimaging (structural MRI + PET for amyloid) + allostatic load calculation + cognitive testing
**Sample**: N=111 cognitively normal older adults (aged 65+)
**Allostatic Load Calculation**: 18 biomarkers (most comprehensive AL composite in a neuroimaging study to date)
**AL Biomarkers Include**: Noradrenaline, epinephrine, HbA1c, cortisol, CRP, HDL, fibrinogen, BMI + others
**Key Results**:
- AL negatively correlated with: total brain volume, white matter integrity, gray matter volume in frontal/temporal regions
- AL negatively correlated with: attentional performance on cognitive battery
- AL showed: **NO association with beta-amyloid accumulation** (PET-measured)
**Novel contribution**: First multi-modal study directly comparing AL vs amyloid in same cohort; first to distinguish these pathways in cognitively normal adults
**Citation**: Palix et al. 2025, *Frontiers in Aging Neuroscience*

---

## Why This Distinction Matters

**Two distinct brain aging trajectories:**

1. **Allostatic Load pathway:**
   - Mechanism: chronic cortisol → HPA dysregulation → neuroinflammation → structural damage
   - Regions affected: frontal and temporal gray matter, white matter integrity
   - Clinical outcome: attentional deficits, structural brain aging
   - Dementia type: Potentially vascular dementia, cognitive reserve depletion
   - **Modifiable through**: stress reduction, allostatic load management

2. **Alzheimer's pathway:**
   - Mechanism: amyloid-beta and tau accumulation → neurodegeneration
   - Separate process from stress-induced structural aging
   - **NOT modifiable through stress management**

**Clinical implication:** Stress reduction interventions (mindfulness, exercise, sleep) may prevent structural brain aging and cognitive decline WITHOUT affecting amyloid burden. These are complementary - not competing - prevention strategies.
