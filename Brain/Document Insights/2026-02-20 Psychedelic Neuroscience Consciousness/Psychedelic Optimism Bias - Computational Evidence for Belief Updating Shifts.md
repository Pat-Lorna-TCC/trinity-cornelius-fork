# Psychedelic Optimism Bias - Computational Evidence for Belief Updating Shifts

**Source**: "Psilocybin increases optimistic engagement over time: computational modelling of behaviour in rats." Translational Psychiatry (Nature), 2024. https://www.nature.com/articles/s41398-024-03103-7

## Core Insight

Using computational modeling of animal behavior, psilocybin was shown to modify **forgetting rates** (how quickly outdated beliefs decay) and **reduce loss aversion** (the tendency to weight losses more heavily than gains). This suggests psilocybin creates an **optimism bias** through altered belief updating - not by changing emotional valence directly, but by changing the computational parameters governing how beliefs about outcomes are updated over time.

---

## Evidence

**Study Design**: Computational modeling of reward-learning behavior in rats following psilocybin vs. placebo
**Key Results**:
- Psilocybin modified **forgetting rates** - old negative beliefs decayed faster
- Psilocybin reduced **loss aversion** - negative outcomes weighted less heavily in future predictions
- Net effect: optimism bias emerging from altered belief updating parameters (not direct hedonic shift)
- Translational potential for depression and anxiety characterized by pessimism bias

**Limitations**: Animal study - cannot confirm phenomenological aspects or applicability to human identity-level beliefs; computational models require validation

---

## Why This Matters Beyond Rodents

The computational finding is significant because it characterizes the MECHANISM of optimism bias with precision:

**Standard hedonic shift hypothesis**: "Psilocybin makes people feel better, so they're more optimistic"
**Computational evidence**: "Psilocybin changes the rate at which past negative information is weighted in forming future predictions"

This is qualitatively different - and more interesting. It means psilocybin doesn't just suppress negative affect; it changes the **forgetting/updating parameters** of the belief formation system itself.

In human terms:
- Normal depression cognition: Past negative experiences have high persistence in prediction models ("it always goes wrong for me")
- Post-psilocybin cognition: Past negative experiences decay faster from the model; new positive information is integrated more readily
- This is the computational implementation of REBAS at the reward-learning level
