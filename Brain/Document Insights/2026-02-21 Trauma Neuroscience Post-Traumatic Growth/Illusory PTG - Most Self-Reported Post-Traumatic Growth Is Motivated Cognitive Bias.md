# Illusory PTG - Most Self-Reported Post-Traumatic Growth Is Motivated Cognitive Bias

**Source**: Jayawickreme E, et al. "Illusory posttraumatic growth is common, but genuine posttraumatic growth is rare: A critical review and suggestions for a path forward." *Clinical Psychology Review*. 2023/2024. https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0272735823000594

## Core Insight

Over 50% of trauma survivors report moderate or greater post-traumatic growth (PTG) on self-report measures - but prospective longitudinal studies with objective behavioral outcomes show genuine PTG is rare. The dominant measurement tool (PTGI) has design flaws that inflate reports: it uses only positive wording and a scale that bars reporting deterioration, creating implicit social pressure to report growth. Most reported PTG is motivated cognitive bias - meaning it reflects what survivors need to believe rather than what has demonstrably changed.

---

## Evidence

**Evidence Level**: Critical review + meta-analytic findings
**Key Evidence**:
- >50% of trauma survivors report moderate or greater PTG on self-report PTGI
- Prospective longitudinal studies with **behavioral outcomes** show genuine PTG is rare
- The PTG-PTSD paradox: PTG is highest when PTSD is **moderate** (curvilinear relationship)
- PTG is **positively correlated** with PTSD symptoms - paradoxically, those reporting most growth also report most symptoms
- 2025 systematic review identified heterogeneous PTG trajectories: stable, increasing, decreasing, constructive, illusory, distressed, struggling, resistant PTG

**PTGI Measurement Flaws**:
1. Exclusively positive wording in all items
2. Response scale has "no change" as minimum (not "got worse") - structurally bars reporting deterioration
3. Demand characteristics: implicit social pressure to report growth
4. Retrospective bias: comparing current to remembered pre-trauma self (both of which are constructed, not recorded)
5. Cultural narrative pressure: Western culture expects trauma to produce transformation

**The PTG-PTSD Paradox Explained**:
- Moderate PTSD creates enough distress to motivate growth-seeking without being so severe as to prevent it
- High PTG scores alongside high PTSD scores suggest co-occurring distress and positive meaning-making, not resolution

**Citation**: Jayawickreme et al. 2023/2024

## Five Mechanisms for Illusory PTG

1. **Measurement design flaws** - PTGI wording structure inflates reports
2. **Emotional bias** - trauma survivors are emotionally motivated to find meaning
3. **Cultural narratives** - Western cultures expect trauma to be transformative
4. **Memory distortion** - retrospective self-assessment compares to a remembered (distorted) pre-trauma baseline
5. **Social expectations** - admitting suffering without growth feels like failure

These five mechanisms are not independent - they form a mutually reinforcing system that makes illusory PTG psychologically compelling and socially rewarded.

## What Genuine PTG May Look Like

The field's emerging consensus on genuine vs. illusory PTG:

**Genuine PTG markers**:
- Behavioral changes observable by others, not just self-report
- Longitudinal stability (not just immediate post-trauma flourishing narrative)
- Accompanied by, not opposed to, willingness to acknowledge ongoing distress
- Lower on positive illusions about pre-trauma self
- Produced through active processing, not avoidance of trauma content

**Illusory PTG markers**:
- High self-report, low behavioral change
- Correlated with high PTSD symptoms (unresolved distress seeking narrative)
- Defensively positive, rejecting acknowledgment of ongoing distress
- Resolves on retrospective measures but not prospective ones
