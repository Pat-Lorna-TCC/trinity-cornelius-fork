# Temporal Discounting as Complexity Response Not Time Preference (HYPOTHESIS)

**STATUS: HYPOTHESIS - THEORETICAL REINTERPRETATION, NOT CONFIRMED MECHANISM**

## Hypothesis

Apparent hyperbolic time preferences (the tendency to discount the future more steeply than classical economics predicts) may not reflect genuine preferences about time itself - they may instead reflect **cognitive responses to the complexity of intertemporal tradeoffs**. When people face complex decisions that require comparing present and future values, they deploy "noisy and heuristic procedures" that mimic hyperbolic discounting patterns. What looks like "present bias" may partly be **cognitive overload**.

---

## Rationale

**Why This Might Be True**:
- Intertemporal choices require mentally simulating future states, estimating future preferences, and comparing across temporal contexts - all cognitively demanding
- Heuristic strategies (simplifying complex comparisons) would systematically favor the concrete present over the abstract future
- Hyperbolic discounting's characteristic "present bias" is most pronounced in complex tradeoffs - consistent with cognitive load explanation
- Supporting evidence: temporal discounting is linked to the "inattention-impulsivity-overactivity" symptom dimension (npj Mental Health Research, 2024, N=731) - suggesting cognitive capacity moderates discounting

**Consistency with Existing Knowledge**:
- Connects to vault's existing insight: Mental discounting might be caused by Dopamine cycles - offers a complementary (not competing) explanation. The dopamine mechanism may be ONE source of present bias; cognitive complexity may be another
- Consistent with behavioral economics observation that heuristics and biases are often adaptations to real cognitive constraints

**Confidence Level**: Medium - The theoretical argument is coherent and has supporting circumstantial evidence, but direct experimental tests isolating complexity from genuine time preferences are limited

---

## Why This is a Paradigm Shift

The conventional view treats temporal discounting as a **preference** - people genuinely want immediate rewards more than future ones. The complexity hypothesis treats it as an **artifact** - people appear to want immediate rewards because calculating intertemporal tradeoffs is hard.

**If true, this has major intervention implications**: Reducing cognitive load during intertemporal choices (through clearer framing, simplified comparisons, decision aids) might reduce apparent present bias more effectively than interventions targeting time preferences directly.

---

## Testable Predictions

If this hypothesis is correct, we would expect:
1. Present bias should decrease when intertemporal choices are simplified (fewer options, clearer presentation)
2. Individuals with higher cognitive capacity (working memory, fluid intelligence) should show less hyperbolic discounting, holding other factors constant
3. Environmental stressors that increase cognitive load (noise, time pressure, emotional arousal) should increase apparent discounting steepness
4. Interventions that reduce complexity (decision aids, simplified choice architecture) should have larger effects on discounting than interventions targeting preferences

**Falsification Criteria**:
- Present bias persists even under maximally simplified intertemporal choices that eliminate cognitive load
- Neural evidence shows clear preference encoding for immediate rewards independent of complexity processing
