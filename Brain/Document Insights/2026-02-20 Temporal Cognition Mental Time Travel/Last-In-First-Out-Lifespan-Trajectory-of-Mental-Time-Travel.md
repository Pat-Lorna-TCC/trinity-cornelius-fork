# Last In First Out - Mental Time Travel Follows Evolutionary Lifespan Trajectory

**Source**: Buzi G, Eustache F, Droit-Volet S, Desaunay P, Hinault T et al. - "Towards a Neurodevelopmental Cognitive Perspective of Temporal Processing" - Communications Biology, 7, Article 987 - August 14, 2024 - Open Access - DOI: 10.1038/s42003-024-06641-4

## Core Insight

Temporal processing abilities develop across the human lifespan following the first unified neurodevelopmental model, and they decline with aging according to a **"last in, first out" hypothesis**: the most recently evolved cognitive abilities (like mental time travel and episodic future thinking) are the first to deteriorate with aging, while the most primitive timing capacities (rhythmic processing) persist longest.

This has a striking implication: mental time travel - our most distinctively human temporal capacity - is simultaneously the most recently evolved, the last to develop in childhood, and the first to be lost in aging.

---

## Evidence

**Study Design**: Comprehensive review synthesizing developmental neuroscience from prenatal to senescence
**Key Evidence**:
- Temporal processing follows non-linear development, concurrent with brain maturation
- "Last in, first out" pattern: most recently evolved abilities first to deteriorate
- Age-related changes in episodic memory and future thinking reduce vividness AND subjective temporal distance
- "A reduced subjective feeling of temporal distance" is consistently reported with increasing age

**Developmental Milestones**:
- **Prenatal**: Basic rhythmic and duration processing
- **Infancy/Early childhood**: Emergence of order and sequence processing
- **Middle childhood**: Working memory supporting duration judgments
- **Adolescence**: Refinement of MTT and future-oriented planning
- **Aging**: Systematic changes in time perception, MTT vividness, and temporal distance estimation

**Clinical Application**:
- Time processing proposed as a potentially sensitive **biomarker for cognitive aging** and early neurodegeneration
- MTT assessment could serve as early marker for cognitive decline BEFORE other symptoms appear

---

## Evolutionary Interpretation

The "last in, first out" pattern suggests a hierarchy of temporal cognition:
1. **Most ancient (most robust)**: Rhythmic processing, duration perception - shared with many species
2. **Intermediate**: Sequential ordering - present in many animals
3. **Most recent (most fragile)**: Mental time travel, episodic future thinking - uniquely or predominantly human

This is consistent with the finding (Philosophical Transactions of the Royal Society B, 2024) that mental time travel and episodic memory are NOT synonymous - MTT is a "later evolutionary innovation" built on top of more primitive episodic memory mechanisms.
