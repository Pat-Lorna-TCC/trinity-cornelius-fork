## Core Insight

The prefrontal cortex serves as a mental simulator, using the hippocampus's cognitive map to mentally "roll out" imagined action sequences before executing them. This mechanism is validated at three independent levels of analysis simultaneously: computational model behavior, human maze navigation data, and rodent hippocampal replay recordings. The same planning-through-imagination algorithm operates across species and scales.

---

## Evidence

**Study Design**: Dual-validation using (1) human online maze navigation with deliberation times tracked, and (2) rodent hippocampal replay recordings during physical maze navigation
**Key Results**:
- The computational agent's policy rollouts closely resemble rodent hippocampal replay patterns
- In humans, deliberation time before each maze step correlated with intensity of neural activity in PFC and hippocampus
- The model learned to plan specifically when *beneficial* to do so - explaining variability in human thinking times
- Behavioral flexibility: could rapidly adapt to new environments by simulating detours before executing them

**Citation**: Mattar et al. (2024), *Nature Neuroscience*

## The "Rollout" Mechanism

When facing a decision point, the brain:
1. Activates the hippocampal cognitive map (spatial/conceptual)
2. Recruits PFC to run forward simulations through that map
3. Evaluates outcomes of simulated paths
4. Selects action based on simulation results
5. Executes - deliberation time reflects simulation depth

**Critical implication**: Planning IS imagination. They are the same computational process.

## Three-Level Consilience

This study achieves something rare - validated the same mechanism across three levels:

| Level | Evidence |
|-------|----------|
| Computational | Model predicts behavior with high fidelity |

This is not analogy. It is the same algorithm expressed in different substrates.
