## Core Insight

Motor imagery (imagining movements without performing them) improves learning not just of the imagined action, but of sequentially linked, physically different movements. The benefits of imagination transfer across motor sequences. Additionally, expertise reverses the neural efficiency pattern: novices show lower activation for simple imagined movements, while experts show lower activation for simple AND higher activation for complex imagined movements - revealing that expertise shapes what gets simulated, not just whether simulation occurs.

---

## Evidence

**Study 1 (Gippert et al. 2025, PNAS)**:
- Design: Hybrid motor sequences where some movements were imagined and others were physically executed
- Finding: Motor imagery of prior reaching movements enabled motor learning in an established motor adaptation paradigm
- Transfer: Benefits extended to linked *different* movements - not just the imagined ones
- Neural: Alpha and beta band synchronization during motor imagery correlated with subsequent motor adaptation
- Mechanism: Different cortical layer involvement may explain why imagined vs. overt prior movements produce different (but both measurable) adaptation effects

**Study 2 (Expert soccer players, fNIRS, PNAS 2025)**:
- Expert athletes show LOWER cortical activation than novices for simple imagined actions (neural efficiency)
- Expert athletes show GREATER activation than novices for complex imagined actions (richer simulation)
- Expertise shapes the *content* of what is simulated, not just whether simulation occurs

---

## The Transfer Effect - Why It Matters

Standard motor imagery research shows that imagining an action improves that action. This study goes further:

**Before this finding**: Imagine action X → improve performance of action X
**After this finding**: Imagine action X → improve performance of action Y (if Y is linked to X in a sequence)

This suggests motor imagery works by updating general motor adaptation mechanisms, not just action-specific representations. The brain's learning from imagery is more general than previously understood.

---

## The Expertise Paradox

| Condition | Novices | Experts |
|-----------|---------|---------|
| Simple imagined action | Higher activation (building basic representation) | LOWER activation (efficient automaticity) |
| Complex imagined action | Lower activation (can't fully represent complexity) | HIGHER activation (rich detailed simulation) |

**Implication**: Expert mental practice is qualitatively different from novice mental practice. Experts simulate more - and in more detail - for complex scenarios. The recommendation "use mental imagery" needs calibration: beginners need to develop the basic motor representation before imagery is effective; experts leverage imagery most powerfully for complex novel variations.
