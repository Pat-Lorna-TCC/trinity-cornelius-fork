## Core Framework

The hippocampus's primary function is not storing memories - it is constructing spatially coherent scenes. This reframing (Scene Construction Theory, updated 2025) explains why hippocampal damage impairs memory, future imagination, counterfactual reasoning, and spatial navigation simultaneously: they all require scene construction. The hippocampus builds the spatial scaffolding within which any mental event - past, future, or imagined - is set.

## Two Types of Mental Imagery - Key Distinction

The 2025 MDPI paper distinguishes critically:

**Object imagery**: Imagining individual objects - does NOT require full hippocampal engagement
**Scene imagery**: Imagining 3D environments one could "step into" - REQUIRES hippocampal spatial scaffolding

This explains why hippocampal patients can still imagine isolated objects but cannot construct navigable mental spaces.

---

## The vmPFC-Hippocampus-Precuneus Network

2025 7T fMRI with Dynamic Causal Modeling reveals the functional architecture:

**During autobiographical memory retrieval**:
- vmPFC drives TOP-DOWN modulation of precuneus
- Temporal and self-relevant structuring is the primary demand

**During scene construction**:
- Hippocampus plays a MORE INDEPENDENT role
- Bidirectional connectivity to both vmPFC and precuneus
- Neither structure dominates

**Key finding**: The *direction* of effective connectivity determines whether a mental event is coded as memory (vmPFC-driven) vs. imagination (hippocampus-driven). Memory and imagination are not different processes - they are the same scene construction system with different driver hierarchies.

---

## Unifying Insight: Four Capacities, One Mechanism

Hippocampal damage impairs all four of these:
1. Episodic memory (past scene recall)
2. Future imagination (future scene construction)
3. Counterfactual reasoning (alternative scene simulation)
4. Spatial navigation (real-time scene building)

They are not separate faculties with shared neural real estate. They are the same capacity - scene construction - applied to different temporal/modal contexts.
