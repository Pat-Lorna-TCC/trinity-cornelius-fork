## Core Framework

Herbert Simon's foundational 1971 assumption - that information abundance necessarily creates attention scarcity because attention is a unified, invariant resource - is challenged by contemporary attention science. Attention is not a single resource allocated by a central "CPU-like allocator" but a **distributed competition between neural subsystems**, shaped by three temporal layers of priority. This shifts the economics of attention from a simple scarcity model to a context-dependent competition model.

## Simon's Two Challenged Assumptions

1. **Central allocator**: Attention is allocated by a unified controller (like a CPU)
2. **Unified invariant resource**: Attention is one resource required equally for all cognitive tasks

**Bruineberg's Critique**: These are artifacts of 20th-century computational metaphors. Contemporary attention science shows:
- Attention is a distributed competition between neural subsystems
- "Top-down" and "bottom-up" processes are temporal expressions of the same underlying mechanism (not separate systems competing for resources)
- Attention scarcity is context-dependent, not invariant

---

## The Tripartite Temporal Framework (Annual Review of Psychology, 2025)

Attention is shaped by three temporal layers, not a single allocator:
1. **Evolutionary imperatives** - ancient sensory priorities (threat detection, novelty)
2. **Learned experiences** - statistical regularities from history
3. **Immediate task demands** - current goals

Selection history (the second layer) is a distinct influence operating through implicit statistical learning - controllable proactively, not just through top-down goals.

---

## Second Wave Attention Economics

A dual-stream model (Oxford, 2025) distinguishes:
- **Calcified attention**: habituated, low-effort, low-quality (scrolling, passive consumption)
- **Flow attention**: engaged, high-quality, intrinsically motivated

Proposal: Attention could function as a "symbolic currency" - potentially playing the role in the cognitive economy that money plays in the material economy.

## Why This Matters for the Knowledge Base

The existing note Attention as Universal Selection Pressure - From Media to AI uses the scarcity framing effectively. This new framework adds a critical nuance:
- Scarcity is real in practice (contextual) but not an inherent biological constant
- The better frame is: **attention as a competition that can be won or lost** depending on which temporal layer is activated
- Technologies exploit ancient evolutionary imperatives (Layer 1) against modern goal-directed attention (Layer 3)
