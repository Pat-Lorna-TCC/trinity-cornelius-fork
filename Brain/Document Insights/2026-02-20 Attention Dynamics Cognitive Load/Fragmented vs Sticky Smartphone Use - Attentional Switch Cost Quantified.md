# Fragmented vs Sticky Smartphone Use - Attentional Switch Cost Quantified

**Source**: Siebers T., Beyens I., Valkenburg P.M. "The effects of fragmented and sticky smartphone use on distraction and task delay." *Communication Research*, SAGE, 2024. ## Core Insight

**Fragmented** smartphone use (many brief sessions) causes significantly greater attentional disruption than **"sticky"** use (fewer, longer sessions) - even at the same total screen time. The reason: fragmented use maximizes the number of costly attentional switches, each of which carries a ~23-minute re-focus penalty. Critically, simply disabling notifications does NOT solve this problem - the checking habit is deeply ingrained and requires structural environment design.

---

## Evidence

**Fragmented vs. Sticky Study (Siebers et al., 2024)**:
- Fragmented use = multiple brief smartphone sessions throughout the day
- Sticky use = fewer, longer sessions
- Fragmented use causes greater attentional disruption than sticky use at equivalent total time
- Mechanism: Each switch creates an "attentional residue" that lingers for up to 23 minutes

**Notification Intervention Failure (2024 Journal of Communication, n=205)**:
- RCT testing notification-disabling intervention
- Did NOT significantly reduce screen time or checking frequency
- Did NOT improve perceived control, productivity, or distraction
- DID reduce perceived checking habit strength (marginally more intentional use)
- Conclusion: The checking behavior is habituated, not notification-driven

**Digital Reading Meta-analysis (Frontiers in Psychology, 2025)**:
- Consistent attentional interference effects across studies
- Hyperlinks and notifications are primary disruptors of reading comprehension
- Sustained reading attention is specifically vulnerable to digital interruption design

---

## Key Insight: Habit vs. Trigger

The notification study reveals a critical distinction:
- **Conventional assumption**: Notifications trigger phone checking → disable notifications → reduce checking
- **Reality**: Checking is habituated independent of notifications → disabling notifications reduces habit salience but not behavior

This mirrors the Dopamine model of habit formation: the dopamine reward loop is established, and the cue (habit impulse) fires automatically even when the external trigger is removed.

---

## Structural Implications

Since surface interventions (notification disabling) don't work, effective solutions require environmental restructuring:
1. **Default-off notification architectures** (organizational policy, not individual choice)
2. **Scheduled "sticky" sessions** vs. allowed-anytime fragmented access
3. **Physical separation** of high-distraction devices from deep work environments
4. **Chronotype-aligned deep work blocks** with device-free periods
