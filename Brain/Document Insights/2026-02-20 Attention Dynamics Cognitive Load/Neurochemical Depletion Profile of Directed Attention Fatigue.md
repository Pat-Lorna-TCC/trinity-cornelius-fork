## Core Insight

Directed attention fatigue involves the depletion of a specific trio of neurotransmitters: **dopamine, serotonin, and acetylcholine**. Each depletion produces qualitatively different subjective and cognitive effects, which explains why "cognitive fatigue" feels different from physical tiredness - and why different recovery strategies address different depletion types. Crucially, **acetylcholine** serves as the brain's "attention broadcaster" via ascending brainstem projections, making its depletion the direct mechanism of attention loss (not dopamine, which affects motivation rather than attention directly).

---

## Evidence

**Neurotransmitter Roles in Attention**:

1. **Dopamine** (D1/D2 receptor balance in PFC):
   - Modulates prefrontal cortex fine-tuning
   - Drives motivation and effort allocation (NOT attention directly - per Cell 2025)
   - Depletion: Reduced motivation for effortful tasks, impaired cognitive flexibility

2. **Norepinephrine** (frontoparietal arousal network):
   - Activates frontoparietal arousal network
   - Mediates novelty-driven attention capture
   - Exploration-exploitation trade-off regulation
   - Depletion: Reduced alertness, impaired novelty detection

3. **Acetylcholine** (ascending brainstem projections):
   - Acts as "brain-wide attention broadcaster"
   - Ascending projections from basal forebrain modulate cortical attention
   - The most direct neurotransmitter for sustained attention maintenance
   - Depletion: Impaired sustained attention, reduced focus capacity

**Subjective Experience of Depletion**: "Impaired motivation, attention, and cognitive flexibility" - each neurotransmitter explains a different component of the fatigue experience

**Source**: Brain Communications 2025 meta-analysis citing thalamo-cortical-striatal modulation; PMC 2024 review

## Why Fatigue Feels Different from Tiredness

Physical tiredness = primarily metabolic depletion
Directed attention fatigue = specific neurochemical depletion + network-level inhibition shift

The neurochemical profile explains subjective fatigue phenomenology:
- "I can't concentrate" = acetylcholine depletion
- "I don't care" = dopamine depletion
- "Everything feels hard" = dopamine + serotonin combined depletion
- "I can't stay alert" = norepinephrine depletion

---

## Recovery Implications

Different restoration strategies target different depletion types:
- **Nature/movement**: May restore all three (via thalamic-loop reset)
- **Mindfulness**: May specifically restore acetylcholine-mediated sustained attention (via LC-NA system modulation)
- **Sleep**: Restores all three via homeostatic neurochemical replenishment
- **Novelty/stimulation**: Temporarily compensates via norepinephrine spike (not true restoration)
