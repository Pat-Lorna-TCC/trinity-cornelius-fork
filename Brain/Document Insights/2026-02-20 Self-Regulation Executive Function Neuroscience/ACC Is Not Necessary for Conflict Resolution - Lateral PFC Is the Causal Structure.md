# ACC Is Not Necessary for Conflict Resolution - Lateral PFC Is the Causal Structure

**Source**: "Cognitive Control and the Anterior Cingulate Cortex: Necessity and Coherence", May 2024, NeuroImage. DOI: 10.1016/j.neuroimage.2024.00323
**Document Type**: Lesion-Mapping Study (causal)
**Extracted By**: AI (document-insight-extractor agent)
**Extraction Date**: 2026-02-20
**Session**: 2026-02-20 Self-Regulation Executive Function Neuroscience

## Core Insight

After 30 years of fMRI studies linking the anterior cingulate cortex (ACC) to conflict monitoring and cognitive control, a lesion-mapping study in 176 patients with focal brain injuries found that Stroop task performance - the gold standard test of conflict resolution - depends on the integrity of left **lateral** frontal regions, NOT medial frontal cortex (ACC). ACC activation in fMRI studies may be correlational, not causal. The causal structure of cognitive control may be lateral, not medial.

---

## Evidence

**Study Design**: Lesion-mapping approach (voxel-based lesion-symptom mapping, VLSM)
**Sample**: 176 patients with focal brain injury
**Task**: Stroop task - the standard paradigm for cognitive conflict monitoring
**Key Finding**: Stroop interference was sensitive to integrity of **left lateral frontal regions** (lateral PFC), NOT medial frontal cortex (ACC)

**Why This Challenges 30 Years of Theory**:
- Hundreds of fMRI studies show ACC activation during Stroop/conflict tasks
- The Conflict Monitoring Theory (Botvinick et al., 2001) was built around ACC as the conflict detector
- Brain stimulation interventions targeting ACC for self-control enhancement (tDCS, rTMS) are based on this theory
- VLSM studies provide causal (lesion = missing function) rather than correlational evidence

**Caveat**: This finding needs replication in larger lesion cohorts with diverse conflict tasks beyond Stroop.

---

## Why This Matters

### The Correlational Trap in Neuroimaging

This finding is a striking example of a broader methodological problem: fMRI activation ≠ necessity. A region can activate during a task without being causally required for the task. ACC may be:
- An epiphenomenal correlate of conflict processing
- A monitoring/signaling region that broadcasts but doesn't implement conflict resolution
- Relevant for conflict *detection* but not conflict *resolution* (lateral PFC handles resolution)

The research report's own synthesis clarifies this: "ACC does not directly suppress thoughts - it functions as a conflict detector that recruits PFC control." The actual inhibitory work is done downstream.

### Implications for Brain Stimulation Interventions

tDCS and TMS studies targeting ACC for cognitive enhancement may be targeting the wrong region. If lateral PFC is the causal structure, stimulation protocols should target left lateral frontal regions for conflict resolution improvements. This invalidates a substantial body of applied neuroscience.
