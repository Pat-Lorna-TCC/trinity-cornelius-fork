# Ternary Reputation Systems Outperform Binary - Justified Defection Should Be Neutral

**Source**: "Gradual reputation dynamics evolve and sustain cooperation in indirect reciprocity", Yamamoto, Okada, and Suzuki, PLOS One, August 2025. DOI: https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0329742
**Document Type**: Research Paper
**Extracted By**: AI (document-insight-extractor agent)
**Extraction Date**: 2026-02-20

## Core Insight

Binary (good/bad) reputation systems, which have dominated indirect reciprocity theory for two decades, are systematically inferior to ternary (good/neutral/bad) systems with gradual updates. The critical insight: defecting against a "bad" person should leave your reputation unchanged (neutral outcome), not penalize you. Binary systems punish people for appropriately sanctioning defectors, creating a perverse incentive that undermines cooperative norm enforcement.

---

## Evidence

**Study Design**: Laboratory scenario experiments + evolutionary game theory simulations
**Key Comparison**: Binary reputation (good/bad) vs. ternary reputation (good/neutral/bad) with gradual updating
**Finding**: Ternary systems with gradual dynamics are both more realistic and more effective at sustaining cooperation
**Prior Literature**: Challenges the "leading eight" norms that have dominated the field since the 2000s

---

## The Problem with Binary Reputation

In binary systems:
- Cooperating with a good person = your reputation improves
- Defecting against a good person = your reputation drops
- But: Defecting against a bad person ALSO drops your reputation

This penalizes justified sanctioning. If you refuse to do business with a known cheater, binary systems treat this as "defection" - hurting your own reputation. This creates a disincentive for appropriate norm enforcement, which weakens the entire cooperative system.

## The Ternary Solution

In ternary systems:
- Defecting against a "bad" actor = reputation unchanged (neutral)
- Cooperating with a "good" actor = reputation improves
- Cooperating with a "bad" actor (unnecessary charity) = debatable

This "contextual amnesty" for justified defection mirrors how human social judgment actually works: we don't think less of someone for refusing to help a known thief. Binary reputation systems are a simplification that fails to capture this moral nuance.

---

## Counterintuitive Implication

Classic efficiency arguments suggest simpler mechanisms are more evolutionarily robust. This finding inverts that: more cognitively demanding (three-level) assessments outperform simpler (binary) ones. Cognitive sophistication in moral assessment is not overhead - it is functionally necessary for cooperation at scale.

---

## Implications for Platform and Community Design

- **Five-star ratings vs. thumbs up/down**: Five-star systems may be closer to the optimal ternary structure than binary like/dislike
- **Content moderation**: Users who report bad actors should not be penalized; their moderation actions should be reputation-neutral
- **Performance reviews**: Good/bad binary reviews may systematically undermine cooperation; graded assessments that recognize legitimate pushback may work better
- **Community banning**: Platforms where reporting abuse hurts the reporter's standing have an architecturally broken reputation system
