## Hypothesis

The 2024-2025 wave of cooperation science research collectively points toward a unified framework for designing human-AI systems: the core challenge of deploying AI is fundamentally a multi-player cooperation problem, and game-theoretic mechanism design provides a more robust foundation for AI alignment than value learning alone.

Specifically, the convergence of findings suggests:
1. AI agents that model human intent (Bayesian Reciprocator style) cooperate better than those that only observe behavior
2. The game structure (mechanism design) matters more than individual agent values for systemic cooperation outcomes
3. Initial conditions dominate mechanism selection (multistability finding)
4. Cultural and linguistic context shapes cooperation at least as much as architecture

## Testable Predictions

**If this synthesis is correct, we would expect**:
1. Multi-agent AI systems designed with explicit mechanism design outperform those designed around individual agent alignment, even when the individual agents have stronger value alignment
2. Human-AI teams with explicit perspective-taking protocols (SCoT-like) will outperform those without, even when the AI has superior individual capability
3. AI systems deployed into already-cooperative human environments (high initial cooperation) will succeed more readily than those deployed into low-cooperation environments, regardless of mechanism design quality
4. The gap in cooperation between individualist and collectivist cultural contexts will predict AI adoption rates better than capability metrics

**Falsification Criteria**:
- If value-aligned AI systems consistently outperform mechanism-designed systems in cooperative tasks
- If initial cooperation level has no predictive power over AI deployment success
- If cultural context explains less variance in human-AI cooperation than capability metrics
