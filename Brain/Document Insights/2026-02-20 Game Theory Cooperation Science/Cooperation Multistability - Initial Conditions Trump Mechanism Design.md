# Cooperation Multistability - Initial Conditions Trump Mechanism Design

**Source**: "Coevolutionary dynamics of cooperation, risk, and cost in collective risk games", PLOS Computational Biology, 2025/2026. DOI: https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pcbi.1013512

## Core Insight

The three-way coevolution of cooperation rate, collective risk perception, and cooperation cost creates a multistable system where both full defection and cooperative equilibria are stable attractors. Which equilibrium a population reaches depends critically on initial conditions - not primarily on which mechanism is used. This means the question "which mechanism should we use to promote cooperation?" is often less important than "what is the current cooperation level, and how do we bootstrap it?"

---

## Evidence

**Study Design**: Mathematical analysis + simulation of coevolutionary feedback dynamics (cooperation x risk x cost)
**Key Finding**: Multiple stable equilibria exist - system is multistable
**Worst Case**: Full defection + maximum risk + highest cost is a stable attractor
**Best Case**: Non-zero cooperation equilibria also exist as stable attractors
**Critical Variable**: Initial cooperation level determines which attractor the system reaches

---

## Why This Is Counterintuitive

Standard mechanism design thinking: choose the right incentives and cooperation will emerge. The multistability finding reframes this: in many real-world cooperation problems, the mechanism matters less than the starting point. The same incentive mechanism can lead to completely different outcomes depending on whether you're in the basin of attraction of the cooperative equilibrium or the defective one.

**Key Implication**: Organizations that are already predominantly defective cannot be rescued by better incentive design alone. They need a phase transition - a discontinuous jump to a new attractor. This requires bootstrapping initial cooperation above the tipping threshold, not just improving mechanisms.

---

## The Coevolution Dynamic

The three variables are interdependent:
- As cooperation increases → risk perception decreases (the problem seems solved)
- As risk perception decreases → free-rider temptation increases
- As free-riding increases → cooperation decreases
- **This creates a cycle that can collapse cooperation even after it has been established**

Conversely:
- High cooperation → reduced actual risk → signals cooperation works → reinforces cooperation
- Low cooperation → high actual risk → signals cooperation matters → may bootstrap more cooperation

The system can tip in either direction depending on which feedback loop dominates.

---

## Implications

1. **Bootstrapping over mechanism design**: Culture change requires first creating islands of cooperation that are large enough to tip the system, not just deploying better incentives into a defective culture
2. **Cooperation maintenance**: Cooperative systems can collapse not because incentives change but because success itself reduces perceived risk, which allows free-riding to creep in
3. **Context-dependent interventions**: The right intervention depends on current cooperation level; punishment works when cooperation is already moderate; nudging works when cooperation is high; structural change is needed when cooperation is near zero
