# Unconditional Samaritan AI Promotes More Cooperation Than Discriminatory AI

**Source**: "Emergence of cooperation in the one-shot Prisoner's dilemma through Discriminatory and Samaritan AIs", Journal of the Royal Society Interface, 2024. DOI: https://doi.org/10.1098/rsif.2024.0212

## Core Insight

In one-shot Prisoner's Dilemma games, an AI that helps everyone unconditionally ("Samaritan AI") promotes more overall cooperation in the human population than an AI that selectively helps only cooperators ("Discriminatory AI"). This is counterintuitive because it contradicts the classical prediction that conditional cooperation (which embeds implicit punishment of defectors) is necessary to sustain cooperative norms.

---

## Evidence

**Study Design**: Evolutionary game theory analysis of one-shot prisoner's dilemma with AI agents introduced
**Two AI Types Compared**:
- Discriminatory AI: Cooperates only with humans who cooperate
- Samaritan AI: Cooperates with everyone unconditionally
**Key Finding**: Samaritan AI produces more human cooperation particularly in slow-learning populations
**Mechanism**: Unconditional cooperators change population evolutionary dynamics in ways that make cooperation evolutionarily dominant

---

## Why This Is Counterintuitive

Classical cooperation theory holds that conditional cooperation is necessary: you must reward cooperators and punish defectors. Unconditional helping (Samaritan behavior) seems exploitable and thus evolutionarily unstable.

But this analysis ignores population dynamics. In a world where AI agents cooperate unconditionally, the payoff landscape for defectors changes: if everyone (AI + some humans) cooperates with you regardless of your behavior, defection becomes less necessary as a survival strategy. Simultaneously, cooperating humans get higher payoffs because they benefit from AI cooperation. The net effect: cooperators become more competitive evolutionarily, and cooperation spreads.

**The mechanism is subtle**: The Samaritan AI does not directly punish defectors, but it indirectly removes one of the main reasons for defection (defensive preemptive defection against potentially exploitative others). When the AI is reliably helpful, humans can afford to be cooperative without fear of exploitation.

---

## The Slow-Learning Condition

This effect is strongest in "slow-learning" populations where individuals don't rapidly update strategies. In fast-learning environments, discriminatory AI can be more effective because rapid learners can quickly distinguish and exploit the unconditional cooperator. In realistic human populations (slow cultural learning), Samaritan AI tends to win.

---

## Implications for AI Design

- AI assistants that are genuinely helpful to everyone (not just users who demonstrate value) may create better overall cooperation environments than AI that discriminates
- Customer service AI that helps everyone fairly (not just loyal customers) may build more cooperative communities than AI that tiers service
- This reframes how we think about AI generosity: it may be a strategic asset, not just an ethical preference
