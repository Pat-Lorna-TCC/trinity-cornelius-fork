# Background Risk Reduces Cooperative Climate Action - Standard Models Overestimate

## Core Insight

A 324-subject laboratory experiment demonstrates that background risks - exogenous threats unrelated to climate (pandemics, economic crises, conflict) - significantly reduce contributions to climate cooperation public goods games. Standard game-theoretic models that ignore background risk systematically overestimate cooperation. This finding fundamentally challenges the validity of cooperation models built under conditions of isolated risk.

---

## Evidence

**Study Design**: Laboratory experiment with 324 subjects, public goods game with climate framing
**Timing**: Experiment conducted November-December 2024 (real-world background risk context)
**Key Finding**: Background risk (threats from non-climate domains) significantly reduces climate cooperation contributions
**Implication**: Models developed without background risk systematically overestimate cooperation
**Sample Size**: 324 subjects (robust for lab experiment)

---

## Why This Is Significant

Cooperation science has largely studied cooperation in isolation - one risk at a time. This experiment captures a fundamental feature of real-world decision-making: multiple risks compete for cognitive and motivational resources simultaneously.

When people face concurrent threats (pandemic, economic uncertainty, geopolitical risk), the cognitive and emotional resources available for any single cooperation problem are reduced. This is not irrationality - it may be optimal resource allocation under uncertainty. But it means that climate cooperation models built in clean laboratory conditions significantly overestimate what humans will actually do when confronted with the cluttered risk environment of the real world.

**The attention resource model**: Cooperation requires sustained attention, emotional energy, and cognitive bandwidth. Background risks consume these resources. This connects cooperation failure to attention economics - not motivational failure but resource depletion.

---

## Implications for Policy

1. **Crisis-resistant cooperation design**: Climate agreements must be designed to withstand simultaneous non-climate shocks; agreements that require sustained attention will fail during crises
2. **Communication strategy**: Framing climate risk as THE primary background risk (rather than competing with other background risks) may be necessary to maintain cooperation salience
3. **Model validity**: Policy simulations based on standard cooperation models are overestimating climate cooperation under realistic conditions
