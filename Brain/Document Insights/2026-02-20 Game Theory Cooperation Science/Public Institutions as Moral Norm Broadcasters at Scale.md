# Public Institutions as Moral Norm Broadcasters at Scale

## Core Insight

A mathematical model demonstrates that taxation and public institutions can function as norm broadcasters - creating synchronized reputation assessments at the scale of large anonymous societies where direct observation is impossible. This extends the "leading eight" norms framework to mass-scale societies. Public institutions solve the opinion synchronization problem (identified in Kawakatsu et al., 2024) for large populations that cannot achieve synchronization through direct interaction.

---

## Evidence

**Study Design**: Mathematical model of institutional norm broadcasting in large populations
**Key Finding**: Taxation enables public institutions to maintain and broadcast moral judgments at societal scale
**Mechanism**: Institutions aggregate and broadcast reputation information that individuals cannot observe directly
**Extension**: Extends "leading eight" norms (which work at small scale) to mass-scale societies

---

## Why This Is Significant

Direct reputation observation works at small scales (village, tribe, small organization) where you can observe others' behavior. At large scales (cities, nations, global communities), you cannot directly observe whether someone cooperated or defected with strangers. This creates the cooperation-at-scale problem.

Public institutions solve this by:
1. **Aggregating**: Collecting dispersed reputation information (courts, regulatory bodies, rating agencies)
2. **Verifying**: Adding credibility to reputation assessments (legal standards, public records)
3. **Broadcasting**: Distributing assessments to people who couldn't observe directly (public records, media)

**Taxation as mechanism**: Taxation funds these institutions, making them a form of collective investment in the cooperation infrastructure. This reframes taxation from pure redistribution to cooperation technology.

---

## The Opinion Synchronization Link

This finding directly complements the opinion synchronization research: institutions create the synchronized reputation assessments that indirect reciprocity requires at scale. Without institutions, large-scale indirect reciprocity fails because opinions fragment. With institutions, moral norms can propagate to people who never interact directly.
