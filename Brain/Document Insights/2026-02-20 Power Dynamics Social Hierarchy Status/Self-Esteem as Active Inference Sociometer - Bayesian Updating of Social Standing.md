## Core Framework

Self-esteem is not a stable psychological trait - it is a dynamic inference process that minimizes prediction error (free energy) between self-perceived social standing and social feedback. Using the active inference framework (Free Energy Principle), self-esteem functions as a **sociometer** that continuously estimates one's social position through Bayesian belief updating. When social feedback matches self-perceived standing (prediction confirmed), the system is stable. When they diverge (prediction error), the system generates affective states (anxiety, elation) that drive either:
- **Active inference**: Behavioral change to restore status
- **Perceptual inference**: Belief revision (accepting new social position)

## Key Predictions of This Framework

| Scenario | Framework Prediction |
|----------|---------------------|
| Low self-esteem | Persistent negative prediction error about social standing; prior beliefs about low social value are well-calibrated to a disappointing social environment |
| High self-esteem | Strong prior beliefs about high social value; resistant to downward revision (high precision priors) |
| Status threat | Generates free energy (prediction error) that must be resolved through action (prove worth) or belief change (accept lower status) |
| Social anxiety | Heightened sensitivity to prediction errors about social standing; excessive free energy generation |

## Why This Is Significant

The active inference framing of self-esteem provides the computational bridge between social neuroscience and identity theory. It explains:

1. **Why status threat feels destabilizing**: Status threat is computationally equivalent to any violated prediction - the brain treats social demotion with the same machinery it uses for any unexpected event
2. **Why identity challenges feel threatening**: Stable identity requires precise priors; challenge to identity = prediction error = free energy = visceral discomfort
3. **Why self-esteem is sticky**: High self-esteem individuals have high-precision priors about their social position; these are resistant to updating (Bayesian prior strength)
4. **Why social anxiety is chronic**: Anxious individuals maintain high sensitivity (low prior precision) leading to excessive prediction error from minor social feedback
