## Core Insight

The right uncinate fasciculus - a white matter tract connecting the anterior temporal lobe to the orbitofrontal cortex - shows strong correlation with social dominance measures (hierarchy position, aggressive behaviors, submissive behaviors) in squirrel monkeys, and this same tract is linked to social aggression and antisocial personality in humans. This specific neural architecture for dominance has been conserved across approximately 35 million years of primate evolution, making social dominance one of the most evolutionarily ancient organized behavioral capacities in the primate brain.

---

## Evidence

**Study Design**: Advanced diffusion MRI tractography (multishell high-resolution diffusion weighted imaging) on 15 female squirrel monkeys; behavioral hierarchy correlation
**Sample Size**: N=15 squirrel monkeys
**Key Results**:
- Strong correlation between right uncinate fasciculus integrity and: hierarchy position, aggressive behaviors, submissive behaviors
- Right hemispheric lateralization was pronounced (bilateral trends noted)
- Fornix (memory control tract) showed NO such correlation - confirming specificity
- Findings align with human studies: uncinate fasciculus linked to social aggression, psychopathy
**Limitations**: Female squirrel monkeys only; requires replication with larger samples and other species
**Citation**: Royo, Orset, Catani, Pouget & Thiebaut de Schotten (2025), *Journal of Neuroscience*

## Why This Is Significant

The evolutionary antiquity of the dominance-uncinate fasciculus relationship (35 million years) places social dominance among the most deeply conserved social behaviors in the primate lineage. This is not a recently evolved human cognitive capacity - it is ancient primate behavioral infrastructure.

The right hemisphere lateralization is notable given that the right hemisphere is broadly associated with social and emotional processing, and the right uncinate fasciculus specifically connects the temporal lobe (face/voice recognition, social memory) with the orbitofrontal cortex (value/status computation). Dominance requires integrating social identity information with value judgments - exactly this connectivity.

The link to psychopathy and antisocial personality disorders in humans suggests that disorders of dominance dysregulation may be understood as dysfunction in this evolutionarily ancient system - either through structural abnormalities in the uncinate fasciculus or its associated circuits.
