# Dopamine Tracks Relative Social Value While Serotonin Tracks Absolute Value - First-in-Humans Evidence

**Source**: Batten, S.R., Bang, D., Kopell, B.H. et al. (February 2024). "Dopamine and serotonin in human substantia nigra track social context and value signals during economic exchange." *Nature Human Behaviour*, 8(4), 718-728. DOI: 10.1038/s41562-024-01831-w

## Core Insight

The first real-time simultaneous measurement of both dopamine and serotonin in awake humans during social exchange reveals a functional dissociation: dopamine acts as a social reward prediction error signal (tracking relative changes in offer value vs. prior offers), while serotonin acts as an absolute value snapshot (tracking the current offer's worth independent of comparison). Together, these two systems generate a comprehensive social value signal - dopamine tells you if this social interaction is better or worse than expected; serotonin tells you what it's worth right now.

---

## Evidence

**Study Design**: Real-time voltammetry during awake deep brain stimulation surgery (Parkinson's disease patients), machine learning signal decoding, Ultimatum Game economic paradigm
**Sample Size**: N=4 patients (groundbreaking methodology; small but unprecedented access)
**Key Results**:
- Dopamine levels higher during human vs. computer partner interactions - tracking social context itself
- Dopamine tracked trial-by-trial changes in offer value relative to prior offers (= reward prediction error)
- Serotonin tracked the absolute current value of the offer independent of comparison
- Participants rejected more offers in the human condition - social fairness norms are neurochemically encoded
- Lead author: "We've seen these signaling molecules before, but this is the first time we've seen them dance."
**Replication**: Requires replication with larger samples; small N is a known limitation
**Citation**: Batten, Bang, Kopell et al. (2024), *Nature Human Behaviour*

## Why This Is Significant

This finding directly extends the existing vault's dopamine framework into the social domain. The dopamine-as-reward-prediction-error framework (already captured in Reward-Prediction Error (RPE)) now applies explicitly to social comparison and status. Dopamine doesn't just track whether you got a reward - it tracks whether you got more or less social value than expected. This is the neurochemical basis of social comparison, relative status sensitivity, and keeping up with the Joneses.

The serotonin dissociation is equally important: absolute social value and relative social value are encoded by different neurotransmitter systems. This explains why someone can be objectively well-off yet feel low status (serotonin says "good" but dopamine says "worse than yesterday"). It also suggests that serotonin deficiency may impair the ability to feel satisfied with one's absolute social position, creating chronic upward comparison.

The social fairness rejection (more offers rejected in human vs. computer condition) confirms that social norms are neurochemically distinct from pure value computation - humans are not rational social actors.
