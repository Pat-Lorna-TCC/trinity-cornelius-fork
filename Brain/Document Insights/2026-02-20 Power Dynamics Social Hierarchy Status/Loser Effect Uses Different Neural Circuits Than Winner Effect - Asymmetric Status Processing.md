# Loser Effect Uses Different Neural Circuits Than Winner Effect - Asymmetric Status Processing

**Source**: Hsu, M.T., Akamine, Y., Kurima, K., Tanaka, K.Z., & Wickens, J.R. (September 2025). "Cholinergic interneurons of the dorsomedial striatum mediate winner-loser effects on social hierarchy dynamics in male mice." *iScience*. DOI: 10.1016/j.isci.2025.113581

## Core Insight

Winning and losing social competitions engage entirely different neural circuits. The winner effect (gaining status after winning) operates through reward-based learning circuits. The loser effect (losing status after defeat) operates through **decision-making circuits in novel contexts** - specifically cholinergic interneurons in the dorsomedial striatum. Selectively lesioning these cholinergic interneurons eliminates the loser effect entirely (mice maintain dominance after defeat) while leaving the winner effect untouched. Social defeat is not the absence of social victory - it is an active cognitive recalibration of decision-making.

---

## Evidence

**Study Design**: Dominance tube tests to establish hierarchy; defeat experiments with dominant mice from other cages; selective cholinergic interneuron (ChI) lesioning in dorsomedial striatum; behavioral tracking
**Sample Size**: Male mice (multiple cohorts)
**Key Results**:
- Mice who lost competitions became less dominant in their home cage (loser effect confirmed)
- Lesioning cholinergic interneurons completely prevented the loser effect
- Winner effect was unaffected by the same lesioning
- Circuit dissociation: winner effect = reward learning; loser effect = decision-making recalibration
- Professor Wickens: "Dominance seems to be a choice, based on previous experience"
**Replication**: Novel finding; requires independent replication
**Citation**: Hsu, Akamine, Kurima, Tanaka & Wickens (2025), *iScience*, OIST

## Why This Is Significant

The asymmetry between winning and losing has profound implications. Status loss is not simply "losing status points" in a symmetric accounting system - it actively reprograms decision-making circuits. This explains why recovery from social defeat takes longer and follows a different trajectory than acquiring dominance. It also suggests that:

1. **Depression following social defeat** may be mechanistically distinct from deprivation of social reward - it involves a different circuit
2. **The persistence of the loser effect** (mice stay dominated even after circumstances change) reflects a genuine cognitive state change, not just emotional reactivity
3. **Human social defeat phenomena** (learned helplessness, chronic self-doubt after status loss) may involve dorsomedial striatum cholinergic mechanisms

The finding that dominance is "a choice based on previous experience" reframes social hierarchy as a set of behavioral decisions shaped by prior outcomes - not fixed by physical attributes.
