## Core Insight

A 2025 *Nature Communications* study overturned the prevailing assumption that language comprehension and autobiographical memory operate via separate neurocognitive systems. Using fMRI neural decoding, researchers found **shared neural representations** elicited in both sentence comprehension and self-generated autobiographical mental imagery - most prominent in DMN midline cortical regions but also present in lateral temporal-parietal-frontal semantic regions.

A shared neural feature space encoded both self-generated autobiographical imagery and externally driven sentence semantics - the "semantic system" and "autobiographical system" are not cleanly separate.

## Why This Is Counter-Intuitive

**Previous Model**: Understanding language about the world (semantic system) and remembering one's own life (episodic/autobiographical system) are functionally distinct - they process different types of information via different neural circuits.

**New Finding**: The self's narrative and language comprehension share representational space. This means:
- Stories we read or hear about **others** may literally encode using the same neural structures as memories of **our own lives**
- The boundary between "understanding a story" and "remembering my life" is neurologically blurry
- Narrative transportation (being absorbed in fiction) may operate through **literal autobiographical memory mechanisms**

---

## Implications for Narrative Persuasion

This neural finding provides the mechanism for why stories change us more deeply than arguments:
- When we comprehend a narrative, we are using autobiographical memory encoding machinery
- The story is not being processed as "external information" - it is being encoded in the same substrate as personal experience
- This explains why **fictional stories can feel like lived experience** and why "I was in a story like that" is a profound moment of identification
