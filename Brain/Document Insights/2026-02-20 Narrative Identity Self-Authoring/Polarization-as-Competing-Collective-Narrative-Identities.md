## Core Framework

2024-2025 research reframes political polarization as fundamentally a **conflict between competing collective narrative identities**, not policy disagreements. Extreme polarization emerges when opposing camps develop contrasting collective narratives - and within those camps, dissent produces splinter narratives of increasing extremity.

---

## Context and Acceptance

**Field Acceptance**: Emerging theoretical consensus (2024-2025); supported by large-scale empirical data
**Supporting Evidence**: Survey of American national narratives + large-scale social media analysis
**Key Innovation**: Shifts analysis from "what do people believe?" to "what story does each side tell about who we are?"

---

## Key Empirical Findings

**Finding 1 - The Mnemonic Standoff**:
Empirical study of American national narratives found three types (progress, glorifying, critical) - but **no single narrative held by a majority**. A "mnemonic standoff" between glorifying and critical national narratives maps directly onto the Democrat-Republican divide. Americans are not arguing about policy; they are arguing about **which story about America is the true one**.

**Finding 2 - Decentralized Narrative Amplification**:
Large-scale Twitter/X analysis found that **ideological alignment** - not specific actors like media or activists - drives narrative polarization. The traditional top-down agenda-setting model has been replaced by "decentralized, bottom-up mechanisms where users collectively amplify narratives aligned with their ideological preferences."

**Finding 3 - Fragmentation Mechanism**:
Polarization = gradual fragmentation of a once-shared story into incompatible identity-defining counter-narratives. Within each camp, further dissent produces splinter narratives of increasing extremity (radicalization = narrative divergence, not just belief intensification).

---

## Why This Is Significant

The conventional political science view: polarization = increasingly different policy preferences. The narrative identity view: polarization = **increasingly incompatible collective self-stories**.

This matters because:
- Policy compromise is possible even with different preferences
- Narrative compromise requires one side to abandon their **identity story** - which is neurologically expensive (dopamine cost of belief change, identity-protective cognition)
- Political interventions focused on policy will fail if the underlying narrative conflict is not addressed
