# Catalyst-as-Cause Confusion

**Source**: Nassim Nicholas Taleb, *Antifragile: Things That Gain from Disorder* (Random House, 2012) — "Catalyst-as-Cause Confusion"
**Document Type**: Book
**Extracted By**: AI (document-insight-extractor, Book Mode)
**Extraction Date**: 2026-06-28
**Scope**: Books/antifragile-taleb

## Core Insight
In fragile complex systems, collapse gets mis-attributed to the proximate trigger ("the last grain of sand," the subprime loan, the commodity price) rather than to the **accumulated underlying fragility**. The right object of study is the system's fragility/terrain, not which catalyst will fire.

## Mechanism / Why It Holds
Triggers are abundant and unpredictable; fragility is the actual cause and is detectable. "It would be unintelligent to attribute the collapse of a fragile bridge to the last truck that crossed it, and even more foolish to try to predict in advance which truck might bring it down." Trying to forecast the catalyst (funding "intelligence" to predict revolutions) is a category error — one should study the *statistical properties of the terrain* (physicists' **percolation theory**), not any single element. Blaming "intelligence failure" reveals a misunderstanding of complex systems.

## Evidence
- The **"Arab Spring" (2011)** blamed on rising commodity prices — debunked: wealthy Bahrain and Libya could afford to import grain, and considerably higher commodity prices had occurred a few years earlier with no uprising at all.
- **Subprime (2007-2008)** treated as the cause when it was a symptom of the crisis, not its underlying cause.
