# Via Negativa - Knowledge by Subtraction
**Source**: Nassim Nicholas Taleb, *Antifragile: Things That Gain from Disorder* (Random House, 2012) — Book V, "Via Negativa" / "Subtractive Knowledge" / "Less Is More" / "Learning to Subtract"
**Document Type**: Book
**Extracted By**: AI (document-insight-extractor, Book Mode)
**Extraction Date**: 2026-06-28
**Scope**: Books/antifragile-taleb

## Core Insight
Knowledge and action grow more reliably by *subtraction* than by addition. Negative knowledge (what is wrong, what does not work) is more robust to error than positive knowledge, because one observation can disconfirm a universal while no number can confirm it. The robust move is therefore to remove fragility and the harmful first, not to add the beneficial. A corollary - "less is more": in rare-event domains a tiny number of factors drives almost all the outcome, so simple fast-and-frugal rules beat complex models, and roughly a 1% modification of a system can cut about 99% of its fragility.

## Mechanism / Why It Holds
The asymmetry of disconfirmation versus confirmation: spotting one black swan kills "all swans are white," but seeing millions of white swans never proves it. Elimination compounds; addition exposes you to hidden error. Charlatans are detectable because they offer only positive advice ("Ten Steps to X"); professionals win by *not losing* (chess by not blundering, wealth by not going bust). If you need several reasons to do something, you are talking yourself into it - obvious, robust decisions require a single reason.

## Evidence
Popperian disconfirmation (rooted in the older skeptical-empirical medical school). Steve Jobs: "Innovation is saying no to 1,000 things." Pareto 80/20 compounding toward ~99/1 (99% of internet traffic from <1% of sites). Ezekiel Emanuel's health-cost figures: the sickest 10% consume ~64% of costs. (Flag: Pareto figures and the 2012-era health-cost statistics may be dated; the disconfirmation logic is timeless.)
