# Convex Tinkering - Trial and Error as Optionality

**Source**: Nassim Nicholas Taleb, *Antifragile: Things That Gain from Disorder* (Random House, 2012) — "Thank You, Errors"; "Search and How Errors Can Be Investments"; "To Fail Seven Times, Plus or Minus Two"; "Nature's Opaque Logic"; "Guilty or Innocent"
**Document Type**: Book
**Extracted By**: AI (document-insight-extractor, Book Mode)
**Extraction Date**: 2026-06-28
**Scope**: Books/antifragile-taleb

## Core Insight
Trial and error with **small bounded errors and large open payoffs** (tinkering / bricolage / fail-fast) is a process that *harvests optionality* and substitutes for directed intelligence. It is not blind randomness: rationality enters only at the end — in recognizing and keeping a result markedly better than the prior state (exercising the option). Each failure is information — an investment, not a loss.

## Mechanism / Why It Holds
Stack many small, survivable, independent errors under capped downside and you converge on solutions while discovering unplanned ones. Negative/subtractive knowledge ("where the wallet is *not*") is more reliable than positive. Bottom-up tinkering is gradual and reversible; top-down design makes large irreversible errors — hence Taleb's asymmetry: "what Mother Nature does is rigorous until proven otherwise; what humans and science do is flawed until proven otherwise." Evolution itself proceeds by "undirected, convex bricolage... continuous, repetitive, small, localized mistakes" — selection over blueprint.

## Evidence
- **Marc Andreessen's modus**: invest in people who can pivot "six or seven times" over a career, not in business plans — immunity from back-fit narratives.
- **Greg Stemm** shipwreck/oil search (2007 "Black Swan" find): a "bad quarter" (cost, no find) is investment, not distress — controlled, not foolish, randomness.
