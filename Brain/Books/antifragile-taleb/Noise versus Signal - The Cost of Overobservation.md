# Noise versus Signal - The Cost of Overobservation

**Source**: Nassim Nicholas Taleb, *Antifragile: Things That Gain from Disorder* (Random House, 2012) — Chapter 7 ("Neuroticism in Industrial Proportions," "Media-Driven Neuroticism")
**Document Type**: Book
**Extracted By**: AI (document-insight-extractor, Book Mode)
**Extraction Date**: 2026-06-28
**Scope**: Books/antifragile-taleb

## Core Insight
The more frequently you sample data, the worse the signal-to-noise ratio, because noise scales up far faster than signal. Over-monitoring manufactures overreaction — turning a calm decision-maker into a neurotic one — and is a primary engine of overintervention. "Data is toxic in large quantities"; ration it and look only at large changes.

## Mechanism / Why It Holds
At a fixed underlying signal:noise ratio, increasing observation frequency multiplies the noise term disproportionately. You then mistake noise for information, act on it, and cause iatrogenic harm. Survival-relevant signals reach you anyway: "it is almost impossible for someone... not drowning in data, to mistake a vital signal... for noise." The robust rule is to "only look at very large changes in data or conditions, never at small ones."

## Evidence
A 50:50 yearly signal:noise ratio degrades to ~5:95 daily and ~0.5:99.5 hourly — "two hundred times more noise than signal" at hourly cadence, so "anyone who listens to news... is one step below sucker." Media distortion: ~6,200 persons die daily in the US, many of preventable causes, yet coverage fixates on anecdotal/sensational deaths, distorting the public's risk map. (Quantified but stylized model, 2012.)
