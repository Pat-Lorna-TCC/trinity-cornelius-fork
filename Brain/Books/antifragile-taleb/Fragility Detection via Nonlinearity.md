# Fragility Detection via Nonlinearity

**Source**: Nassim Nicholas Taleb, *Antifragile: Things That Gain from Disorder* (Random House, 2012) — Chapter 18 ("On the Difference Between a Large Stone and a Thousand Pebbles") and Chapter 19 ("How to Detect Who Will Go Bust")
**Document Type**: Book
**Extracted By**: AI (document-insight-extractor, Book Mode)
**Extraction Date**: 2026-06-28
**Scope**: Books/antifragile-taleb

## Core Insight
Fragility is detectable as concavity — the acceleration of harm. If doubling the dose/shock *more* than doubles the damage, the thing is fragile; if the response curves outward (a "smile"), it is antifragile. This is a model-free second-derivative test: you measure the acceleration of harm, not the probability of events.

## Mechanism / Why It Holds
Anything unbroken but exposed to nonlinearity is fragile to large, rare events, because one large shock harms more than the cumulative sum of equivalent small shocks — "the difference between a thousand pebbles and a large stone of equivalent weight." (Driving into a wall once at 50 mph does far more than ten times the harm of ten hits at 5 mph.) Measuring convexity needs only a defective "ruler" — second-order effects survive bad models, so you can detect fragility without naming which variable will move.

## Evidence
Fannie Mae's secret 2003 internal risk report (obtained via NYT journalist Alex Berenson) showed losses accelerating as an economic variable moved — "monstrous" concavity — making the 2008 collapse predictable from the curvature *alone*, without knowing the trigger. Later formalized with Raphael Douady as the IMF-proposed fragility-detection heuristic. Per the gem set, markets show ~10,000x more 0.1% events than 10% events (2012-era figures).
