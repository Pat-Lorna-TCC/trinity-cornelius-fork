# Naive Interventionism and Action Bias
**Source**: Nassim Nicholas Taleb, *Antifragile: Things That Gain from Disorder* (Random House, 2012) — Book III, "Not Doing Nothing" / "Non-Naive Interventionism" / "In Praise of Procrastination - The Fabian Kind"
**Document Type**: Book
**Extracted By**: AI (document-insight-extractor, Book Mode)
**Extraction Date**: 2026-06-28
**Scope**: Books/antifragile-taleb

## Core Insight
We systematically over-intervene because action is visible and rewarded while prevention and non-action are invisible and unrewarded - so we "fix" systems that would self-correct. The same actors who over-intervene on the trivial under-intervene on the consequential (over-intervention comes bundled with under-intervention). The corrective is not anti-intervention but a *protocol*: intervene only to cap size, concentration, and speed where risks are nonlinear; otherwise leave robust systems alone.

## Mechanism / Why It Holds
"Look what I did for you" sells better than "look what I avoided"; bonus and recognition systems reward salient action over absence of error. Procrastination and deliberate delay (festina lente, the Fabian/Cunctator strategy, wu-wei) act as a naturalistic filter: they let a system exercise its own antifragility and let moving targets resolve before commitment. Intervention also depletes mental and economic resources, so it is rarely available when truly needed.

## Evidence
The copy-editor experiment: each editor proposes ~5 edits per page, the next partly reverses them; an over-edited *Washington Post* op-ed missed the one real error (a date), while the *Financial Times* made a single correction (1989 → 1990). Drachten, Netherlands removed all street signs and accidents fell - attention is antifragile, whetted by felt danger (fewer pedestrians die jaywalking than at regulated crossings). Highway speed limits are justified because accident risk rises *nonlinearly* with speed. (Illustrative cases, not controlled trials.)
