# The Nonpredictive Approach

**Source**: Nassim Nicholas Taleb, *Antifragile: Things That Gain from Disorder* (Random House, 2012) — Prologue; Book III, Chapters 8–9 ("The Predictive," "What the Nonpredictor Can Predict")
**Document Type**: Book
**Extracted By**: AI (document-insight-extractor, Book Mode)
**Extraction Date**: 2026-06-28
**Scope**: Books/antifragile-taleb

## Core Insight
Fragility is a present, detectable property; the probability of the rare event that would harm it is not computable. So base decisions on measuring and comparing fragility, not on forecasting events. After a failure, shift the question from "why didn't we predict it?" (excusable) to "why did we build something fragile to it?" (inexcusable). The one thing you *can* reliably forecast is that the fragile will eventually blow up.

## Mechanism / Why It Holds
Model error explodes precisely for the rarest, most consequential events, so prediction is weakest exactly where it matters most. The robust party survives any cause without naming it: "if you have extra cash in the bank... you do not need to predict much, unlike those who are in debt." Redundancy is a nonpredictive mode of action. Forecasting is itself iatrogenic — "numerical prediction leads people to take more risks," and "an overconfident pilot will eventually crash the plane."

## Evidence
Post-Fukushima redesign focused on exposure-to-failure rather than failure-probability; Sweden's post-1991 fiscal robustness. Of ~1 million economics professionals, "fewer than a handful" foresaw 2008. Taleb cites a Kahneman-referenced finding that arbitrary numerical anchors raise risk-taking even when subjects know the numbers are random. (2012-era examples.)
