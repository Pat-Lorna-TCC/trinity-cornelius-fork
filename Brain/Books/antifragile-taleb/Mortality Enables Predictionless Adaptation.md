# Mortality Enables Predictionless Adaptation

**Source**: Nassim Nicholas Taleb, *Antifragile: Things That Gain from Disorder* (Random House, 2012) — "Evolution and Unpredictability"; "Organisms Are Populations and Populations Are Organisms"
**Document Type**: Book
**Extracted By**: AI (document-insight-extractor, Book Mode)
**Extraction Date**: 2026-06-28
**Scope**: Books/antifragile-taleb

## Core Insight
An immortal organism would have to predict *all* future random events perfectly — "near perfection is not enough" — because it cannot adapt after the fact. **Mortality plus generational turnover is the mechanism by which nature adapts WITHOUT forecasting**: each generation is modified slightly, so the system tracks the environment with no need to foresee it. Nature prefers diversity *between* short-lived units over diversity *within* one immortal unit.

## Mechanism / Why It Holds
Random events give no advance warning, so post-event adaptation always lags ("Post-event adaptation, no matter how fast, would always be a bit late"). Letting units die one lifespan at a time means every random event brings its own antidote via ecological/genetic variation — "It is as if nature changed itself at every step and modified its strategy every instant." An economy run by nature would not "continuously bail out its living members to make them live forever," nor keep forecasting departments trying to outsmart the future.

## Evidence
The immortal-organism thought experiment and the evolutionary argument (conceptual, not statistical); formalized as a nonanticipative stochastic strategy. No empirical statistics — this is a logical/biological argument.
