# Selection versus Hormesis - The Survivorship Illusion
**Source**: Nassim Nicholas Taleb, *Antifragile: Things That Gain from Disorder* (Random House, 2012) — Book II, "What Does Not Kill Me Kills Others" / "National Entrepreneur Day"
**Document Type**: Book
**Extracted By**: AI (document-insight-extractor, Book Mode)
**Extraction Date**: 2026-06-28
**Scope**: Books/antifragile-taleb

## Core Insight
When a population looks "strengthened" by an ordeal, it may not be that the survivors got stronger (hormesis) but that the ordeal *killed the weak* (selection). "What doesn't kill me makes me stronger" is routinely mistaken for individual antifragility when it is actually a culling that produces a sturdier *average* at the expense of dead individuals. This distinguishes individual-level antifragility (hormesis - the same organism overcompensates and improves) from system-level antifragility bought by individual sacrifice (selection - "someone paid a price for the system to improve").

## Mechanism / Why It Holds
A ruthless filter removes the bottom of a distribution; the surviving cohort's higher mean creates a false illusion of causal strengthening. This is truncated-distribution statistics: the cohort is stronger, but not the individuals - the weaker ones simply died. Mistaking the antifragility of the *system* for that of the *individual* is the core error Taleb warns against when "seeing antifragility where it is not really there."

## Evidence
The newspaper claim that new mafia members - former Soviet exiles - had been "hardened by a visit to the Gulag," when the Gulag had in fact killed the weakest (survivorship, not hardening). A mathematical footnote on absorbing-barrier filtering raising observed means is the real content; the example merely illustrates the truncated-distribution mechanism.
