# Why Size and Scale Fragilize

**Source**: Nassim Nicholas Taleb, *Antifragile: Things That Gain from Disorder* (Random House, 2012) — "How to Be Squeezed"; "Kerviel and Micro-Kerviel"; "The Nonlinearity of Wealth"; "Lenin in Zurich"
**Document Type**: Book
**Extracted By**: AI (document-insight-extractor, Book Mode)
**Extraction Date**: 2026-06-28
**Scope**: Books/antifragile-taleb

## Core Insight
Size and over-optimization create fragility because **"squeezes"** (forced action regardless of cost) become *nonlinearly* more expensive as scale increases; the gains from size are visible while the risks are hidden. "Small may be ugly, but it is certainly less fragile." Conversely, a collection of small, autonomous units aggregates to a stable, antifragile whole — and scale is not invariant (a large state behaves nothing like a big municipality).

## Mechanism / Why It Holds
Harm scales superlinearly with exposure ("more is different" — emergent nonlinearity), so a large unit suffers disproportionate damage from a shock a collection of small independent units would absorb. Bottlenecks and tight coupling ("efficiency") convert small perturbations into catastrophes. Small scale also restores accountability and keeps errors local and self-correcting.

## Evidence
- **Société Générale / Kerviel** fire-sale (Jan 21, 2008, thin MLK-Day market): a ~$70B forced sale produced ~$6B in losses (markets dropped ~10%); a sale 1/10 the size (~$7B) would have caused near-zero loss.
- **Bent Flyvbjerg**: cost overruns rise with size *per non-divisible segment* — monolithic bridges/dams/tunnels worst; segmentable roads scale fine.
- **Richard Roll (1978) "hubris hypothesis"**: mergers show at best no efficiency gain; data three decades later still confirm it. *(Pre-2012 data; merger evidence is robust but the hubris framing remains debated.)*
