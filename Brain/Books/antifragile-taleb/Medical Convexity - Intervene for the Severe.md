# Medical Convexity - Intervene for the Severe

**Source**: Nassim Nicholas Taleb, *Antifragile: Things That Gain from Disorder* (Random House, 2012) — Chapter 21 ("Medicine, Convexity, and Opacity") and Chapter 22 ("To Live Long, but Not Too Long")
**Document Type**: Book
**Extracted By**: AI (document-insight-extractor, Book Mode)
**Extraction Date**: 2026-06-28
**Scope**: Books/antifragile-taleb

## Core Insight
Medical (and any) intervention should be reserved for severe cases and withheld from marginal ones, because treatment benefit is convex to the severity of the condition while iatrogenic harm is roughly constant. Hence "the iatrogenics is in the patient, not in the treatment." Intervene only when the payoff is large and visibly exceeds harm; otherwise let nature work.

## Mechanism / Why It Holds
Benefit rises disproportionately with severity (conditional-survival structure); a treatment with fixed side-effect cost is net-positive for the very ill and net-negative for the near-healthy. There is no free lunch for the healthy — nature would already have found an unconditional improvement — and illness severity is exponentially rare, so the sicker the patient the more room medicine has to help. For convex responses, variable/random dosing beats steady dosing.

## Evidence
Hypertension-drug benefit rises 5.6% (mild, ~1 in 18 benefits) → 26% (high) → 72% (severe). Of ~120,000 drugs available, almost none unconditionally improve a healthy person. Deviation rarity: 3 sigma ~300x rarer, 5 sigma >1,000,000x rarer than normal. Brewster et al. found variable lung-ventilator pressure cut mortality vs constant pressure. Fasting/autophagy (Longo's starved mice tolerating chemotherapy). Source-tier credible-interpreter; clinical figures are pre-2012 and should be checked against current trial data.
