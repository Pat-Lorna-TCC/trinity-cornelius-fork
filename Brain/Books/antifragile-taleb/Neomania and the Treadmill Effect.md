# Neomania and the Treadmill Effect
**Source**: Nassim Nicholas Taleb, *Antifragile: Things That Gain from Disorder* (Random House, 2012) — Book V, Ch. 20: "A Few Mental Biases" / "Neomania and Treadmill Effects"
**Document Type**: Book
**Extracted By**: AI (document-insight-extractor, Book Mode)
**Extraction Date**: 2026-06-28
**Scope**: Books/antifragile-taleb

## Core Insight
Neomania - the love of the new for its own sake - systematically overvalues novel technology and undervalues the time-tested. We perceive change and variation rather than statics, and we hedonically adapt to each upgrade (the treadmill), so we keep chasing version 2.0 while the satisfaction from the last upgrade decays back to baseline.

## Mechanism / Why It Holds
A perceptual bias (Kahneman/Tversky): we notice and store *differences*, not *levels*, so things that change (phones) feel more important than things that don't (water, the wheel). New technology also benefits from survivorship blindness - its failures are buried - and is far easier to hype and monetize than the value of the past ("someone who sells futuristic ideas will not make money selling the value of the past"). Practical heuristic: items with an on/off switch invite neomania and tend to be fragile; artisanal, non-switch items don't.

## Evidence
David Edgerton: in the early 2000s we produced ~2.5x as many bicycles as cars and spent most technological resources maintaining or refining old technologies. Kahneman's hedonic-adaptation research underpins the treadmill claim. (Mostly conceptual and illustrative; flag: the hedonic-adaptation literature is pre-replication-crisis psychology and the bicycle statistic is pre-2012.)
