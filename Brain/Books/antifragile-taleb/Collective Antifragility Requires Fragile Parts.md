# Collective Antifragility Requires Fragile Parts

**Source**: Nassim Nicholas Taleb, *Antifragile: Things That Gain from Disorder* (Random House, 2012) — "Antifragility by Layers"; "Organisms Are Populations..."; "Why the Aggregate Hates the Individual"
**Document Type**: Book
**Extracted By**: AI (document-insight-extractor, Book Mode)
**Extraction Date**: 2026-06-28
**Scope**: Books/antifragile-taleb

## Core Insight
Antifragility is a **layer-relative** property, not a node property. For a system (an economy, an industry, a gene pool) to be antifragile, its constituent units (firms, entrepreneurs, organisms) must be individually fragile and fail frequently. The aggregate literally "wants harm to the parts"; the same event is antifragile at level N+1 and fragile at level N. Corollary: the dividing line between antifragile and fragile systems is whether errors are **independent** (confined, even self-limiting) or **contagious** (correlated and compounding).

## Mechanism / Why It Holds
Improvement of the whole proceeds by selection — weak units fail and are supplanted by fitter ones, transferring benefit upward. Removing unit fragility (bailouts, immortal firms) stagnates the collective; bailouts amount to "transferring fragility from the collective to the unfit." The system needs *local, but not global,* risk-taking: no single failure may drag others down.

## Evidence
- **Restaurant industry**: restaurants go bankrupt constantly; the *collective* is antifragile and reliably high-quality precisely because of it. Were restaurants individually immortal, the result would be "Soviet-style cafeteria food."
- **Aviation vs. banking** (the independent/contagious contrast): each aviation failure makes the next *less* likely (errors independent); each banking failure makes the next *more* likely (errors contagious).
