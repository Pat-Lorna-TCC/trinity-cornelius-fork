# The Soviet-Harvard Illusion
**Source**: Nassim Nicholas Taleb, *Antifragile: Things That Gain from Disorder* (Random House, 2012) — Book IV, Ch. 13 "Lecturing Birds on How to Fly": "The Soviet-Harvard Department of Ornithology" / "Epiphenomena" / "Debunking Epiphenomena" / "Cherry-Picking" / "The Industrial Revolution"

## Core Insight
The "lecturing birds how to fly" illusion: we credit formal, academic knowledge with producing the skills, technology, and wealth that actually arise from antifragile trial-and-error doing. The real causal arrow runs Wealth → Education (and Practice → Theory), not the reverse; much academic theorizing is an *epiphenomenon* - commentary written after the fact. An epiphenomenon is a causal illusion: the compass does not steer the ship.

## Mechanism / Why It Holds
The Baconian linear model (Academia → Applied Science → Practice) is reversed in most domains; the true loop is Random Tinkering → Heuristics → Practice/Apprenticeship. Confirmation bias sustains the illusion because history is written by those who can codify ("if the student is smart, the teacher takes the credit"), and the iatrogenics of education is never tallied. The fix: check temporal sequence (Granger-style causality) and study *changes*, not levels.

## Evidence
Lant Pritchett (World Bank): no evidence that raising national education raises income. Ha-Joon Chang: Korea vs Argentina and Taiwan vs Philippines - far lower 1960 literacy/income yet far higher income today. Sub-Saharan Africa: rising literacy alongside falling living standards. Terence Kealey and David Edgerton: the Industrial Revolution's textile breakthroughs (Kay's flying shuttle 1733, Arkwright's water frame 1769, Hargreaves' spinning jenny 1770) "owed nothing to science"; Bryson's *Home* found ~10x more clergymen-hobbyists than scientists leaving recorded contributions. (Flag: all pre-2012; the cross-country comparisons are observational, not causal-controlled.)
