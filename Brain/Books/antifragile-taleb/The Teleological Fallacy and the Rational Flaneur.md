# The Teleological Fallacy and the Rational Flaneur
**Source**: Nassim Nicholas Taleb, *Antifragile: Things That Gain from Disorder* (Random House, 2012) — Book IV, "Do You Really Know Where You Are Going?" / "The Teleological Fallacy" / "America's Principal Asset"; Ch. 13 "Matt Ridley's Anti-Teleological Argument" / "Corporate Teleology"
**Document Type**: Book
**Extracted By**: AI (document-insight-extractor, Book Mode)
**Extraction Date**: 2026-06-28
**Scope**: Books/antifragile-taleb

## Core Insight
The teleological fallacy (from *telos*, "end") is the illusion that you know exactly where you are going, knew it in the past, and that others succeeded by knowing their destination. The antidote is the *rational flâneur*: someone who rationally revises his targets at every step as new information arrives, treating the ability to switch course as an option to be preserved. A fixed plan is "tourism" - it assumes completeness of vision and locks you into a hard-to-revise program.

## Mechanism / Why It Holds
In an opaque world you cannot project the future from the past, so a revisable, opportunistic posture dominates a teleological one - the ability to switch is itself an option that makes you antifragile to uncertainty. Corollary: never ask people what they will want (Jobs distrusted focus groups - "people don't know what they want until you provide it"); strategic planning makes firms option-blind by locking them into a non-opportunistic course. (Note: opportunism applies to business, not to loyalty in human relationships.)

## Evidence
Management scholar William Starbuck published papers finding no evidence that strategic planning works - it makes corporations option-blind. Business "drift" illustrations: Coca-Cola began as a pharmaceutical; Raytheon (first missile guidance) was a refrigerator maker; Nokia began as a paper mill; DuPont started as an explosives company; Avon began in door-to-door book sales. (Illustrative, not statistical; the Starbuck finding is the empirical anchor.)
