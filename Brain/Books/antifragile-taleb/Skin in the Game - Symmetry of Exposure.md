# Skin in the Game - Symmetry of Exposure
**Source**: Nassim Nicholas Taleb, *Antifragile: Things That Gain from Disorder* (Random House, 2012) — Book VII, Ch. 23 "Skin in the Game" / "Hammurabi" / "Soul in the Game" / "The Antifragility and Ethics of (Large) Corporations" / "Artisans, Marketing, and the Cheapest to Deliver"; Ch. 25 Conclusion

## Core Insight
The central ethical pathology of modernity is the malignant *transfer of fragility*: one party takes the upside while another - often unknowingly - carries the downside. This is the agency problem reframed as an asymmetry of exposure, and ethics reduces largely to removing such stolen optionality. Heroism is its exact inverse (electing to bear downside for others), and "soul in the game" (doxastic commitment to one's beliefs) goes a step beyond skin in the game. A listed corporation has no native ethics - no shame, pity, or honor - because it lacks skin in the game and obeys only the balance sheet, structurally delivering the "cheapest-to-deliver." Heuristic corollary: anything requiring heavy marketing is, by that fact, suspect.

## Mechanism / Why It Holds
Re-attaching the downside to the decider aligns incentives and forces hidden tail-risk into the open. The builder, not an inspector, knows what is buried in the foundation - "the best place to hide risk" - so the remedy is to make the agent eat his own risk. These asymmetries are most severe for small-probability extreme events (Black Swans), whose exposure is easiest to conceal.

## Evidence
Hammurabi's code (~3,800 years old): a builder whose house collapses and kills the owner is put to death - Taleb calls it "the best risk-management rule ever." Roman engineers stood under their own bridges (the English added their families); Fat Tony's rule: "never board if the pilot isn't on the plane"; Ralph Nader's rule that war-voters have a descendant exposed to combat. (Conceptual and historical, not statistical.)
