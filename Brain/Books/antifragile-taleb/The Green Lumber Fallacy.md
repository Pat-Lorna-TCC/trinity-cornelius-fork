# The Green Lumber Fallacy
**Source**: Nassim Nicholas Taleb, *Antifragile: Things That Gain from Disorder* (Random House, 2012) — Book IV, Ch. 14 "When Two Things Are Not the 'Same Thing'": "The Green Lumber Fallacy" / "Conflation" / "Is It Like Cooking?"; Book VII, "Empedocles' Dog" / "What Does Not Make Sense" / "Plead Ignorance of Biology"

## Core Insight
The green lumber fallacy is mistaking the knowledge that *sounds* necessary and narratable for the tacit, less-visible knowledge that actually drives success. (A top "green lumber" trader thought green lumber meant lumber painted green, not freshly cut - and traded it superbly anyway.) The most useful knowledge is often the least teachable; the map (theory, the model of reality) and the territory (reality, the price) are not the same thing. A complementary rule: trust the empirical regularity over the causal story - things that have survived without explanation likely encode a hidden fit, since causal opacity means theories get revised while the effect holds constant.

## Mechanism / Why It Holds
Doers are selected non-narratively - "evolution does not need a word for the color blue" - so they can hold false explicit beliefs yet make superior decisions. "Price and reality as seen by economists are not the same thing": the relation has optionality and is too complex to map mathematically, so the practitioner's tacit feel beats the theorist's narrative. The "something" (reality) and the "function of something" (theory) diverge more as the asymmetry between them grows.

## Evidence
The green-lumber trader (from *What I Learned Losing a Million Dollars*) succeeded knowing nothing real about lumber, while the theory-laden narrator went bust. Taleb's foreign-exchange colleagues - hugely successful traders who could not place Switzerland on a map. James Le Fanu: deeper biological theory coincided with a *decline* in pharmaceutical discovery. (Illustrative cases plus the Le Fanu observation; not statistical.)
