# Inject Randomness to Unstick Systems

**Source**: Nassim Nicholas Taleb, *Antifragile: Things That Gain from Disorder* (Random House, 2012) — "Political Annealing" / "Hungry Donkeys"
**Document Type**: Book
**Extracted By**: AI (document-insight-extractor, Book Mode)
**Extraction Date**: 2026-06-28
**Scope**: Books/antifragile-taleb

## Core Insight
Beyond merely *not suppressing* volatility, deliberately **adding** low-intensity randomness can strengthen, unstick, or stabilize an antifragile system — sometimes randomness is the only thing that frees a system from a dangerous local optimum or a deadlock. "Absence of randomness equals guaranteed death."

## Mechanism / Why It Holds
Three distinct mechanisms:
1. **Escaping deadlock** — Buridan's donkey, frozen between equal food and water, is saved only by a random nudge.
2. **Simulated annealing / stochastic resonance** — random energy lets a system unstick from poor configurations and find better global ones; low-level noise can also lift a weak signal above the detection threshold (a weak SOS becomes audible against background hiss).
3. **Forced turnover** — random selection breaks entrenchment when incumbents (CEOs, tenured academics, politicians) overstay.

## Evidence
- **Pluchino et al. simulation**: adding randomly selected legislators improves the functioning of a parliamentary system.
- A controversial **1975 *Science* paper** (relayed via Nathan Myhrvold): random drilling beat the prevailing search method.
- **Athenian sortition**: assembly members were chosen by lot to protect the system from degeneracy.
- *(Note: core evidence is a relayed simulation and a pre-replication-crisis 1975 study — treat the specific results as illustrative; the underlying optimization principle (annealing) is mathematically sound.)*
