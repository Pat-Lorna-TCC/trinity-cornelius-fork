# The Lindy Effect
**Source**: Nassim Nicholas Taleb, *Antifragile: Things That Gain from Disorder* (Random House, 2012) — Book V, Ch. 20 "Time and Fragility": "To Age in Reverse - The Lindy Effect"; "What Does Not Make Sense"
**Document Type**: Book
**Extracted By**: AI (document-insight-extractor, Book Mode)
**Extraction Date**: 2026-06-28
**Scope**: Books/antifragile-taleb

## Core Insight
For the *non-perishable* - technologies, ideas, books, anything with an informational rather than organic nature - every additional period of survival *increases*, rather than decreases, remaining life expectancy. Robustness is proportional to age. This is the opposite of human actuarial aging: a person's expected remaining life shrinks each year, but a book in print 40 years can be expected to last another 40, and if it survives a further decade the expectation extends again.

## Mechanism / Why It Holds
Things prone to aging and fragility have already been broken by time, so survivorship is itself evidence of hidden fitness ("those technologies that were prone to aging are already dead"). Expected residual life becomes proportional to observed past life - a winner-take-all effect in longevity. Important caveat: this is a *probabilistic average*, not a guarantee for any single item; a technology already known to be dying (like a land line) is the conditional equivalent of a patient with a terminal diagnosis.

## Evidence
Richard Gott's test: listing Broadway shows running on 17 May 1993, he predicted the longest-running would last longest - proven right with ~95% accuracy. As a child Gott correctly guessed the Great Pyramid (~5,700 years) would outlive the Berlin Wall (12 years). Taleb credits Benoît Mandelbrot's formalization (life expectancy proportional to past life). (Gott's study is the empirical anchor; the rest is illustrative. Pre-2012.)
