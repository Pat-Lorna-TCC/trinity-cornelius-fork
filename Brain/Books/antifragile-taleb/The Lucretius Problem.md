# The Lucretius Problem

**Source**: Nassim Nicholas Taleb, *Antifragile: Things That Gain from Disorder* (Random House, 2012) — "Antifragile Responses as Redundancy" (Ch. 2)
**Document Type**: Book
**Extracted By**: AI (document-insight-extractor, Book Mode)
**Extraction Date**: 2026-06-28
**Scope**: Books/antifragile-taleb

## Core Insight
A systematic risk error: we take the worst event we have ever observed as the worst that can possibly occur, and size our defenses to it — ignoring that **every record-breaking event, when it happened, exceeded the worst case known at the time.** Stress-testing against the historical worst is therefore structurally blind to the next, larger shock.

## Mechanism / Why It Holds
Named after Lucretius, who wrote that the fool believes the tallest mountain in the world equals the tallest he has seen. The largest object/event one has encountered anchors the imagination as the maximum possible; because that past maximum was itself an unprecedented surprise, using it as a ceiling guarantees under-preparation. Nature instead *over-prepares* for the unprecedented — "If humans fight the last war, nature fights the next one."

## Evidence
- **Fukushima (2011)**: the reactor was built to withstand the worst *past* historical earthquake, with builders not imagining worse — and not registering that the worst past event had itself been a surprise without precedent.
- **Pharaonic Egypt**: scribes tracked the Nile's high-water mark and used it as the future worst-case ceiling.
- **Alan Greenspan** to Congress: the classic "It never happened before."
