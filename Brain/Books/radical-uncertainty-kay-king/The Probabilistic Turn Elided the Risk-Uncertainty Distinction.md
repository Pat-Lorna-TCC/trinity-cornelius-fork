# The Probabilistic Turn Elided the Risk-Uncertainty Distinction

**Source**: John Kay & Mervyn King, *Radical Uncertainty: Decision-Making Beyond the Numbers* (W. W. Norton, 2020) - Ch. 4 / 5
**Document Type**: Book
**Extracted By**: AI (document-insight-extractor, Book Mode)
**Extraction Date**: 2026-06-28
**Scope**: Books/radical-uncertainty-kay-king

## Core Insight

Over the twentieth century, economists deliberately erased the risk/uncertainty boundary that Frank Knight and John Maynard Keynes had drawn, extending probability to cover "every instance of our imperfect knowledge of the future." Kay & King call this the probabilistic turn. It spread out of economics into statistics, sociology, psychology, and law, and they argue it is a root cause of failures like the 2008 financial crisis. The point is sharp: this is a diagnosable intellectual error, not intellectual progress.

## Mechanism / Why It Matters

The error is one of conceptual annexation. Knight and Keynes held that some futures are radically uncertain - there is no meaningful probability to attach because the possible states of the world are not even enumerable. The probabilistic turn redefined all such ignorance as merely a probability we have not yet measured, collapsing a categorical distinction into a quantitative one. Once that move is made, every problem becomes a problem of estimating a number, every decision becomes an expected-value calculation, and the option "we do not know, and cannot put a number on it" disappears from the vocabulary of the discipline. The consequence is systematic overconfidence: institutions build risk models that report precise probabilities for events that are genuinely unknowable, then act as if the precision were real. Distinguish this from the bare risk/uncertainty distinction itself - the value of this note is the historical claim that the discipline consciously eliminated Knightian uncertainty, and the identification of that elimination as causally implicated in real-world catastrophe.

## Evidence

Kay & King: "in the last two decades probabilistic reasoning has almost completely dominated the description and analysis of decision-making under uncertainty." And on the historical defeat: "Keynes and Knight lost the battle." The diffusion claim - that the turn propagated from economics into statistics, sociology, psychology, and law - documents the breadth of the conceptual annexation. As a history-of-ideas claim about a documented intellectual movement, acceptance is established; the causal link to 2008 is the authors' interpretive thesis.
