# Resolvable versus Radical Uncertainty
**Source**: John Kay & Mervyn King, *Radical Uncertainty: Decision-Making Beyond the Numbers* (W. W. Norton, 2020) - Ch. 4 / Part II intro
**Document Type**: Book
**Extracted By**: AI (document-insight-extractor, Book Mode)
**Extraction Date**: 2026-06-28
**Scope**: Books/radical-uncertainty-kay-king

## Core Insight
Kay & King re-cut Knight's risk/uncertainty line into RESOLVABLE uncertainty (removable by looking it up, or governed by a known probability distribution) versus RADICAL uncertainty (no probability can be formed at all - "we simply do not know"). Radical uncertainty is the broad MIDDLE GROUND - obscurity, ignorance, vagueness, ambiguity, ill-defined problems - and is explicitly NOT merely Taleb's black swans, which are one extreme example of it.

## Mechanism / Why It Matters
The distinction is load-bearing because the two classes demand different cognitive tools. Resolvable uncertainty yields to research or to probability calculus; radical uncertainty does not, because there is no stable distribution to estimate. The error it guards against is treating the second as if it were the first - manufacturing probabilities for situations where none can legitimately be formed, which dresses ignorance up as calculable risk. The delta from the Knightian anchor is twofold: the resolvable/radical relabel sharpens the line at where probability can be applied, and it reframes radical uncertainty as the broad everyday middle ground rather than only the rare catastrophic tail. Black swans live inside radical uncertainty as one extreme; most of the territory is ordinary obscurity and ill-defined problems.

## Evidence
"Radical uncertainty has many dimensions: obscurity; ignorance; vagueness; ambiguity; ill-defined problems... We simply do not know." (Ch. 4 / Part II intro)
