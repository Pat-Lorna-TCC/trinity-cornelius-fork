# Mysteries Can Decay Into Puzzles As Knowledge Accumulates

**Source**: John Kay & Mervyn King, *Radical Uncertainty: Decision-Making Beyond the Numbers* (W. W. Norton, 2020) - Ch. 9, "From unknown unknowns to known unknowns"
**Document Type**: Book
**Extracted By**: AI (document-insight-extractor, Book Mode)
**Extraction Date**: 2026-06-28
**Scope**: Books/radical-uncertainty-kay-king

## Core Insight

The puzzle/mystery boundary is historically MOBILE. Advancing knowledge can convert a mystery into a soluble puzzle: dinosaur extinction became the asteroid-impact hypothesis, the Black Death yielded to germ theory, the unpredictable collapse of bridges under wind became a matter for wind-tunnel testing. But some mysteries remain mysteries forever, because although a definite answer exists it is permanently unrecoverable - the fate of the Mary Celeste crew has an answer no one will ever know. The taxonomy therefore has a direction of travel: unknown unknowns can become known unknowns, but not always.

## Mechanism / Why It Matters

This refines the static puzzle/mystery dichotomy by giving it dynamics. The classification of a problem is not fixed by its nature but by the current state of knowledge, so the right strategic question is which mysteries are candidates for decay into puzzles (worth investment in measurement and modelling) versus which are irreducibly mysterious (where the honest move is coping, not solving). Misclassifying a permanent mystery as a soluble puzzle invites the false-precision error; misclassifying a soluble puzzle as a mystery forecloses genuine progress. The arrow runs from unknown unknowns toward known unknowns as comprehension grows - which is itself the central work of inquiry.

## Evidence

Kay & King: "Mysteries can sometimes be resolved by advances in knowledge... Over the course of a century, the scope of mysteries has been diminished." They give worked historical cases of mysteries that became puzzles (asteroid extinction, germ theory of plague, aerodynamic loads on structures) and a contrasting permanent-mystery case (the Mary Celeste): "Even though there is an answer, we will never know what it is." The evidence is the historical record of inquiry rather than a controlled study.
