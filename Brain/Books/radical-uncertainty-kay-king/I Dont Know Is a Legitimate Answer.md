# I Dont Know Is a Legitimate Answer

**Source**: John Kay & Mervyn King, *Radical Uncertainty: Decision-Making Beyond the Numbers* (W. W. Norton, 2020) - Ch. 3
**Document Type**: Book
**Extracted By**: AI (document-insight-extractor, Book Mode)
**Extraction Date**: 2026-06-28
**Scope**: Books/radical-uncertainty-kay-king

## Core Insight

Decision tools that refuse to accept "I don't know" - retirement planners demanding 20-year inflation, life-expectancy and salary forecasts - force users to fabricate false knowledge. The disciplined response to a genuinely unknowable input is to refuse to invent it: "we do not make good decisions by professing knowledge we do not and cannot have." The value of attempting the exercise is not the spurious answer but the diagnosis - afterwards "we at least know what we don't know."

## Mechanism / Why It Matters

Software and procedures encode a hidden assumption: every input field must be filled. The structure of the tool thereby converts genuine ignorance into manufactured certainty, because the user has no way to register "unknowable" and so supplies a guess that the model then treats as data. This is how false precision enters at the very first step, before any calculation. Refusing to fabricate is not evasion - it is the honest mapping of the boundary of knowledge, and that boundary is itself decision-relevant information. Knowing what you cannot know reframes the problem from "compute the answer" to "decide robustly given that this is unknowable."

## Evidence

Kay & King describe retirement-planning programs that require multi-decade forecasts of inflation, salary growth and longevity: "One answer... the programs generally do not allow is 'I don't know'. But that is the answer which the authors, professional economists all our lives, would give." The point is delivered as testimony from domain experts about their own honest epistemic state, not as an empirical study - the authors, lifelong economists, decline to supply numbers their discipline cannot legitimately produce.
