# Puzzles versus Mysteries - Two Classes of Problem
**Source**: John Kay & Mervyn King, *Radical Uncertainty: Decision-Making Beyond the Numbers* (W. W. Norton, 2020) - Ch. 9 (Treverton's distinction)
**Document Type**: Book
**Extracted By**: AI (document-insight-extractor, Book Mode)
**Extraction Date**: 2026-06-28
**Scope**: Books/radical-uncertainty-kay-king

## Core Insight
Problems split into PUZZLES (well-defined rules, a single knowable correct answer that exists even if hard to find - probability applies) and MYSTERIES (no objectively correct solution; ill-defined, unbounded answer space; approachable only by framing - "what is going on here?"). The same cut recurs independently across fields: tame vs wicked problems (Rittel & Webber, 1973), aleatory vs epistemic uncertainty (engineering), and known vs unknown unknowns. Most real decisions are mysteries mis-handled as puzzles.

## Mechanism / Why It Matters
The puzzle/mystery line tells you which toolkit is even admissible. For puzzles, more data and sharper computation converge on the right answer, and probabilistic methods are legitimate. For mysteries, more data does not converge anything because there is no fixed answer to converge on; the leverage is in framing, judgment and robustness. Treating a mystery as a puzzle is the central methodological error of the book - it generates false-precision answers to questions that have no correct answer, and it crowds out the comprehension work that mysteries actually require. The cut's authority is amplified by cross-domain consilience: four independent fields (intelligence analysis, design theory, engineering, defence) arrive at the same two-class structure without borrowing from one another, which is strong evidence the distinction tracks something real rather than disciplinary convention.

## Evidence
"A puzzle has well-defined rules and a single solution... Mysteries offer no such clarity of definition, and no objectively correct solution... We approach mysteries by asking 'What is going on here?'"; "life comprises many more mysteries than puzzles." (Ch. 9, on Gregory Treverton's distinction)
