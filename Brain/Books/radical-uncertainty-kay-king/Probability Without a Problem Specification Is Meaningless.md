# Probability Without a Problem Specification Is Meaningless

**Source**: John Kay & Mervyn King, *Radical Uncertainty: Decision-Making Beyond the Numbers* (W. W. Norton, 2020) - Ch. 23
**Document Type**: Book
**Extracted By**: AI (document-insight-extractor, Book Mode)
**Extraction Date**: 2026-06-28
**Scope**: Books/radical-uncertainty-kay-king

## Core Insight

A probability number is undefined until the question it is supposed to answer has been precisely specified. Under radical uncertainty the same event can be assigned wildly different "probabilities" depending on trivial framing details, which means the number tracks the specification, not the world. The problem-specification step both PRECEDES and DOMINATES any probability assignment - get the question wrong and no amount of probabilistic sophistication can rescue the answer.

## Mechanism / Why It Matters

Probability statements are answers, and an answer is empty until its question is fixed. In a small world the question is implicit and stable, so the specification step is invisible and can be skipped. In a large world the question is genuinely ambiguous, and the act of specifying it does most of the actual work - while the subsequent probability arithmetic merely inherits whatever was decided at the specification stage. Because subjective probabilities are sensitive to trivial information and to incidental details of how the problem is posed, two analysts who agree on all the facts can still produce different numbers simply because they fixed different questions.

The practical consequence is that quoting a probability without stating its problem specification is not rigour, it is noise dressed as precision. The danger is acute precisely where the question is hardest to pin down - which is also where decision-makers most crave a number. The discipline Kay & King urge is to spend the effort on "what exactly is the question here?" before, and instead of, refining the decimal places of an answer to an unstated one.

## Evidence

Kay & King, Ch. 23, on Nate Silver's retrospective assignment of a probability to the 9/11 attacks: "But what is the question to which this number is the answer? Is it the probability of an attack that morning? That day? That year? At all?... Without a clear problem specification, there is no reason to anticipate meaningful, consistent or useful answers to questions about probability." Reinforced by the classic two-child problem, where the "probability the other child is a boy" shifts with incidental phrasing: "subjective probabilities are necessarily sensitive to trivial information and details of problem specification." These are demonstrating cases for a conceptual claim, not statistical findings.
