# Bogus Quantification - A Number Does Not Add Knowledge

**Source**: John Kay & Mervyn King, *Radical Uncertainty: Decision-Making Beyond the Numbers* (W. W. Norton, 2020) - Appendix, "Ambiguity and Vagueness"
**Document Type**: Book
**Extracted By**: AI (document-insight-extractor, Book Mode)
**Extraction Date**: 2026-06-28
**Scope**: Books/radical-uncertainty-kay-king

## Core Insight

Putting a numerical probability on an inherently qualitative judgement - "95% certain you moved the ball" - confers false objectivity and the appearance of scientific precision WITHOUT adding any information. The number is theatre: it borrows the authority of science while contributing nothing the qualitative assessment did not already contain. "If you cannot measure, measure anyhow" is a pernicious modern reflex masquerading as rigour.

## Mechanism / Why It Matters

The error is not merely useless but actively misleading. A qualitative judgement honestly signals that it is a judgement; once a number is attached, downstream users treat it as a measurement - an objective fact about the world rather than a subjective estimate. The quantification launders the judgement's epistemic status, hiding the uncertainty it was supposed to express. The Royal and Ancient Golf Club fell into exactly this trap, believing a number gave their ruling an objectivity a qualitative call would have lacked. This is the operational danger: bogus quantification disguises the absence of knowledge as the presence of it, and decisions then inherit a confidence the underlying judgement never warranted.

## Evidence

Kay & King: "The Royal and Ancient Golf Club had fallen into a common and modern trap of bogus quantification. The authors of the rule believed that attaching a number to their judgement gave it an objectivity and scientific precision which a qualitative assessment would have lacked." They trace the reflex to Frank Knight's caustic gloss on Lord Kelvin's measurement creed: "if you cannot measure, measure anyhow!" The argument is conceptual rather than empirical - a critique of a category error in how numbers are used.
