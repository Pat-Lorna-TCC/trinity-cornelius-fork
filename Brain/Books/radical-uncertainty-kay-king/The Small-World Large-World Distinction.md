# The Small-World Large-World Distinction

**Source**: John Kay & Mervyn King, *Radical Uncertainty: Decision-Making Beyond the Numbers* (W. W. Norton, 2020) - Ch. 7
**Document Type**: Book
**Extracted By**: AI (document-insight-extractor, Book Mode)
**Extraction Date**: 2026-06-28
**Scope**: Books/radical-uncertainty-kay-king

## Core Insight

Optimisation and subjective-expected-utility maximisation presuppose a CLOSED, fully-enumerated problem space - what Leonard Savage called a "small world," in which every relevant state and option is already listed. Most consequential decisions occur in a "large world" of unknown unknowns, where the option set itself is incomplete. There, no optimum can be computed over a space you cannot specify; the only way to resolve the residual is to act and find out.

## Mechanism / Why It Matters

The mathematics of expected-utility maximisation requires an exhaustive list of states of the world and their consequences. That requirement is satisfiable only in a small world - a board game, a casino bet, an actuarial table. In a large world, the consequential gap is not missing probabilities over known outcomes but missing outcomes: the engineering "unk-unks" you have not imagined and therefore cannot have enumerated. Optimising over an incomplete option set produces an answer that is precise about the wrong problem.

The decisive tell is that Savage himself regarded extending his axioms beyond small worlds as "preposterous" / unreasonable - the originator of the formal apparatus drew the boundary that later users ignored. Kay & King's practical corollary is that in a large world the resolution mechanism is empirical, not computational: you act, observe, and revise the option set. Boeing's engineers used "unknown unknowns" / "unk-unks" decades before the phrase entered public discourse precisely because aircraft design is a large-world problem - the only way to learn what a metal tube does at 500 mph and 35,000 feet is to fly it.

## Evidence

Kay & King, Ch. 7. Savage's small-world/large-world distinction from *The Foundations of Statistics* (1954), with Savage's own warning against applying the axioms outside the small world. Engineering cases: Boeing's "unknown unknowns / unk-unks" terminology, predating Rumsfeld's 2002 remark by roughly 50 years; the de Havilland Comet's square windows, whose fatigue-cracking failure mode was an unk-unk not on any pre-flight option list. Quote: "the only way to resolve the mystery of what happens to a metal tube flying at 500 mph at 35,000 feet is to try it." These are illustrative cases supporting a conceptual claim, not a statistical result.
