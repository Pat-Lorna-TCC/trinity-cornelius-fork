# The Optimising Model of Rationality Fails Under an Unknowable Future
**Source**: John Kay & Mervyn King, *Radical Uncertainty: Decision-Making Beyond the Numbers* (W. W. Norton, 2020) - Preface / Ch. 1
**Document Type**: Book
**Extracted By**: AI (document-insight-extractor, Book Mode)
**Extraction Date**: 2026-06-28
**Scope**: Books/radical-uncertainty-kay-king

## Core Insight
The axiomatic "agents maximise X subject to constraints" framework breaks down NOT because decision-makers are irrational but because "maximise shareholder value / utility / social welfare" is not a coherent guide to action when the future cannot be described, let alone assigned probabilities. The information optimisation assumes could not even be imagined, so the maximand is undefined.

## Mechanism / Why It Matters
Optimisation requires a fully specified objective function and a complete enumeration of states of the world with probabilities attached. Under radical uncertainty both inputs are missing: the state space is open, and the maximand ("shareholder value", "utility") has no determinate referent across futures that cannot be described. The failure is therefore structural, not a matter of cognitive limitation - the rational agent declines to pretend to knowledge it could not possess. The error this guards against is the reflex, deep in economics and decision theory, of reading any deviation from the optimisation model as a "bias" to be corrected, when the model itself is the thing that does not apply. It transfers anywhere a global objective is asserted over an unknowable future: corporate strategy, central banking, public policy.

## Evidence
The axiomatic approach "comprehensively fails when applied to decisions... about an uncertain future... not because these economic actors are irrational, but because they are rational, and... do not pretend to knowledge they do not and could not have"; "an injunction to maximise shareholder value... is not a coherent guide to action." (Preface / Ch. 1)
