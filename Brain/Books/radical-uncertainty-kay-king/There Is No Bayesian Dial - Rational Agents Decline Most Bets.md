# There Is No Bayesian Dial - Rational Agents Decline Most Bets

**Source**: John Kay & Mervyn King, *Radical Uncertainty: Decision-Making Beyond the Numbers* (W. W. Norton, 2020) - Ch. 22
**Document Type**: Book
**Extracted By**: AI (document-insight-extractor, Book Mode)
**Extraction Date**: 2026-06-28
**Scope**: Books/radical-uncertainty-kay-king

## Core Insight

Real decision-makers do not carry a consistent set of subjective probabilities over all events that they read off like a dial whenever asked to bet. Rational behaviour under radical uncertainty is frequently to REFUSE to bet - to decline participation when information is imperfect or asymmetric - which is the opposite of the Ramsey / de Finetti "Dutch book" model that infers a probability from every willingness to wager. People act only once they have built sufficient confidence in a narrative, not because an internal probability crossed a threshold.

## Mechanism / Why It Matters

The Dutch-book construction treats willingness to bet at given odds as revealing a coherent subjective probability, and assumes such a probability exists for every proposition. Kay & King invert the logic: there is no Bayesian dial to read, because for most real propositions people simply have no probability at all - and the rational response to that absence is abstention, not the manufacture of a number. When information is imperfect and may differ from what a counterparty holds, accepting a bet exposes you to adverse selection; declining is the calibrated move.

This reframes "rationality" away from always having a price and toward knowing when to have no opinion. Decision follows narrative confidence, not probabilistic threshold: agents act when "what is going on here" has cohered enough to warrant action, and otherwise wait. Held against the indifference-gamble construction of utility, this is a PRODUCTIVE TENSION - that construction assumes the agent always has a coherent indifference point to elicit, whereas Kay & King argue the honest agent often has none and rightly refuses to be drawn.

## Evidence

Kay & King, Ch. 22. "They do not act until they have sufficient confidence in the narrative they have constructed. There is no Bayesian dial." And: "Rational people will decline to participate in any proposed wager when their information is imperfect and may differ from that held by other people." Illustrated by Warren Buffett's no-called-strikes image of investing: "you never have to swing... All day you wait for the pitch you like." These are argument and illustrative cases, not statistical results.
