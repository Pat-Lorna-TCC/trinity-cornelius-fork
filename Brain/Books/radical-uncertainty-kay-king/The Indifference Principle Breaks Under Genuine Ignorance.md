# The Indifference Principle Breaks Under Genuine Ignorance

**Source**: John Kay & Mervyn King, *Radical Uncertainty: Decision-Making Beyond the Numbers* (W. W. Norton, 2020) - Ch. 18 / 19
**Document Type**: Book
**Extracted By**: AI (document-insight-extractor, Book Mode)
**Extraction Date**: 2026-06-28
**Scope**: Books/radical-uncertainty-kay-king

## Core Insight

The principle of indifference - assign equal probabilities "because we have no reason to prefer one outcome over another" - generates logical contradictions whenever the outcome space can be carved up in more than one way. The supposedly neutral move of spreading probability uniformly is not neutral at all; it is parasitic on an arbitrary choice of partition. Under genuine ignorance the only honest answer is "I do not know," not a uniform prior dressed up as knowledge.

## Mechanism / Why It Matters

A uniform prior looks like the canonical expression of having no information. But "equal probability to each outcome" requires first deciding what counts as an outcome, and different partitions of the same ignorance yield mutually inconsistent assignments. Keynes's example: from total ignorance of where a man lives, he is "as likely to inhabit France as Britain," and also "as likely to inhabit France as the British Isles" - two indifference judgments that cannot both hold, since the British Isles strictly contain Britain. The contradiction is not a quirk; it is structural to any attempt to manufacture a probability distribution out of the absence of information.

This matters because it removes the last refuge of false precision. The move "we don't know, so let's assume uniform" is frequently treated as the rigorous, conservative default. Kay & King show it smuggles in unwarranted structure: the answer depends entirely on the analyst's arbitrary framing of the state space. Genuine ignorance has no probability distribution; pretending otherwise converts a confession of not-knowing into a spurious number.

## Evidence

Kay & King, Ch. 18 / 19, quoting Keynes, *A Treatise on Probability* (1921), where Keynes both names the principle of indifference and exposes its partition-dependence. Keynes's epigram on the principle's overreach: "No other formula in the alchemy of logic has exerted more astonishing powers. For it has established the existence of God from the premiss of total ignorance." The France/Britain/British Isles partition is the canonical demonstrating case. This is a logical/conceptual result, not an empirical one.
