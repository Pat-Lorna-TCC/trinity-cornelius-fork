# Axiomatic versus Evolutionary Rationality

**Source**: John Kay & Mervyn King, *Radical Uncertainty: Decision-Making Beyond the Numbers* (W. W. Norton, 2020) - Ch. 4 (Proposition 2)
**Document Type**: Book
**Extracted By**: AI (document-insight-extractor, Book Mode)
**Extraction Date**: 2026-06-28
**Scope**: Books/radical-uncertainty-kay-king

## Core Insight

Kay & King reject the optimisation-versus-bias frame entirely. Humans neither optimise nor are they "irrational." They draw a distinction between AXIOMATIC rationality - the economist's internally-consistent optimiser whose choices satisfy a set of consistency axioms - and EVOLUTIONARY rationality, the behaviour that actually survives and propagates in a world of radical uncertainty. Many of the "biases" catalogued by behavioural science are not defects measured against a small-world benchmark; they are adaptive responses to a large world that cannot be fully specified.

## Mechanism / Why It Matters

Axiomatic rationality declares a behaviour rational if it is internally consistent against a closed, enumerated set of states and probabilities. But that benchmark only exists in a "small world." In a large world of radical uncertainty, consistency against a fully-specified state space is unavailable, so the axiomatic test itself loses meaning - and behaviour that fails it may nonetheless be exactly what evolution shaped for coping.

This directly challenges the Kahneman heuristics-and-biases program and is best held as a PRODUCTIVE TENSION rather than a refutation. Where that program reads a deviation from the normative optimum as an error to be corrected, Kay & King read the same deviation as plausibly adaptive: a heuristic that performs well across the open-ended set of real situations an agent faces, even if it underperforms on a contrived small-world test. The disagreement is not about the data - the behaviours are real - but about the benchmark. Calling a response a "bias" presupposes the small-world optimum is the correct standard; that presupposition is what Kay & King contest.

## Evidence

Kay & King, Ch. 4, Proposition 2: "individuals cannot and do not optimise; nor are they irrational, victims of 'biases'... We distinguish axiomatic rationality, as used by economists, from evolutionary rationality, as practised by people. Many so-called 'biases' are responses to the complex world of radical uncertainty." This is an argument-level claim (a reframing of decades of behavioural findings), not a single empirical result; its force is conceptual rather than statistical.
