# True Black Swans Are Unimaginable Not Low-Probability

**Source**: John Kay & Mervyn King, *Radical Uncertainty: Decision-Making Beyond the Numbers* (W. W. Norton, 2020) - Ch. 11 / 14, "Unknown unknowns"
**Document Type**: Book
**Extracted By**: AI (document-insight-extractor, Book Mode)
**Extraction Date**: 2026-06-28
**Scope**: Books/radical-uncertainty-kay-king

## Core Insight

A genuine unknown unknown is not a tail event on a known distribution but a state of the world you cannot CONCEIVE - and to which, therefore, no probability however small can be coherently attached. Kay & King's distinct refinement of Taleb: the defining property is unconceivability, not rarity. The probability question is not merely hard but incoherent for the genuinely unimaginable - "to identify a probability of inventing the wheel is to invent the wheel."

## Mechanism / Why It Matters

The low-probability framing smuggles in the very thing that is absent: a specified outcome space over which a small number can be defined. But if you could enumerate the outcome and assign it 0.001, you would already have conceived it - and it would no longer be an unknown unknown. The wheel example makes this exact: to state the probability of the invention you must already hold its concept, which is the invention. This collapses any attempt to "fat-tail" your way to safety against true black swans - you cannot reserve probability mass for states you cannot describe. The practical consequence is that robustness and resilience, not probabilistic provisioning, are the only defences against the genuinely unimaginable.

## Evidence

Kay & King: "the observation of a black swan was not a low-probability event; it was an unimaginable event"; and on the conceivability collapse, "to identify a probability of inventing the wheel is to invent the wheel." The argument is conceptual - a critique of the category, demonstrating that probability is undefined where the outcome space cannot be specified - rather than an empirical claim about frequencies.
