# Dennett's Rapoport Rules - Charitable Argument Before Critique

## Core Framework

Philosopher Daniel Dennett formalized rules for intellectual debate originally articulated by the social psychologist Anatol Rapoport. The rules form a **mandatory sequence before critique is permitted**:

1. **Summarize the opponent's position** so accurately and completely that they say: "Thank you - I wish I'd put it that way." Not a charitable paraphrase; a genuinely superior rendering.
2. **List points of agreement** between yourself and your opponent (especially agreements that are not trivial or widely shared).
3. **Acknowledge what you learned** from the position you are about to critique. Something specific, not performative.
4. **Then, and only then**, criticize.

---

## Why This Order Matters

The sequence is not merely polite; it is epistemically necessary. Steps 1-3 require you to genuinely understand the position before you evaluate it - which is Adler's reading rule applied to arguments. If you cannot successfully complete Step 1, you are not yet in a position to argue with the conclusion.

Most debate begins at Step 4. The result is that arguers are typically responding to a caricature of the opponent's position (the straw man) rather than the position itself. Rapoport's Rules make this invisible substitution visible.

---

## Tribalism and the Straw Man Problem

Dennett's observation: **we systematically misrepresent the positions of people we disagree with**. This is not primarily dishonesty - it is a product of tribal cognition. Once you identify an out-group, you perceive their arguments through a lens of motivated disconfirmation. Rapoport's Rules are a structural countermeasure: you cannot complete Step 1 through motivated misreading.

This connects to Haidt's work: the emotional position comes first (the elephant moves), and then the reasoning is constructed to justify it (the rider rationalizes). Rapoport's Rules interrupt this process by requiring genuine comprehension before evaluation is permitted.

---

## The "Surely" Tell

Dennett also identifies a diagnostic: when you see the word **"surely"** in an argument, pause. "Surely" is deployed when the writer knows their claim needs support but doesn't have it. It is an appeal to the reader's intuition as a substitute for evidence. Trained readers of philosophy spot this as a warning sign.
