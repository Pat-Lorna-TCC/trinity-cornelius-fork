# Shiller's Narrative Contagion of Speculative Bubbles

**Source**: Russell, J.M. (2020). *A Brief Guide to Smart Thinking*, pp. 81-87, summarizing Shiller, R. (2000). *Irrational Exuberance*. Princeton University Press. (Nobel Prize in Economics 2013 for empirical analysis of asset prices.)

## Core Framework

Shiller's argument against efficient market hypothesis (EMH) in the context of speculative bubbles: **asset prices contain a substantial component that is not determined by rational discounting of future cash flows, but by social contagion of attention and narrative**.

His explanation of bubble formation as a self-reinforcing feedback loop:

1. **Price increase** (for any reason, including random noise)
2. **Media coverage** of the price increase
3. **Investor enthusiasm** generated by stories of people making money
4. **New investor entry** - people who previously sat out now participate
5. **More stories** (more people are in the market and have gains to report)
6. **Further price increase** → loop repeats
7. **Critical mass failure** - some shock (or simple exhaustion of new buyers) breaks the loop; rapid exit triggers collapse

---

## The Efficient Market Hypothesis Critique

EMH claims that prices at any moment fully reflect all available information. Shiller's counter: if prices were purely information-driven, we would not see the magnitude of boom-bust cycles observed in stock markets, housing markets, and emerging market assets. The excess volatility - far greater than can be explained by variation in dividends or fundamentals - requires a social-psychological explanation.

This is not merely a theoretical claim: Shiller predicted the 2000 dot-com crash (the book was published in March 2000, the market peaked simultaneously) and the 2008 housing collapse (based on the same feedback-loop analysis applied to housing prices from 2004 onwards).

---

## Narrative Economics

Shiller later developed this into "Narrative Economics" (2019): the specific stories (narratives) that circulate in a culture drive economic behavior at scale. An investment narrative ("housing prices have never fallen nationally") spreads virally and alters the aggregate behavior of millions of economic actors simultaneously, producing macro effects that no individual rational agent intended.

The mechanism is the same as epidemic contagion: narratives spread through social contact, with a reproduction number (how many new adopters does each adopter generate), and can reach epidemic or sub-epidemic scale.
