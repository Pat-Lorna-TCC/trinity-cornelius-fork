# Adler Four Levels of Reading - Structural, Interpretive, Critical, Syntopical

## Core Framework

Adler distinguishes four ascending levels of reading engagement, each requiring the previous:

| Level | Name | Core Question | What You Do |
|-------|------|--------------|-------------|
| 1 | **Elementary** | What does it say? | Decode the words |
| 2 | **Structural** (Inspectional) | What is the book about as a whole? | Survey structure, outline, unity of the argument |
| 3 | **Analytical** (Interpretive + Critical) | What is being said in detail, and is it true? | Identify key terms, propositions, arguments; then evaluate |
| 4 | **Syntopical** | What do multiple books collectively illuminate? | Compare across sources; build your own framework |

---

## Analytical Reading - The Three Sub-Moves

Level 3 has three distinguishable moves that must occur in sequence:

1. **Structural**: Understand the book's overall logic and architecture before engaging parts
2. **Interpretive**: Identify key terms, propositions (the author's claims), and arguments (how claims are connected)
3. **Critical**: Engage and evaluate - agree, disagree, or suspend judgment - but ONLY after completing interpretive reading

Adler's rule: you cannot legitimately criticize a book you have not fully understood. Premature critique is not critical thinking - it is a defense mechanism against comprehension.

---

## Syntopical Reading - The Cross-Source Method

The most demanding level. When reading across multiple sources on a single theme:

1. **Build a bibliography**: identify all relevant texts on the question before reading any deeply
2. **Inspect before reading**: survey each text first to confirm relevance and identify which passages actually address your question
3. **Reformulate the question**: translate the author's language into your own neutral terms (authors on the same theme use different vocabulary for the same concept)
4. **Identify and compare passages**: extract and juxtapose what each author says about the shared theme
5. **Define the issues**: where authors disagree, name the issue explicitly - what is actually in dispute?
6. **Analyze the discussion**: arrange positions so the underlying logic of each becomes clear
7. **Formulate your own view**: synthesize across positions rather than simply reporting them

The key insight: syntopical reading constructs a framework that **none of the individual books contains**. The reader becomes the author of a new text composed from multiple sources.
