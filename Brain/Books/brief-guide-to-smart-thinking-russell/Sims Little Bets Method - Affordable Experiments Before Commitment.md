# Sims' Little Bets Method - Affordable Experiments Before Commitment

## Core Framework

The conventional creative model: **conceive the brilliant idea → then execute it**.

The empirical pattern Sims documents across successful innovators: **run small affordable experiments → let the brilliant idea emerge from them**.

The difference is epistemological. Before execution, you cannot fully know which idea is brilliant. Small experiments are the mechanism by which you find out at low cost.

---

## The Little Bets Principle

A "little bet" has three properties:
1. **Affordable to lose** - failure costs little enough that it does not kill the project or the person
2. **Feedback-rich** - it generates real information about whether the underlying assumption holds
3. **Fast** - quick enough to permit many iterations before resource exhaustion

The constraint is not budget minimization - it is that the bet must be small enough that its failure is a data point rather than a disaster.

---

## Case Study: Chris Rock's Method

Before a major tour, Chris Rock performs unannounced at a small New Jersey club. He works through new material joke by joke, carrying a notepad, watching audience reaction. Most material fails. The jokes that get genuine laughter are retained; the rest are abandoned. By tour time, the material is proven.

**The principle**: the comedian does not know in advance which jokes are funny. The audience decides, not him. The small-venue tests are essential to discover what he cannot predict.

---

## Pixar's Structural Application

Pixar runs short films as experimental vehicles before committing to feature production. The shorts serve the same function as Rock's club sets: low-stakes environments that generate real creative and technical learning.

The key phrase (from Sims): brilliant ideas are discovered **on the journey**, not conceived **before** it.

---

## Affordable Loss vs Expected Value

The little bets logic is explicitly not expected-value maximization. It is **affordable-loss framing**: focus on what you can afford to lose, not on projecting end-state gains. This reduces the cognitive and emotional cost of failure sufficiently to permit experimentation.

This connects to effectuation theory (Sarasvathy, 2001) - expert entrepreneurs use "affordable loss" framing, not "maximize expected return" framing.
