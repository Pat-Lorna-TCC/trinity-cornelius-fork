# Foer's Method of Loci - Visual-Spatial Memory Superiority

## Core Principle

Humans are poor at memorizing abstract symbols (names, phone numbers, lists of words), but have **remarkably good visual-spatial memory** - the capacity to remember detailed spatial layouts, images, and routes. This asymmetry is the basis for every effective mnemonic system.

Foer: "As bad as we are at remembering names and phone numbers... we have really exceptional visual and spatial memories."

The architectural technique known as the Method of Loci exploits this asymmetry systematically.

---

## The Method of Loci (Memory Palace)

**Origin**: Attributed to the Greek poet Simonides of Ceos (~5th-6th century BCE), who reportedly survived a banquet hall collapse and could identify all the buried dead by visualizing where each person had been seated.

**Mechanism**:
1. Select a familiar spatial route (your childhood home, a regular walk, a building you know well)
2. Identify a sequence of distinct **loci** (locations) along the route - specific spots in a specific order
3. Convert each piece of information to be memorized into a **vivid, memorable image** (bizarre, lewd, emotionally striking images are retained better than neutral ones)
4. **Place** each image at a specific locus in the route, using the image in interaction with the space
5. To recall, mentally **walk the route** and observe what is at each locus

The technique transfers the cognitive load from abstract verbal memory (weak) to episodic visual-spatial memory (strong).

---

## Why Bizarreness Works

The images work best when they are strange, exaggerated, or emotionally charged. This is not arbitrariness - it reflects the evolutionary priority of episodic memory for survival-relevant events. Banal information is tagged as low-relevance by the encoding system; surprising or emotionally significant information triggers more thorough encoding.

---

## Scope

The method of loci is not limited to memory competitions. It is used for:
- Long speeches and sermons (classical orators)
- Medical memorization (anatomy sequences)
- Language vocabulary in unfamiliar scripts
- Card sequences and numerical sequences
