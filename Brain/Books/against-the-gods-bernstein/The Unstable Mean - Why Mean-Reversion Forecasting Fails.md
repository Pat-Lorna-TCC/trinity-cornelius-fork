# The Unstable Mean - Why Mean-Reversion Forecasting Fails
**Source**: Peter L. Bernstein, *Against the Gods: The Remarkable Story of Risk* (Wiley, 1996) - Ch. 11 (Bernstein's framing; Hoover and 1959 yield-reversal cases, stripped to principle)
**Document Type**: Book
**Extracted By**: AI (document-insight-extractor, Book Mode)
**Extraction Date**: 2026-06-28
**Scope**: Books/against-the-gods-bernstein

## Core Insight
Regression to the mean is a tool, not a law. It misleads under three conditions, the most dangerous being that the mean itself can shift to a new location. "Yesterday's normality may be supplanted today by a new normality we know nothing about." Applying mean-reversion mechanically assumes a fixed target that may no longer exist.

## Mechanism / Why It Matters
Bernstein identifies three failure modes of regression as a forecasting device:
1. **Too slow** - regression toward the mean is so gradual that a fresh shock disrupts the process before it can complete.
2. **Overshoot** - the variable oscillates around the mean rather than settling at it, so betting on convergence at any moment is betting on timing you do not control.
3. **Shifting mean** - the mean is in flux; the center you are regressing toward has itself moved, making the historical average a fictional anchor.

The transferable rule: never apply mean-reversion mechanically. Before betting that something will revert, ask whether the mean it would revert *to* still exists. This is the caveat that converts regression from dogma into a usable instrument, and it is fundamentally a statement about regime change and structural breaks.

## Evidence
Bernstein illustrates with cases of economic and market variables (e.g., the 1959 bond-yield reversal and Hoover-era expectations) where confident mean-reversion bets failed because the underlying regime had shifted - the "normal" level participants were anchored to had been replaced. Stripped to principle: the empirical record shows mean-reversion strategies breaking precisely when structural conditions change.
