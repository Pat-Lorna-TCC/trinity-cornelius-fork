# Risk versus Genuine Uncertainty
**Source**: Peter L. Bernstein, *Against the Gods: The Remarkable Story of Risk* (Wiley, 1996) — Ch. 13-14 (Knight, *Risk, Uncertainty and Profit*, 1921; Keynes, *A Treatise on Probability*, 1921 & 1937)
**Document Type**: Book
**Extracted By**: AI (document-insight-extractor, Book Mode)
**Extraction Date**: 2026-06-28
**Scope**: Books/against-the-gods-bernstein

## Core Insight
Frank Knight (1921) split uncertainty in two. *Risk* is measurable uncertainty - the odds are knowable, as in games of chance. *Genuine uncertainty* is unmeasurable - no probability distribution can be assigned at all. In Knight's words, "a measurable uncertainty, or 'risk' proper, is so far different from an unmeasurable one that it is not in effect an uncertainty at all." This is the foundational boundary of all risk thinking: situations you can model probabilistically versus situations where the model itself does not exist.

## Mechanism / Why It Matters
Two supporting mechanisms make the boundary bite. (a) Knight's **uniqueness problem**: real economic events are each so singular that there are never enough comparable cases to compute a genuine frequency, and because the world is non-stationary, "what was 75% probable yesterday has an unknown probability tomorrow." (b) Keynes's **"we simply do not know"**: for many questions - a future war, the copper price in twenty years - "there is no scientific basis on which to form any calculable probability whatever." Keynes reframed probability as a *degree of belief* about a proposition, not a frequency. The transferable error this guards against: treating uncertainty as if it were risk. Plugging false precision into a domain where no distribution exists is the root of most quantitative overconfidence - the model returns a confident number that the world never authored.

## Evidence
Knight, *Risk, Uncertainty and Profit* (1921): distinguishes insurable, computable risk from the unmeasurable uncertainty that is the true source of entrepreneurial profit. Keynes, *A Treatise on Probability* (1921) and his 1937 restatement: probability as rational degree of belief; for many real questions no calculable probability exists. This is a shared anchor concept - the not-yet-ingested *Radical Uncertainty* (Kay & King) later extends it.
