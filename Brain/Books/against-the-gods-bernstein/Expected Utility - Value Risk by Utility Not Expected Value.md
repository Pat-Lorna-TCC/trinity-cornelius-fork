# Expected Utility - Value Risk by Utility Not Expected Value
**Source**: Peter L. Bernstein, *Against the Gods: The Remarkable Story of Risk* (Wiley, 1996) — Ch. 6-7 (Daniel Bernoulli, 1738 St. Petersburg paper)
**Document Type**: Book
**Extracted By**: AI (document-insight-extractor, Book Mode)
**Extraction Date**: 2026-06-28
**Scope**: Books/against-the-gods-bernstein

## Core Insight
Daniel Bernoulli (1738) showed that a risky prospect must be valued by the *utility* it yields to a specific person, not by its objective monetary expected value. The rational agent maximizes expected utility, not expected value. This single move converts risk from a fact-to-be-computed into a choice-to-be-evaluated, and is the hinge that connects probability theory to all of decision science.

## Mechanism / Why It Matters
The engine is **diminishing marginal utility**: each added unit of wealth yields less satisfaction than the last. Bernstein's image is a pile of bricks - the brick added to a large pile matters less than the same brick added to a small one. Because utility is concave in wealth, the curve bends, and the same gamble is worth different amounts to different people depending on where they sit on it. A poor person and a rich person facing an identical bet are facing genuinely different decisions.

The operational tool is the **certainty equivalent**: the sure sum that makes you indifferent between taking the gamble and pocketing the cash. The gap between a gamble's expected value and its (lower) certainty equivalent is the **risk premium** - a measurable price for bearing uncertainty. This is why risk preference is not irrationality to be corrected but a structural feature of a concave utility curve.

## Evidence
Daniel Bernoulli's 1738 St. Petersburg memoir introduced the principle that "the value of an item must not be based on its price, but rather on the utility it yields," and that utility rises with wealth at a diminishing rate. The framework resolved the St. Petersburg Paradox and seeded the entire expected-utility tradition formalized later by von Neumann and Morgenstern (1944).
