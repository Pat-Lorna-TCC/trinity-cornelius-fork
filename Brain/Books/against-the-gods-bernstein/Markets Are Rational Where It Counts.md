# Markets Are Rational Where It Counts
**Source**: Peter L. Bernstein, *Against the Gods: The Remarkable Story of Risk* (Wiley, 1996) — Ch. 17-18 ("where it really counts"; Cowles; Samuelson)
**Document Type**: Book
**Extracted By**: AI (document-insight-extractor, Book Mode)
**Extraction Date**: 2026-06-28
**Scope**: Books/against-the-gods-bernstein

## Core Insight
Even when millions of investors demonstrably violate the axioms of rational choice, the market behaves AS IF rationality prevailed — because the exploitable opportunities that irrationality creates are too few, too small, or too fleeting to be profited from consistently. This reconciles behavioral finance with the Efficient Market Hypothesis rather than treating them as rivals.

## Mechanism / Why It Matters
The argument is no-arbitrage plus emergence: individual-level irrationality does not propagate to the aggregate when the cost of betting against it exceeds the edge it offers. Each behavioral bias is real at the level of the individual, but to convert it into profit you must identify it, trade it, and bear the frictions before it closes — and on average that net edge is gone. So the macro behaves rationally not because the micro is rational, but because the micro's deviations are not reliably harvestable. The KB lacked exactly this reconciliation: the bridge between the documented reality of bias and the empirical reality of market efficiency.

## Evidence
The empirical anchor is that most active managers underperform the index over time — if mispricings created by irrationality were systematically exploitable, skilled professionals would capture them and beat the market, and they do not. Bernstein traces the lineage through Cowles (1930s, finding forecasters could not beat the market) and Samuelson (rigorous statement of why correctly anticipated prices fluctuate randomly), concluding that markets are rational "where it really counts" even amid pervasive individual irrationality.
