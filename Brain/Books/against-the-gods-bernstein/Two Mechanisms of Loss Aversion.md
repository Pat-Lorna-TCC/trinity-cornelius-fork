# Two Mechanisms of Loss Aversion
**Source**: Peter L. Bernstein, *Against the Gods: The Remarkable Story of Risk* (Wiley, 1996) — Ch. 6-7 (Daniel Bernoulli) + Ch. 11 (Jevons / Bentham)
**Document Type**: Book
**Extracted By**: AI (document-insight-extractor, Book Mode)
**Extraction Date**: 2026-06-28
**Scope**: Books/against-the-gods-bernstein

## Core Insight
The same observed behavior - a loss hurts more than an equal gain pleases - can be produced by two genuinely different mechanisms. Daniel Bernoulli (1738) DERIVED loss aversion from the **global curvature** of a single concave utility-of-wealth function. Kahneman and Tversky derive it from **reference-dependence and a kink** at the current reference point. This note extends Loss Aversion; it does not restate the phenomenon, only contrasts its two engines.

## Mechanism / Why It Matters
- **Bernoulli's engine (curvature):** there is one smooth utility curve over total wealth, concave throughout. Removing a brick from the top of the pile subtracts more utility than the next brick added contributes. Loss aversion falls out as a consequence of the curve's shape across your whole wealth level - no special status for the present moment.
- **Kahneman-Tversky's engine (reference + kink):** value is defined over *changes* from a reference point, not total wealth, and the value function bends sharply at that reference point so the loss limb is steeper than the gain limb. The asymmetry is local and reference-relative, not a property of global wealth.

The distinction matters because the two engines predict differently: a curvature account ties asymmetry to absolute wealth, while a reference account makes it shift with whatever you currently treat as the baseline (which is manipulable - the framing effect).

Bernstein's striking historical point: although Bernoulli's concave utility implicitly contained loss aversion, the word "loss" did not appear in utility theory for roughly **200 years** after 1738. The blind spot was conceptual, not mathematical - the asymmetry was sitting in the equations, unnamed, until reference-dependence made it visible.

## Evidence
Daniel Bernoulli (1738) concave utility; Jeremy Bentham and W. S. Jevons (Ch. 11) developing utility into a felt psychological quantity; Kahneman and Tversky's prospect theory (1979) supplying the reference-dependent account.
