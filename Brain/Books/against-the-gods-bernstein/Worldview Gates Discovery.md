# Worldview Gates Discovery
**Source**: Peter L. Bernstein, *Against the Gods: The Remarkable Story of Risk* (Wiley, 1996) - Ch. 2-4
**Document Type**: Book
**Extracted By**: AI (document-insight-extractor, Book Mode)
**Extraction Date**: 2026-06-28
**Scope**: Books/against-the-gods-bernstein

## Core Insight
The Greeks, Arabs, and early Christians all possessed the mathematical firepower to invent probability, yet for roughly 2,000 years they did not. Their fatalistic worldview - the future belongs to the gods or to fate - made measuring the future literally unthinkable. The missing ingredient was not an equation but a belief in human agency, supplied later by the Renaissance and Reformation.

## Mechanism / Why It Matters
Technical capability is necessary but not sufficient for a conceptual breakthrough. The binding constraint is often the prevailing frame about what is even worth asking, not the available tooling. A civilization with the algebra to compute odds will still not compute them if it believes the future is sealed. The concrete agency mechanism Bernstein identifies: the Reformation removed the confessional, forcing people to own the consequences of their own decisions - which made the future feel like something individuals act upon rather than something handed down.

## Evidence
Bernstein contrasts Greek and Arab mathematical sophistication (they had the numeral systems and combinatorial reach) with their total absence of a probability theory (Ch. 2-4). The gap closes only when the Renaissance-Reformation worldview supplies human agency. The confessional point is his concrete causal claim: removing institutionalized absolution shifted responsibility for outcomes onto the decision-maker.
