# Ambiguity Aversion and the Competence Hypothesis
**Source**: Peter L. Bernstein, *Against the Gods: The Remarkable Story of Risk* (Wiley, 1996) — Ch. 16-17 (Ellsberg, 1961; Tversky and Fox)
**Document Type**: Book
**Extracted By**: AI (document-insight-extractor, Book Mode)
**Extraction Date**: 2026-06-28
**Scope**: Books/against-the-gods-bernstein

## Core Insight
People prefer to bet on KNOWN probabilities over UNKNOWN ones even when the math is identical (Ellsberg's two-urn paradox, 1961). But this aversion REVERSES where people feel competent: they will bet on vague, unquantified beliefs in domains they know well — sports, politics — while retreating to pure chance where they feel ignorant (Tversky and Fox).

## Mechanism / Why It Matters
This distinguishes Knightian AMBIGUITY (probabilities themselves unknown) from mere RISK (probabilities known). The aversion to ambiguity is not driven by the odds but by "the feeling of incompetence" — a meta-belief about whether one's own probability estimate can be trusted. The competence reversal shows the feeling is the operative variable, not the information: hand someone identical uncertainty in a domain they feel expert in and the aversion flips to appetite. Transferable to forecasting and confidence calibration: willingness to bet is a function of perceived expertise, not just information held — which means felt competence can both motivate good bets and mask overconfidence.

## Evidence
Ellsberg (1961): given one urn with a known 50/50 split and one with an unknown split, subjects systematically pay to bet on the known urn though the expected values are equal — a violation of expected-utility theory. Tversky and Fox: the same subjects who shun unknown odds will wager readily on their own vague judgments about the outcome of a game or election in a domain where they feel knowledgeable, demonstrating the competence-driven reversal.
