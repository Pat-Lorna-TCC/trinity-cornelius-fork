# Risk-Pooling Requires Independence
**Source**: Peter L. Bernstein, *Against the Gods: The Remarkable Story of Risk* (Wiley, 1996) — Ch. 13 (Kenneth Arrow; Law of Large Numbers)
**Document Type**: Book
**Extracted By**: AI (document-insight-extractor, Book Mode)
**Extraction Date**: 2026-06-28
**Scope**: Books/against-the-gods-bernstein

## Core Insight
Insurance works only when the Law of Large Numbers can operate: the pooled risks must be numerous, **independent**, and rationally calculable. When those conditions hold, individual unpredictability averages out into near-certain aggregate cost. When they fail, the mechanism fails. Correlated or systemic risks (a market-wide crash, a war) and genuinely novel risks violate independence or calculability - and are therefore essentially uninsurable.

## Mechanism / Why It Matters
The principle draws the sharp line between **diversifiable / idiosyncratic** risk - the kind that can be pooled away because one policyholder's fire tells you nothing about the next - and **systemic / correlated** risk, which cannot be transferred to anyone, only borne. Pooling dissolves randomness only when the draws are independent; correlated draws all move together, so the pool offers no protection at the exact moment protection is needed. This ties directly back to Knight: the uninsurable residual *is* genuine uncertainty - the part no pooling can dissolve. The principle also surfaces **moral hazard**: removing all fear of loss breeds antisocial risk-taking. Deposit insurance that fully shields depositors can incubate reckless banks and taxpayer-funded blowups - the cure for risk manufacturing more of it.

## Evidence
Kenneth Arrow on the economics of insurance and the limits of risk-bearing markets; the Law of Large Numbers (Bernoulli, Poisson) as the mathematical engine of pooling. Canonical insurance economics - established. The independence requirement is a theorem-level condition, not an empirical tendency: the variance of an average falls only when the terms are uncorrelated.
