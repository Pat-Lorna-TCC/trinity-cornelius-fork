# The Port-Royal Rule - Weigh Probability and Consequence
**Source**: Peter L. Bernstein, *Against the Gods: The Remarkable Story of Risk* (Wiley, 1996) — Ch. 5 / Ch. 7 (Port-Royal *Logic*, 1662)
**Document Type**: Book
**Extracted By**: AI (document-insight-extractor, Book Mode)
**Extraction Date**: 2026-06-28
**Scope**: Books/against-the-gods-bernstein

## Core Insight
The Port-Royal *Logic* (1662) was the first text to state that a decision or a fear should be weighted by BOTH the magnitude of the consequence AND the probability of its occurrence - never by either alone. In its own words: "Fear of harm ought to be proportional not merely to the gravity of the harm, but also to the probability of the event." This is the two-axis primitive from which all expected-value and expected-utility reasoning descends.

## Mechanism / Why It Matters
Risk is irreducibly two-dimensional: **risk = probability x consequence**. Collapsing it to one axis produces predictable pathologies, and Bernstein names both failure modes:

- The **pathologically risk-averse** fixate on the gravity of a harm while ignoring how unlikely it is - they refuse to fly because a crash is catastrophic, never mind that it almost never happens.
- The **foolhardy** fixate on probability while ignoring magnitude - they take a near-certain small edge that carries a rare but ruinous downside.

Sound judgment lives in the product, not in either factor. This primitive is what expected value formalizes (sum of consequence weighted by probability) and what expected utility refines (replace raw consequence with its utility). It is the conceptual floor under the whole edifice.

## Evidence
The principle appears in *La Logique, ou l'art de penser* (the Port-Royal *Logic*, 1662), associated with Antoine Arnauld and Pierre Nicole - the first known formulation tying rational fear to the joint product of severity and likelihood, predating formal decision theory by centuries.
