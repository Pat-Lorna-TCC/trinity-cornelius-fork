# Cardinal Utility via Indifference Gambles
**Source**: Peter L. Bernstein, *Against the Gods: The Remarkable Story of Risk* (Wiley, 1996) — Ch. 15 (von Neumann & Morgenstern, *Theory of Games and Economic Behavior*, 1944)
**Document Type**: Book
**Extracted By**: AI (document-insight-extractor, Book Mode)
**Extraction Date**: 2026-06-28
**Scope**: Books/against-the-gods-bernstein

## Core Insight
Von Neumann and Morgenstern (1944) showed that utility - usually a vague, ordinal preference - can be pinned to a hard cardinal number. Find the probability at which a person is *indifferent* between a sure thing and a gamble over a better and a worse outcome, and you have measured the utility of the sure thing on a real scale. Diminishing marginal utility of money then falls out automatically: if someone is indifferent between $100 for sure and a 67%-chance-of-$200 gamble, the second $100 is revealed to be worth less to them than the first.

## Mechanism / Why It Matters
The construction turns subjective preference into a quantitative, internally consistent scale. The indifference point is a *measurement instrument* - it forces a fuzzy feeling to declare itself as a number, and the resulting utility function's curvature *is* the formal definition of risk aversion (concave = risk-averse, the gamble's expected dollars exceed the sure thing yet are valued equally). This is the rationality backbone of expected-utility decision theory: rank options by expected utility, not expected money. Crucially for the knowledge base, this is the *constructive method* itself - the "thesis." The KB holds many behavioral *attacks* on expected utility (framing, loss aversion, probability weighting) but lacks the positive object those critiques target. This note supplies it.

## Evidence
Von Neumann & Morgenstern, *Theory of Games and Economic Behavior* (1944): the expected-utility axioms (completeness, transitivity, continuity, independence) imply a cardinal utility function unique up to positive affine transformation. Canonical decision theory; normatively accepted, descriptively contested by behavioral economics since Kahneman & Tversky (1979).
