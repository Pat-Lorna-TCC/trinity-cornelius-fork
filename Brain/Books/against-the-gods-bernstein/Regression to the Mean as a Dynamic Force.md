# Regression to the Mean as a Dynamic Force
**Source**: Peter L. Bernstein, *Against the Gods: The Remarkable Story of Risk* (Wiley, 1996) - Ch. 9-10 (Francis Galton, sweet-pea experiment 1877, human stature 1885)
**Document Type**: Book
**Extracted By**: AI (document-insight-extractor, Book Mode)
**Extraction Date**: 2026-06-28
**Scope**: Books/against-the-gods-bernstein

## Core Insight
Galton did not merely observe that outliers tend toward the average - he discovered that offspring distributions are systematically *tighter* than parent distributions, making regression a continuous restoring force rather than a statistical snapshot. Tall parents have tall children who are nonetheless closer to the mean; short parents have taller children. Without this inward pull "the world would consist of nothing but midgets and giants."

## Mechanism / Why It Matters
The distinct contribution here is reframing regression from a static descriptive fact into a *dynamic process*. As Pearson put it, Galton "transformed probability from a static concept into a dynamic process in which the successors to the outliers are predestined to join the crowd at the center." Each extreme observation is systematically followed by a less extreme one, and the dispersion of the next generation shrinks toward the population mean. This restoring force is the engine underneath three reasoning patterns: contrarian investing (extremes revert), base-rate reasoning (the population mean predicts better than the recent outlier), and skepticism toward trend extrapolation (a streak is more likely to be pulled back than to continue).

## Evidence
Galton's sweet-pea seed experiment (1877) and his study of human stature (1885) both showed the same signature: extreme parents produce offspring shifted toward the mean, with each successive generation displaying narrower dispersion. The phenomenon is empirically confirmed and treated as established statistical fact.
