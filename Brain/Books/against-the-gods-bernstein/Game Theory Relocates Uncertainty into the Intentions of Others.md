# Game Theory Relocates Uncertainty into the Intentions of Others
**Source**: Peter L. Bernstein, *Against the Gods: The Remarkable Story of Risk* (Wiley, 1996) — Ch. 15 (von Neumann & Morgenstern, 1944)
**Document Type**: Book
**Extracted By**: AI (document-insight-extractor, Book Mode)
**Extraction Date**: 2026-06-28
**Scope**: Books/against-the-gods-bernstein

## Core Insight
Earlier theories of risk treated uncertainty as a brute fact of nature - dice, weather, mortality tables. Game theory's reframe (von Neumann & Morgenstern, 1944) is that "the true source of uncertainty lies in the intentions of others." Most real decisions are not gambles against an indifferent world but negotiations in which other people are reacting to you exactly as you are reacting to them.

## Mechanism / Why It Matters
This converts uncertainty from a one-player problem-against-nature into a multi-player **reflexive** problem with strategic interdependence and feedback. Nature does not adjust its odds when it sees your bet; a rival does. Each actor's forecast alters others' behavior, which in turn alters the forecast - a moving target that no static probability distribution can capture. The practical consequence: in any contested arena (markets, negotiation, war, competitive strategy) you cannot model the future as a fixed lottery; you must model the *response function* of intelligent adversaries who are modeling yours. Uncertainty is no longer "out there" - it is generated by the interaction itself.

## Evidence
Von Neumann & Morgenstern, *Theory of Games and Economic Behavior* (1944): recast economic decisions as games of strategy rather than games of chance, with payoffs determined jointly by all players' choices. Interpretation of the field's significance (Bernstein's framing) rather than a single proven theorem - hence emerging rather than canonical-established.
