# The Danger of Assuming Normality
**Source**: Peter L. Bernstein, *Against the Gods: The Remarkable Story of Risk* (Wiley, 1996) - Ch. 9 (Bernstein's S&P 500 equity-tail analysis, 1926-1995)
**Document Type**: Book
**Extracted By**: AI (document-insight-extractor, Book Mode)
**Extraction Date**: 2026-06-28
**Scope**: Books/against-the-gods-bernstein

## Core Insight
Real-world distributions that look normal through the body deviate badly at the extremes, where outliers ("superswings") appear far more often than a bell curve predicts and are often skewed to the downside. Normality is a convenient assumption that fails precisely where the consequential events live - this is a pre-Taleb empirical articulation of fat tails, grounded in equity data rather than theory.

## Mechanism / Why It Matters
The body of a distribution and its tails are governed by different regimes. Fitting a normal curve to the dense central mass produces a model that is comfortable, tractable, and dangerously wrong about the rare large moves that actually determine ruin or fortune. Because the deviation is downside-skewed, the error is not symmetric: the assumption understates exactly the losses that matter most. The transferable rule is to treat any "it's approximately normal" claim as a statement about the body only, and to model the tails separately and pessimistically.

## Evidence
Bernstein's S&P 500 monthly-return data (1926-1995): the returns resemble a normal curve but with "untidy bulges" at the edges. Of 840 months, 33 exceeded two standard deviations - materially more than a normal distribution predicts - and the excess was overrepresented on the downside. His conclusion: "At the extremes, the market is not a random walk; at the extremes, the market is more likely to destroy fortunes than to create them." This is interpretation (an empirical foreshadowing of fat-tail theory), hence emerging rather than canonical.
