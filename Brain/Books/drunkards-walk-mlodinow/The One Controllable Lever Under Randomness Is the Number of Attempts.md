# The One Controllable Lever Under Randomness Is the Number of Attempts
**Source**: Leonard Mlodinow, *The Drunkard's Walk: How Randomness Rules Our Lives* (Pantheon, 2008) - Ch. 10 + Ch. 8
**Document Type**: Book
**Extracted By**: AI (document-insight-extractor, Book Mode)
**Extraction Date**: 2026-06-28
**Scope**: Books/drunkards-walk-mlodinow

## Core Insight
When outcomes are driven by cumulative random shocks, any individual result is unpredictable - but the RATE of exposure to chance is controllable. Increasing the number of attempts and opportunities seized raises the probability of eventual success even when each single trial is weighted toward failure. Persistence is therefore the rational response to a random world: you cannot control which try wins, but you can control how many times you try.

## Mechanism / Why It Matters
If each attempt has a low but nonzero success probability and attempts are roughly independent, the probability of at least one success grows toward certainty as the number of attempts increases. The actionable corollary Mlodinow draws is blunt: to succeed, "double your failure rate" - because more failures means more attempts, and more attempts means more exposure to the chance of a hit. The lever is not the per-trial odds (often fixed and unfavorable) but the count of trials.

This reframes the role of effort and talent. Mlodinow notes that persistence is found to matter at least as much as talent - consistent with the ten-year rule of sustained practice underlying expert achievement - because sustained effort is precisely what multiplies the number of tries. In a world where outcomes are heavily randomized, the disciplined response is not to perfect a single shot but to keep generating shots, expanding the surface area over which luck can land.

## Evidence
- Structural: P(at least one success over n independent low-probability attempts) rises toward 1 as n grows.
- "Double your failure rate" - more attempts mechanically raise the chance of eventual success.
- Research finding: persistence matters at least as much as talent; the ten-year rule links sustained effort (many attempts) to expert-level success.
