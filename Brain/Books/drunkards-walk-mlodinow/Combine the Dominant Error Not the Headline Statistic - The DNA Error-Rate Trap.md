# Combine the Dominant Error Not the Headline Statistic - The DNA Error-Rate Trap
**Source**: Leonard Mlodinow, *The Drunkard's Walk: How Randomness Rules Our Lives* (Pantheon, 2008) - Ch. 2
**Document Type**: Book
**Extracted By**: AI (document-insight-extractor, Book Mode)
**Extraction Date**: 2026-06-28
**Scope**: Books/drunkards-walk-mlodinow

## Core Insight
When two error sources of very different magnitude exist, the overall error is governed by the LARGER one (the OR / sum rule), not by an intuitive average and not by the smaller, more impressive figure. The dominant error swamps the negligible one, so reporting only the small statistic radically understates the true chance of being wrong.

## Mechanism / Why It Matters
For two ways an outcome can go wrong, the probability that at least one occurs is approximately the sum of the two probabilities (the OR-rule, when both are small). If one error is 1-in-1-billion and the other is 1-in-100, the sum is dominated entirely by the larger term - the total error is about 1-in-100, essentially unaffected by the tiny number. Averaging the two, or quoting only the small one, produces a wildly over-confident result.

This is the complement to the AND-rule case where independent partial proofs multiply. Here the question is "what is the chance of ANY error," so the errors add and the biggest one rules. The cognitive trap is selective attention: the precise, scientific-sounding rarity figure captures attention while the mundane, larger human-process error is suppressed.

Why it matters: in adversarial settings this asymmetry is exploited. Courts and juries fixate on an impressive but near-irrelevant statistic and ignore the dominant, decision-determining one - structurally similar to the prosecutor's fallacy, where a rare-match number is presented out of its proper probabilistic context.

## Evidence
DNA testimony commonly cites an accidental-match probability near 1-in-1-billion while ignoring the laboratory human-error rate, estimated near 1-in-100. Because the lab-error rate is roughly 10 million times larger than the accidental-match rate, it dominates the sum: the true probability of an erroneous DNA match is about 1-in-100, not 1-in-1-billion. The impressive headline figure is essentially irrelevant once the dominant error is included.
