# Confirmation Bias - Falsification Finds Rules That Verification Cannot
**Source**: Leonard Mlodinow, *The Drunkard's Walk: How Randomness Rules Our Lives* (Pantheon, 2008) - Ch. 9
**Document Type**: Book
**Extracted By**: AI (document-insight-extractor, Book Mode)
**Extraction Date**: 2026-06-28
**Scope**: Books/drunkards-walk-mlodinow

## Core Insight
Once a hypothesis is held, people seek confirming instances and read ambiguous evidence in its favor, which is why they fail to discover rules that a single disconfirming test would expose. Verification - piling up cases that fit - cannot distinguish a narrow guess from the true broad rule; only falsification can. The faster path to truth is to spend as much effort trying to disprove a belief as to confirm it.

## Mechanism / Why It Matters
A confirming instance is consistent with many different hypotheses at once, so it rarely narrows the field. A disconfirming instance, by contrast, eliminates a hypothesis outright and is therefore far more informative. Because people instinctively generate tests they expect to PASS, they gather confirmations that feel like progress while leaving the actual rule untested - and frequently wrong.

This asymmetry is why belief polarizes rather than converges when people share the same evidence: each side counts the agreeable parts and discounts the rest, so identical data pushes opposed camps further apart. It is also why good scientific and forecasting practice deliberately courts disconfirmation - designing the experiment most likely to break the hypothesis, not the one most likely to flatter it.

The practical lever is a habit reversal: before accepting a pattern, ask "what test, if it came out a certain way, would prove me wrong?" and run it. A single well-chosen falsifying probe outperforms any number of confirmations.

## Evidence
- Wason's 2-4-6 task: subjects told a triple fits a hidden rule almost universally test only ascending-even triples (consistent with their guessed rule "even numbers increasing by two") and so never discover the actual, broader rule "any increasing sequence" - which one odd-numbered test would have revealed.
- A death-penalty belief study: partisans on both sides, shown the same pair of studies, rated whichever study used the methodology that supported THEIR position as higher quality - so shared evidence increased polarization instead of convergence.
