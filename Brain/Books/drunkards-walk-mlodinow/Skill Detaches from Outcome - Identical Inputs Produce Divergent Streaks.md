# Skill Detaches from Outcome - Identical Inputs Produce Divergent Streaks
**Source**: Leonard Mlodinow, *The Drunkard's Walk: How Randomness Rules Our Lives* (Pantheon, 2008) - Ch. 1 + Prologue
**Document Type**: Book
**Extracted By**: AI (document-insight-extractor, Book Mode)
**Extraction Date**: 2026-06-28
**Scope**: Books/drunkards-walk-mlodinow

## Core Insight
Outcomes are a skill signal blurred by random forces, so identical inputs can produce wildly divergent results - and that randomness clusters into streaks rather than spreading evenly. Because we observe only the realized streak, we read it as a trend caused by changing ability, when it is often just noise clumping. Managers then reward and punish people for outcomes their decisions did not control.

## Mechanism / Why It Matters
Mlodinow's identical-film thought experiment isolates the effect. Imagine two indistinguishable movies released to a stream of viewers who each prefer whichever has more prior "votes." With genuinely identical quality, the most probable number of lead changes across 20,000 viewers is 0 - and it is roughly 88 times more likely that one film "wins" throughout than that the lead seesaws back and forth. Equal inputs reliably produce unequal, streaky outcomes.

The consequence for evaluation is severe. Random processes do not distribute results smoothly; they clump into runs that look exactly like momentum or decline. Decision-makers interpret these runs as trends and act - firing or anointing coaches and CEOs - even though, on average, such firings produce no improvement in performance. The streak is in the data, not in the person. Separating the controllable skill signal from the streaky random blur is the core analytical task, and our instinct to narrate the streak is precisely what fails.

## Evidence
- Identical-film model: most probable number of lead changes among 20,000 viewers is 0; one film leading throughout is ~88x more likely than a seesawing lead.
- Studies of executive/coach firings find no average improvement in subsequent performance attributable to the change.
