# Perceived Quality Is Constructed From Expectation Not Just Stimulus
**Source**: Leonard Mlodinow, *The Drunkard's Walk: How Randomness Rules Our Lives* (Pantheon, 2008) - Ch. 7 + Ch. 10
**Document Type**: Book
**Extracted By**: AI (document-insight-extractor, Book Mode)
**Extraction Date**: 2026-06-28
**Scope**: Books/drunkards-walk-mlodinow

## Core Insight
The brain forms an assessment by integrating prior expectation with raw sensory input, so labels, prices, and context alter the experience itself - not merely the report of it. Expectation is a load-bearing INPUT to perception, which is why blind tests collapse brand premiums that seem real when the label is visible.

## Mechanism / Why It Matters
We treat perceived quality as if it were a clean readout of the stimulus. It is not. Perception is constructive: the brain combines what arrives at the senses with what it expects to arrive, and the blend is what reaches consciousness. A prior - a price tag, a color, a brand - is not a bias layered on top of an honest experience; it changes the experience at the neural level.

The strongest evidence is neuroimaging. When subjects believed a wine was expensive, the brain region that encodes experienced pleasure was genuinely more active - the enjoyment was real, just caused by the label rather than the liquid. This makes "objective" quality ratings deeply unstable, because the rater's experience is contaminated by everything they know about the object before tasting it.

The practical implication is that blind testing is the only way to isolate the stimulus from expectation, and brand premiums frequently evaporate under it. Conversely, much of what we pay for in premium goods is the expectation effect - which is a genuine, felt benefit, just not one located in the product.

## Evidence
- Tasters rated identical white wine dyed red as "sweeter," with red-wine descriptors.
- Subjects rated identical wine higher when told it cost $90 versus $10; fMRI showed the pleasure-encoding region was genuinely more active for the "expensive" wine.
- In a cola test, 21 of 30 drinkers "confirmed" their stated brand preference even after the bottles were secretly swapped.
- A blind vodka tasting put cheap Smirnoff on top, collapsing the premium brands' edge once the label was hidden.
