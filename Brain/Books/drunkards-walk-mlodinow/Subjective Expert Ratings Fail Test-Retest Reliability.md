# Subjective Expert Ratings Fail Test-Retest Reliability
**Source**: Leonard Mlodinow, *The Drunkard's Walk: How Randomness Rules Our Lives* (Pantheon, 2008) - Ch. 7
**Document Type**: Book
**Extracted By**: AI (document-insight-extractor, Book Mode)
**Extraction Date**: 2026-06-28
**Scope**: Books/drunkards-walk-mlodinow

## Core Insight
When the same subjective object is scored independently by trained experts, their agreement is far lower than the numerical authority of the scores implies. Grades, essay assessments, and wine ratings carry decimal precision the underlying judgment cannot support. The numerical packaging manufactures false confidence.

## Mechanism / Why It Matters
A score of "92" looks like a measurement of a stable property. But if a second equally-qualified expert, scoring the same object blind, would have said "84," then the "92" is one draw from a wide distribution of expert judgments - not a reading of the thing itself. Test-retest reliability (or inter-rater agreement) measures how reproducible the judgment is, and for subjective ratings it is routinely poor.

This matters because we make consequential decisions - admissions, prizes, purchases, rankings - on the basis of such numbers, treating their precision as informative. The decimal points are theater. The honest representation of an expert rating is the rating plus the spread across experts, and that spread is often large enough to swallow the distinctions we act on.

The wine case is the cleanest demonstration: even with a controlled "triangle" design that removes ambiguity, expert discrimination is barely above what marginal chance plus a little real skill would produce. The lesson generalizes to any field where credentialed humans assign numbers to subjective quality.

## Evidence
- Eight faculty members grading 120 term papers differed, on average, by nearly a full letter grade.
- Trained essay assessors agreed with each other only about half the time.
- Wine experts given a "triangle" test (two identical wines plus one different) misidentified the odd wine roughly 1 time in 3.
- The same experts disagreed on 9 of 12 sensory components being scored.
