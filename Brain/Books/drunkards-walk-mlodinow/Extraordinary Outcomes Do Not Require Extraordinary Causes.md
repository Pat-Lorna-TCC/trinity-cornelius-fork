# Extraordinary Outcomes Do Not Require Extraordinary Causes
**Source**: Leonard Mlodinow, *The Drunkard's Walk: How Randomness Rules Our Lives* (Pantheon, 2008) - Ch. 1
**Document Type**: Book
**Extracted By**: AI (document-insight-extractor, Book Mode)
**Extraction Date**: 2026-06-28
**Scope**: Books/drunkards-walk-mlodinow

## Core Insight
Across a large number of independent trials, rare aberrant outcomes are statistically expected even when no special cause is present. An exceptional achievement is therefore often a lucky spike from an ordinary producer rather than evidence of extraordinary ability. The load-bearing idea is the base rate of aberration over many trials: with enough attempts somewhere in the pool, an extreme result is not just possible but probable.

## Mechanism / Why It Matters
The intuition that says "that result was too good to be chance" fails to account for the size of the pool generating results. If thousands of normal-talent performers each take many shots over many years, the probability that NONE of them ever produces an extreme spike falls toward zero - so the appearance of a record-breaker is close to guaranteed, with no exceptional cause required.

Mlodinow models Roger Maris's 1961 home-run record as a coin-toss process: over a talent pool spanning roughly seventy years, the probability that SOME normal-talent player breaks Babe Ruth's record by chance alone exceeds 50%. The record-holder is then deified for what is, mechanistically, a random crackle in the static. This is distinct from regression to the mean (which describes the expected pull-back of an extreme observation toward average): here the point is the prior expectation that aberrations WILL appear given enough trials. Misattributing an outlier to special skill - rather than to the arithmetic of many tries - is one of the most common errors in evaluating success.

## Evidence
- Coin-toss model of Maris's 1961 record: over a ~70-year talent pool, P(some normal-talent player breaks Ruth's record by chance) > 50%.
- General structure: as the number of independent trials grows, P(at least one extreme outcome) approaches certainty even with no above-average input.
