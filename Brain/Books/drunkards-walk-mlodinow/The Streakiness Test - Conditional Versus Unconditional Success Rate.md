# The Streakiness Test - Conditional Versus Unconditional Success Rate
**Source**: Leonard Mlodinow, *The Drunkard's Walk: How Randomness Rules Our Lives* (Pantheon, 2008) - Ch. 9
**Document Type**: Book
**Extracted By**: AI (document-insight-extractor, Book Mode)
**Extraction Date**: 2026-06-28
**Scope**: Books/drunkards-walk-mlodinow

## Core Insight
A genuine "hot hand" or momentum effect requires that the probability of success conditional on a prior success EXCEED the performer's baseline (unconditional) success rate. When this comparison is measured directly, the conditional rate matches the unconditional rate - making a make after a make no more likely than a make after a miss. This conditional-versus-unconditional comparison is the operational test for any momentum claim.

## Mechanism / Why It Matters
The intuition of streakiness confuses two things: longer runs and a raised conditional probability. A higher base-rate performer mechanically produces longer streaks under pure chance, exactly as a weighted coin produces longer runs of heads than a fair one - without any of the tosses influencing the next. Longer streaks among better players are therefore expected even if every attempt is independent. They are evidence of a higher base rate, not of momentum.

The discriminating test is to compute P(success | prior success) and compare it to the unconditional P(success). If they are equal, the streak is consistent with independence and the "hot hand" is illusory. Controlled measurements of basketball shooting - 76ers field goals, Celtics free throws, and a Cornell experiment with controlled shot conditions - all show the conditional rate failing to beat the unconditional rate. The lesson generalizes: before crediting any run to momentum, ask whether the conditional rate actually exceeds the baseline. Usually it does not.

## Evidence
- Philadelphia 76ers field goals: P(make | previous make) does not exceed P(make).
- Boston Celtics free throws: no elevation of success rate following a success.
- Cornell controlled-shot experiment: hits after hits are no more likely than hits after misses.
