# Optimistic Bias Is the Engine of Capitalism and a Mispricing of Risk

**Source**: Daniel Kahneman, *Thinking, Fast and Slow* (Farrar, Straus and Giroux, 2011) - Part III, "The Engine of Capitalism"
**Document Type**: Book
**Extracted By**: AI (document-insight-extractor, Book Mode)
**Extraction Date**: 2026-06-28
**Scope**: Books/thinking-fast-and-slow-kahneman

## Core Insight
Optimistic overconfidence drives the entrepreneurs, inventors, and leaders who disproportionately shape outcomes. Risk-takers mostly UNDERESTIMATE the odds rather than truly loving risk ("bold forecasts and timid decisions"). The same bias is simultaneously the source of economic dynamism and of predictable individual loss - an ensemble-versus-individual split. Competition neglect compounds it. The premortem is a partial remedy.

## Mechanism / Why It Matters
At the population level, a steady supply of overconfident risk-takers seeds the rare successes that drive growth, even though most individuals lose. The system harvests value from the failures of "optimistic martyrs," so what is irrational for the individual is productive for the ensemble. Competition neglect - planning as if rivals do not exist - amplifies the error precisely where many optimists converge on the same opportunity.

## Evidence
US 5-year small-business survival is about 35%; entrepreneurs estimated 60% for "any business like yours" but 81% put their OWN odds at 7/10 or higher and 33% said zero chance of failure. Astebro: of inventions graded D/E (predicting failure for over 70%), 47% persisted after being told it was hopeless and on average doubled their losses. Malmendier-Tate: optimistic CEOs overpay and make value-destroying mergers, and award-winning CEOs subsequently underperform. Camerer-Lovallo "competition neglect" (the Disney chairman on same-weekend blockbusters).
