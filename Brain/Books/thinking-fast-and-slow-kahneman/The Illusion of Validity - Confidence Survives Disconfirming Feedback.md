# The Illusion of Validity - Confidence Survives Disconfirming Feedback

**Source**: Daniel Kahneman, *Thinking, Fast and Slow* (Farrar, Straus and Giroux, 2011) - Part III, "The Illusion of Validity"
**Document Type**: Book
**Extracted By**: AI (document-insight-extractor, Book Mode)
**Extraction Date**: 2026-06-28
**Scope**: Books/thinking-fast-and-slow-kahneman

## Core Insight
Subjective confidence is a feeling of internal coherence, not a signal of accuracy. Cognitive illusions of skill are MORE stubborn than visual ones: the feeling and the action persist even after the disconfirming fact is intellectually accepted. The mind does not digest threatening facts. The only valid test of skill is persistence of performance across time.

## Mechanism / Why It Matters
Confidence is manufactured by the ease and coherence of the story System 1 constructs, so it is decoupled from the world it claims to describe. Knowing the statistics that destroy your credential does not dissolve the feeling of skill, because the illusion is generated by a faster process than the correction. Institutions therefore keep paying for skill that does not exist.

## Evidence
Army leaderless-group test: assessors felt total confidence in their officer predictions even after repeated feedback showed the predictions were "little better than random guesses"; confidence returned in full the next day. Wealth-advisory firm: 25 advisers over 8 years yielded 28 cross-year skill correlations averaging roughly 0.01 (zero); shown this, executives "swept it under the rug." Mutual funds: about 2 of 3 underperform yearly, cross-year correlation near zero.
