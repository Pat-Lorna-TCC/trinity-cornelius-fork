# Mental Effort Has a Measurable Signature and Obeys the Law of Least Effort
**Source**: Daniel Kahneman, *Thinking, Fast and Slow* (Farrar, Straus and Giroux, 2011) - Part 1, Effort
**Document Type**: Book
**Extracted By**: AI (document-insight-extractor, Book Mode)
**Extraction Date**: 2026-06-28
**Scope**: Books/thinking-fast-and-slow-kahneman

## Core Insight
Cognitive effort is a real, metered, costly resource, not a metaphor. Pupil diameter tracks it second-by-second, rising and falling with task demand. Because effort is a genuine cost, people gravitate to the least-demanding path - "laziness is built deep into our nature" - which is why the lazy System 2 so often ratifies System 1 rather than checking it.

## Mechanism / Why It Matters
Effort behaves like a budgeted physiological cost, so the mind economizes on it by default. This is the deep reason biases persist: correcting System 1 requires spending a resource the system is built to conserve. The transferable error this guards against is assuming that more careful thinking is freely available on demand - it is rationed, and the ration favors the cheap, automatic answer. The metered-cost framing also maps cleanly onto compute budgets in machine reasoning.

## Evidence
Beatty-Kahneman pupillometry: the Add-3 task dilates the pupil by roughly 50% and raises heart rate about 7 beats per minute. Dilation follows an inverted-V, peaking at maximum load and collapsing the instant the subject solves the problem or gives up. When demand exceeds capacity, the pupils STOP dilating rather than expanding further. Both skill and intelligence REDUCE the effort signature for the same task - experts spend less to do the same work.
