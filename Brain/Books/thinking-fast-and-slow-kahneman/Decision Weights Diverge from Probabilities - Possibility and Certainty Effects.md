# Decision Weights Diverge from Probabilities - Possibility and Certainty Effects

**Source**: Daniel Kahneman, *Thinking, Fast and Slow* (Farrar, Straus and Giroux, 2011) - Part IV, "The Fourfold Pattern" / "Rare Events"
**Document Type**: Book
**Extracted By**: AI (document-insight-extractor, Book Mode)
**Extraction Date**: 2026-06-28
**Scope**: Books/thinking-fast-and-slow-kahneman

## Core Insight
People do not weight outcomes by their probabilities. They OVERWEIGHT the move away from impossibility (possibility effect) and the move toward certainty (certainty effect), while compressing the middle range. This is prospect theory's SECOND engine, largely independent of the value function.

## Mechanism / Why It Matters
Decision weights are psychological responses to probability, not probabilities themselves. There is a further description/experience gap: rare events are overweighted when they are DESCRIBED but underweighted when learned from repeated EXPERIENCE, because most people simply never sample the rare event. This explains both lottery appeal and systemic blindness to tail risk.

## Evidence
Measured decision weights: a 2% chance carries weight ~8.1 (overweighted roughly fourfold); moving from 100% to 98% certainty costs about 13% of subjective value. The Allais paradox: leading economists - Samuelson, Arrow, Friedman, Savage - reversed preference between a 61-vs-63% choice and a 98-vs-100% choice, the certainty effect violating expected-utility axioms. Experienced-vs-described: no banker in 2007 had lived through a devastating crisis, so the rare event was effectively ignored.
