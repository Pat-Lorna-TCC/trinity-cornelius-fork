# The Fourfold Pattern of Risk Attitudes

**Source**: Daniel Kahneman, *Thinking, Fast and Slow* (Farrar, Straus and Giroux, 2011) - Part IV, "The Fourfold Pattern"
**Document Type**: Book
**Extracted By**: AI (document-insight-extractor, Book Mode)
**Extraction Date**: 2026-06-28
**Scope**: Books/thinking-fast-and-slow-kahneman

## Core Insight
The interaction of the value function and decision weights produces four predictable risk attitudes: risk-AVERSE for high-probability gains and low-probability losses; risk-SEEKING for low-probability gains (lotteries) and high-probability losses (desperate gambles). This is why the same person buys both lottery tickets and insurance.

## Mechanism / Why It Matters
Each cell is the joint output of two forces - the curvature of the value function (concave for gains, convex for losses) and overweighting at the probability extremes. Mapping a real decision to its cell predicts whether a person will lock in, gamble, overpay, or escalate. The high-probability-loss cell (top right) is the dangerous one: it drives people to fight past certain defeat.

## Evidence
The 2x2: high-prob gain -> lock in a sure thing or accept a settlement; low-prob gain -> buy lottery tickets; low-prob loss -> overpay for insurance; high-prob loss -> fight a losing case and throw good money after bad. Guthrie's litigation data: strong-case plaintiffs (risk-averse cell) settle for less than statistical expected value, while frivolous-claim plaintiffs (the lottery cell) extract more than justified. The top-right cell is where losing armies fight past certain defeat.
