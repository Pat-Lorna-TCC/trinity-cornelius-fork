# Regression to the Mean Has an Explanation but No Cause
**Source**: Daniel Kahneman, *Thinking, Fast and Slow* (Farrar, Straus and Giroux, 2011) - Part II, "Regression to the Mean"
**Document Type**: Book
**Extracted By**: AI (document-insight-extractor, Book Mode)
**Extraction Date**: 2026-06-28
**Scope**: Books/thinking-fast-and-slow-kahneman

## Core Insight
Whenever a correlation is less than perfect, extreme scores are followed by more average ones for PURELY statistical reasons - luck does not repeat. Regression has an explanation but no cause. Because the mind demands causation, it manufactures spurious causal stories, and this systematically INVERTS the feedback we receive about reward versus punishment.

## Mechanism / Why It Matters
This DEEPENS the static definition of regression by adding three things: the no-cause-but-an-explanation asymmetry, the feedback-inversion it produces, and the control-group rationale that follows. Any performance = skill + luck; the lucky component cannot persist, so extremes regress regardless of intervention. The mind then misattributes the regression to whatever it did in between.

## Evidence
- Israeli Air Force instructors concluded punishment works and praise backfires: they praised after unusually good (lucky) flights that then regressed down, and screamed after unusually bad ones that regressed up.
- A coin-toss-at-target demo reproduced "improve after bad / deteriorate after good" with ZERO feedback.
- Galton (1886) and the best statisticians of the era needed years to grasp it.
- Depressed children "improve" on any treatment by regression alone - which is WHY control and placebo groups exist.
- General implication: "we are statistically rewarded for being nasty and punished for being nice."
