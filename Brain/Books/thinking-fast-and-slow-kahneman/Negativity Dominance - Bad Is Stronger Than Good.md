# Negativity Dominance - Bad Is Stronger Than Good

**Source**: Daniel Kahneman, *Thinking, Fast and Slow* (Farrar, Straus and Giroux, 2011) - Part IV, "Prospect Theory" / "The Endowment Effect"
**Document Type**: Book
**Extracted By**: AI (document-insight-extractor, Book Mode)
**Extraction Date**: 2026-06-28
**Scope**: Books/thinking-fast-and-slow-kahneman

## Core Insight
Loss aversion is one instance of a broad biological asymmetry: negative information, events, and emotions are processed faster, weighted heavier, and resist disconfirmation more than positive ones. Because a goal functions as a reference point, falling short looms larger than exceeding it.

## Mechanism / Why It Matters
The asymmetry is wired deep enough to be pre-attentive, which means it shapes judgment before System 2 can intervene. Treating loss aversion as a special case of a general negativity dominance explains why it shows up everywhere from threat perception to relationships to performance against targets - the reference point recasts any shortfall as a loss.

## Evidence
The amygdala responds to masked threatening faces shown under 2/100 of a second, below awareness. Bad words (war, crime) capture attention faster than good words. Rozin's demonstration: a cockroach touching a bowl of cherries ruins them, but a cherry does nothing for a bowl of roaches. Gottman found stable marriages need roughly a 5:1 ratio of good to bad interactions. Goals as reference: Pope and Schweitzer analyzed over 2.5 million golf putts - players sink par putts (avoiding a bogey, framed as a loss) about 3.6% more often than birdie putts (a foregone gain).
