# Representativeness Substitutes Similarity for Probability

**Source**: Daniel Kahneman, *Thinking, Fast and Slow* (Farrar, Straus and Giroux, 2011) - Part II, "Representativeness"
**Document Type**: Book
**Extracted By**: AI (document-insight-extractor, Book Mode)
**Extraction Date**: 2026-06-28
**Scope**: Books/thinking-fast-and-slow-kahneman

## Core Insight
Asked for a probability, the mind silently answers an easier question - how similar is this case to a prototype or stereotype. This single substitution commits TWO sins at once: it neglects base rates, and it treats worthless evidence as if it were diagnostic (WYSIATI). The fix is also twofold: anchor on a plausible base rate, then interrogate the diagnosticity of your evidence.

## Mechanism / Why It Matters
This note's contribution is representativeness AS the substitution mechanism - not base-rate neglect in general, which existing core notes already cover. The second, often-overlooked sin is the evidence-quality failure: people use a description even after being told it is uninformative. Because the failure is usually laziness rather than ignorance, forcing System 2 to engage restores some statistical sensitivity.

## Evidence
- Tom W: 114 psychology grad students who KNEW the base rates and were told the description came from tests "of uncertain validity" still ranked graduate fields purely by stereotype-similarity, predicting rare computer science over the populous humanities.
- Even statistician Robyn Dawes, who knew better, guessed "computer scientist."
- "Frowning" (an induced state of System-2 vigilance) restored some base-rate sensitivity - proving the failure is often laziness, not missing knowledge.
