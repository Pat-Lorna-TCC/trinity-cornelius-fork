# The Peak-End Rule and Duration Neglect
**Source**: Daniel Kahneman, *Thinking, Fast and Slow* (Farrar, Straus and Giroux, 2011) - Part V
**Document Type**: Book
**Extracted By**: AI (document-insight-extractor, Book Mode)
**Extraction Date**: 2026-06-28
**Scope**: Books/thinking-fast-and-slow-kahneman

## Core Insight
The remembering self scores an episode by AVERAGING its most extreme moment (peak) and its final moment (end), while assigning essentially ZERO weight to how long the episode lasted. This is duration neglect. The deep cause: System 1 represents a set of moments by a prototype or average, not by a sum - the very same averaging-not-summing operation that drives the Linda conjunction fallacy.

## Mechanism / Why It Matters
Because a memory is built from a prototype rather than an integral, total experienced quantity drops out. This opens a practical fork. To reduce REMEMBERED pain, lower the peak and taper the ending. To reduce pain ACTUALLY suffered, finish as fast as possible. The two goals can recommend opposite actions - the operational signature of the two-selves conflict.

## Evidence
Colonoscopy study (Redelmeier-Kahneman, N=154, pain rated every 60s): retrospective "total pain" tracked the average of PEAK and END, not the duration-weighted area under the curve. Patient A (8 min, ended at 7/10) recalled it as WORSE than Patient B (24 min, far more total pain, but ended at 1/10). Rats also show duration neglect - only intensity registers.
