# The Law of Small Numbers - Small Samples Manufacture Extreme Results

**Source**: Daniel Kahneman, *Thinking, Fast and Slow* (Farrar, Straus and Giroux, 2011) - Part II, "The Law of Small Numbers"
**Document Type**: Book
**Extracted By**: AI (document-insight-extractor, Book Mode)
**Extraction Date**: 2026-06-28
**Scope**: Books/thinking-fast-and-slow-kahneman

## Core Insight
Extreme outcomes - both the highest and the lowest - are a mathematical artifact of small sample size, not the fingerprint of any cause. Yet the mind reflexively attaches a causal story to them: it sees randomness as regularity and clustering, and trusts tiny samples far more than the arithmetic warrants.

## Mechanism / Why It Matters
Small samples vary more, so they over-produce extreme averages at both ends. The same cause cannot produce opposite effects, so when the best AND the worst share a feature, that feature is a sampling artifact, not an explanation. We over-detect causes in noise because false-positive causal vigilance was evolutionarily cheap - mistaking a rustle for a predator costs less than the reverse.

## Evidence
- US kidney-cancer study across 3,141 counties: lowest AND highest incidence counties are both disproportionately small rural counties - pure variance, not lifestyle.
- Urn math: samples of 4 yield homogeneous (all-red/all-white) outcomes ~8x more often than samples of 7 (12.5% vs 1.56%).
- Gates Foundation spent ~$1.7B favoring small schools because small schools were over-represented among the BEST - but they are equally over-represented among the WORST.
- Feller's WWII London-bombing gaps looked deliberate but were statistically typical of a random (Poisson) process.
- The basketball "hot hand" passed all randomness tests (Gilovich-Vallone-Tversky). Caveat: Miller-Sanjurjo (2018) show a small real effect survives a selection-bias correction, so the "pure illusion" verdict is now contested.
