# The Availability Heuristic - Ease of Retrieval Can Override Count

**Source**: Daniel Kahneman, *Thinking, Fast and Slow* (Farrar, Straus and Giroux, 2011) - Part II, "The Science of Availability"
**Document Type**: Book
**Extracted By**: AI (document-insight-extractor, Book Mode)
**Extraction Date**: 2026-06-28
**Scope**: Books/thinking-fast-and-slow-kahneman

## Core Insight
People judge frequency by how fluently instances come to mind rather than by counting them. The sharper finding, beyond "unusual cases feel more frequent": the experienced EASE of retrieval can override the NUMBER of instances retrieved - the feeling of fluency, not the tally, drives the judgment.

## Mechanism / Why It Matters
Availability is itself an instance of attribute substitution: the hard target ("how frequent / how true of me?") is answered with the easy felt-quality "how smoothly did examples come?" Crucially, the cue is metacognitive, so it can be unmasked. When the difficulty of retrieval is given a spurious external cause, the brain stops reading fluency as evidence - revealing the heuristic as misattributed "unexplained unavailability."

## Evidence
- Schwarz et al.: people asked to list 12 instances of their own assertiveness rated themselves LESS assertive than those who listed only 6 - the last few items were hard to produce, and that difficulty was read as "I'm not very assertive." Fluency beat count.
- The effect VANISHES when the difficulty is attributed to an external cause (background music said to impair retrieval), so the brain no longer treats the strain as self-relevant evidence.
