# The Conjunction Fallacy - A Coherent Story Beats Logical Probability
**Source**: Daniel Kahneman, *Thinking, Fast and Slow* (Farrar, Straus and Giroux, 2011) - Part II, "Linda: Less is More"
**Document Type**: Book
**Extracted By**: AI (document-insight-extractor, Book Mode)
**Extraction Date**: 2026-06-28
**Scope**: Books/thinking-fast-and-slow-kahneman

## Core Insight
People judge a more detailed, more plausible scenario as MORE probable than the single broader event that strictly contains it. This violates the logical rule that adding a conjunct can only lower probability. System 1 substitutes coherence and plausibility for probability - and it AVERAGES across details rather than summing them. Hence "less is more": adding a vivid condition raises felt likelihood while lowering actual likelihood.

## Mechanism / Why It Matters
A representative, story-like description fires System 1's coherence detector. Because the judgment runs on average plausibility rather than set logic, a richer story scores higher than its own superset. For forecasters this is lethal: every added detail makes a scenario feel more credible while making it strictly less probable.

## Evidence
- Linda: 85-89% of undergraduates - and 85% of Stanford decision-science doctoral students trained in probability - rated "bank teller AND active feminist" as more probable than "bank teller."
- Hsee dinnerware: in single evaluation a 24-piece intact set was priced higher ($33) than a 40-piece set containing it plus 7 extra intact pieces ($23), because broken pieces dragged down the AVERAGE.
- A detailed "California earthquake causes a flood" scenario was rated more probable than the broader "a flood somewhere in North America."
- Frequency framing ("how many of 100?") cut errors from 65% to 25%.
