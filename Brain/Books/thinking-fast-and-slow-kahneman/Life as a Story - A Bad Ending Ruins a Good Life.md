# Life as a Story - A Bad Ending Ruins a Good Life
**Source**: Daniel Kahneman, *Thinking, Fast and Slow* (Farrar, Straus and Giroux, 2011) - Part V
**Document Type**: Book
**Extracted By**: AI (document-insight-extractor, Book Mode)
**Extraction Date**: 2026-06-28
**Scope**: Books/thinking-fast-and-slow-kahneman

## Core Insight
Duration neglect and the peak-end rule scale up from minutes to entire lifetimes. The remembering self represents a whole life as a prototypical slice plus its ending - so adding pleasant-but-lesser years can REDUCE the judged quality of an otherwise-excellent life. This is a less-is-more effect over a lifespan: we care about the narrative ARC of a life independent of the total happiness actually experienced within it.

## Mechanism / Why It Matters
Because the remembering self averages rather than sums, more good years do not reliably make a better-judged life, and weaker good years can dilute the prototype and drag the verdict down. A life is scored like a story, with a privileged ending - which means our intuitions about a "good life" can diverge sharply from any accounting of experienced welfare.

## Evidence
Diener "Jen" studies. Between-subjects: doubling the duration of Jen's happy life (30 vs 60 years) had NO effect on judged total happiness - duration neglect over a lifetime. Adding 5 "slightly happy" years onto a very happy life caused a SUBSTANTIAL DROP in judged total happiness. Within-subjects, the intuition that the lesser extra years made the whole life worse was, Kahneman reports, "overwhelming."
