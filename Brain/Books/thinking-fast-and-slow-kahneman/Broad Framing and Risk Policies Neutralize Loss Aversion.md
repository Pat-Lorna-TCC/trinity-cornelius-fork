# Broad Framing and Risk Policies Neutralize Loss Aversion

**Source**: Daniel Kahneman, *Thinking, Fast and Slow* (Farrar, Straus and Giroux, 2011) - Part IV, "Risk Policies"
**Document Type**: Book
**Extracted By**: AI (document-insight-extractor, Book Mode)
**Extraction Date**: 2026-06-28
**Scope**: Books/thinking-fast-and-slow-kahneman

## Core Insight
Loss aversion is destructive only under NARROW framing. Bundling many small, independent, favorable gambles into one broad frame - a "risk policy" - shrinks the probability of a net loss and neutralizes loss aversion's tax. The practical rule: adopt risk policies, think like a trader, and check outcomes less often.

## Mechanism / Why It Matters
Narrow framing evaluates each bet in isolation, so each one triggers loss aversion separately. Aggregating independent favorable bets lets the law of large numbers do its work: the ensemble has a tiny chance of net loss even when any single bet feels frightening. Less-frequent monitoring removes the emotional triggering that narrow framing supplies.

## Evidence
Decisions i/ii: 73% chose the dominated combination under narrow framing, while only 3% chose the dominant one - the broad frame makes dominance obvious. Samuelson's friend rejects one 50-50 lose-$100/win-$200 bet yet wants 100 of them (EV +$5,000, only about 1/2,300 chance of any loss). Quarterly rather than daily portfolio checking improves both emotion and returns. Three caveats: gambles must be independent, the loss must not threaten total wealth, and the rule does not apply to genuine long shots.
