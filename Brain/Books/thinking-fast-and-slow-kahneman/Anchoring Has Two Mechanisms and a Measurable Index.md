# Anchoring Has Two Mechanisms and a Measurable Index

**Source**: Daniel Kahneman, *Thinking, Fast and Slow* (Farrar, Straus and Giroux, 2011) - Part II, "Anchors"
**Document Type**: Book
**Extracted By**: AI (document-insight-extractor, Book Mode)
**Extraction Date**: 2026-06-28
**Scope**: Books/thinking-fast-and-slow-kahneman

## Core Insight
Anchoring is not one effect but two distinct processes converging on the same outcome: a deliberate System-2 adjustment that stops too soon at the near edge of the plausible range, and an automatic System-1 priming in which associative memory selectively recruits anchor-compatible evidence. It is also one of the few biases that is quantifiable.

## Mechanism / Why It Matters
The deeper point, beyond the parent note's "brain reacts to relative changes": anchors are not treated as evidence at all. Random, uninformative anchors move judgments as strongly as plausibly informative ones - so the effect is associative contamination plus lazy adjustment, not Bayesian updating. This makes anchoring exploitable in any negotiation or estimate where a number is spoken first.

## Evidence
- Adjustment mechanism (Epley-Gilovich): head-shaking moves estimates farther from the anchor; cognitive load reduces adjustment.
- Priming mechanism (Mussweiler-Strack): a high-temperature anchor for Germany primed summer words; high car-price anchors primed luxury brands.
- Anchoring index = estimate-difference / anchor-difference, typically ~55%. Redwood-height study: anchors 1,200 vs 180 ft produced estimates 844 vs 282 ft (index 55%). Experienced German judges rolling loaded dice gave 8 vs 5 month sentences (index 50%). Real-estate agents were anchored 41% by an asking price they swore they ignored.
