# The Outside View and the Planning Fallacy

**Source**: Daniel Kahneman, *Thinking, Fast and Slow* (Farrar, Straus and Giroux, 2011) - Part III, "The Outside View"
**Document Type**: Book
**Extracted By**: AI (document-insight-extractor, Book Mode)
**Extraction Date**: 2026-06-28
**Scope**: Books/thinking-fast-and-slow-kahneman

## Core Insight
Forecasters spontaneously take the INSIDE view (their specific plan and current progress) and ignore the OUTSIDE view (the statistical distribution of outcomes for similar projects), even when they personally hold the reference-class knowledge. So plans cluster unrealistically near the best-case scenario. The remedy is reference-class forecasting: anchor on the base rate of similar projects first, then adjust for case specifics.

## Mechanism / Why It Matters
The inside view builds a coherent, detail-rich narrative of THIS project succeeding, and coherence breeds confidence (see availability of one's own plan). It structurally cannot represent the unknown unknowns that sink the reference class, because those are not features of the specific plan. The fix is not more effort on the plan but a deliberate switch of attention to outcomes of the comparison class - a distinct cognitive operation, not a refinement of the inside view.

## Evidence
Kahneman's own curriculum team estimated about 2 years; the expert's reference-class knowledge implied 7-10 years and a 40% failure rate (actual: 8 years, then unused). Scottish Parliament: estimated GBP 40M in 1997, reached GBP 431M by 2004. A 2005 rail study found passenger forecasts overestimated in over 90% of cases (average 106% too high), cost overruns of 45%, and no improvement over 30 years. US kitchen remodels: expected $18,658, paid $38,769. Flyvbjerg operationalized reference-class forecasting.
