# Mental Accounting Errors - Sunk Cost and the Disposition Effect

**Source**: Daniel Kahneman, *Thinking, Fast and Slow* (Farrar, Straus and Giroux, 2011) - Part IV, "Keeping Score"
**Document Type**: Book
**Extracted By**: AI (document-insight-extractor, Book Mode)
**Extraction Date**: 2026-06-28
**Scope**: Books/thinking-fast-and-slow-kahneman

## Core Insight
Treating outcomes in separate "mental accounts" - each demanded to close at a gain - produces two systematic errors: escalating commitment to losers (the sunk-cost fallacy) and selling winners while holding losers (the disposition effect).

## Mechanism / Why It Matters
Closing an account at a loss inflicts the disproportionate pain of loss aversion, so people defer it - holding losers and escalating into failing projects to avoid booking the loss. The desire to close each account in the black overrides the correct, account-blind question of where future money is best placed.

## Evidence
Investors show a documented "massive preference for selling winners over losers"; the after-tax cost of selling the winner instead of the loser is about 3.4% per year, corrected only in December when tax salience rises. Sunk-cost escalation maps onto the fourfold pattern's high-probability-loss cell. Agency wrinkle: escalation can be rational for the EXECUTIVE (canceling stains the record) even when it is wrong for the firm. Teaching the fallacy works - economics and business students walk away from failing projects more readily.
