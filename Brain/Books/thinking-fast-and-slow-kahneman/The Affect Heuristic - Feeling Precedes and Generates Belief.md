# The Affect Heuristic - Feeling Precedes and Generates Belief
**Source**: Daniel Kahneman, *Thinking, Fast and Slow* (Farrar, Straus and Giroux, 2011) - Part 2, The Science of Availability / Slovic's affect heuristic
**Document Type**: Book
**Extracted By**: AI (document-insight-extractor, Book Mode)
**Extraction Date**: 2026-06-28
**Scope**: Books/thinking-fast-and-slow-kahneman

## Core Insight
Where emotion is involved, like or dislike comes FIRST, and beliefs about facts - risks, benefits, evidence - are recruited afterward to justify the feeling. System 2 acts as "an apologist for the emotions of System 1, an endorser rather than an enforcer." The order of operations is the bias: the conclusion is felt, then the reasons are assembled.

## Mechanism / Why It Matters
A single affective stance toward a thing drives every factual judgment about it, which is why those judgments become improperly coupled. The transferable error: people experience their fact-beliefs as the cause of their attitude when the attitude is actually the cause of the beliefs. This is the substitution of "how do I feel about it?" for "what do I think about it?" - and it explains why presenting one-sided emotional information shifts supposedly independent risk and benefit estimates together.

## Evidence
Slovic's affect heuristic: telling people that an activity's RISK is lower also raises their estimate of its BENEFITS, even when no benefit information is given - one feeling drives both judgments, producing an implausible negative correlation between perceived risk and benefit. Reading a passage extolling only the benefits of a technology LOWERED people's perceived risk of it. Haidt's summary captures the order: "the emotional tail wags the rational dog."
