# Simple Formulas Beat Expert Judgment in Low-Validity Environments

**Source**: Daniel Kahneman, *Thinking, Fast and Slow* (Farrar, Straus and Giroux, 2011) - Part III, "Intuitions vs. Formulas"
**Document Type**: Book
**Extracted By**: AI (document-insight-extractor, Book Mode)
**Extraction Date**: 2026-06-28
**Scope**: Books/thinking-fast-and-slow-kahneman

## Core Insight
A mechanical combination of a few scores matches or beats trained expert judgment in roughly 60% of studies (the rest tie, which still favors the cheaper formula). A major reason is that humans are NOISY: they give different answers to identical inputs, and unreliable judgments cannot be valid. Equal-weight "improper" models often beat optimally-fitted regression because equal weights are not distorted by sampling accidents.

## Mechanism / Why It Matters
Experts try to be clever, weighting subtle interactions that are mostly noise in the specific sample. A formula applies the same rule every time, so it has zero internal inconsistency. In low-validity environments the signal is weak; consistency alone then beats erratic human cleverness. This reframes "trust the expert" as a question about environment validity, not credentials.

## Evidence
Meehl's clinical-vs-actuarial review (about 200 studies today; roughly 60% favor algorithms, none reliably favor experts). Counselors predicting freshman GPA lost to a formula (high-school grades plus one aptitude test) on 11 of 14 measures. Ashenfelter's 3-variable weather formula predicts Bordeaux wine prices at correlation above 0.90. Noise: radiologists contradict their own normal/abnormal reads about 20% of the time on the same X-ray. Dawes's "robust beauty of improper linear models"; the Apgar score (5 signs scored 0-1-2) saved hundreds of thousands of infants.
